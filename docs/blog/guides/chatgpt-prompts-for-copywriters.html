ChatGPT Prompts for Copywriters

15 free prompts to write faster, break through blocks, develop your voice, land better clients, and build a high-income career in copywriting.

Good copywriters don't use AI to write their copy. They use it to research faster, brainstorm angles they'd miss alone, stress-test their logic, and handle the admin that eats billable hours. These 15 prompts are built for that — AI as a thinking partner, not a replacement for craft.

1. Copy Research & Briefing

Prompt 1 — Voice of Customer Research Synthesizer

I'm writing copy for [product/service]. I've gathered this customer research: Reviews: [paste 5–10 Amazon/G2/Trustpilot/app store reviews] Survey responses: [paste key open-ended answers] Sales call notes: [common phrases, objections, outcomes] Synthesize for copywriting use: (1) The 5 most emotionally charged phrases customers use (exact words — copy verbatim), (2) The #1 desired outcome (not what they say they want, but what's underneath), (3) The #1 fear or hesitation, (4) The "before state" (how they felt before buying), (5) The "after state" (how they feel now), (6) The villain — what's to blame for their problem. Format as a swipe card I can reference while writing.
Pro tip: The exact phrases customers use are more powerful than anything you invent. Paste their words directly into headlines. The best copy sounds like it was stolen from the customer's diary.

Prompt 2 — Copy Brief Builder

Help me build a copy brief for a new project. Client: [company name and what they do] Project: [what I'm writing — sales page / email sequence / ads / etc.] Product: [describe in detail] Target customer: [demographics + psychographics] Primary goal of the copy: [conversion / awareness / nurture] Key message: [what the copy must communicate] Tone: [formal / conversational / bold / empathetic] Competitor positioning: [how competitors talk about this space] Constraints: [word count / brand guidelines / compliance requirements] Build a complete copy brief with: Project overview → Audience profile → Core message → Key benefits → Objections to address → Proof points available → Tone guide → Success metrics. I'll use this as my writing foundation.

2. Headline & Hook Writing

Prompt 3 — Headline Generator

Write 20 headlines for [product/service/content piece]. Target audience: [describe] Core benefit: [the #1 outcome they get] Key emotion to trigger: [desire / fear / curiosity / relief / belonging] Copy format: [sales page / ad / email subject / blog / landing page] Successful headlines from this client's market (if any): [list or "none"] Headline formulas to include: How-to → Numbered list → Specific result + timeframe → Question → "Reason why" → Bold claim → Problem + solution → Social proof implied → Negative headline (what they don't want) → Curiosity gap. Rate each 1–10 for likely performance. Flag your top 3 picks and explain.
Pro tip: Never choose the headline you like best. Choose the one your target customer would click on — even if it feels uncomfortable or too direct to you.

Prompt 4 — Email Subject Line Pack

Write 15 email subject lines for [campaign type: launch / nurture / re-engagement / cart abandonment / welcome]. Product/context: [describe] Audience: [describe their mindset at this stage] Email sender name/brand: [name they'll see] Include: 3 curiosity gaps → 3 personal/conversational → 3 urgency/scarcity → 2 question formats → 2 numbered/specific → 2 bold claims. For each: subject line + preview text (50 chars). Mark your predicted top 3 for open rate. Flag any that might trip spam filters and why.

3. Copy Frameworks & Drafting

Prompt 5 — Sales Page Architecture

Build the architecture for a long-form sales page for [product/service]. Price point: $[price] | Category: [digital / physical / service / subscription] Target customer: [describe] Core mechanism: [what makes this product uniquely effective] Proof available: [testimonials / case studies / data / media mentions / credentials] Primary objection: [the #1 reason someone wouldn't buy] Outline every section in order with: Section name → Purpose → Word count target → Key persuasion element to use (scarcity / social proof / logic / emotion / etc.) → Notes for the writer. This is an architecture document, not the copy itself.

Prompt 6 — VSL Script Outline

Build a Video Sales Letter (VSL) script outline for [product/service]. Video length target: [5 / 10 / 20 / 30 minutes] Format: [talking head / voiceover + slides / animated] Viewer awareness level: [unaware / problem-aware / solution-aware / product-aware] Core promise: [the transformation or result] Unique mechanism: [what makes this solution different] Outline every segment with: Timestamp range → What happens → Goal of this segment → Transition to next. Mark where proof, curiosity, and urgency peaks are. Include the open-loop that keeps viewers watching past the halfway point.
Pro tip: VSL viewers decide in the first 90 seconds whether to keep watching. The hook must promise a specific, desirable outcome and create a mystery about HOW.

4. Editing & Voice Development

Prompt 7 — Copy Editing & Strengthening

Edit this copy for strength and clarity. My draft: [paste your draft copy] Edit for: (1) Weak verbs — replace with strong, specific ones, (2) Passive voice — rewrite to active, (3) Buried leads — move the most compelling point earlier, (4) Redundancy — cut words without losing meaning, (5) Vague language — replace with specific and concrete, (6) Clichés — flag and suggest alternatives, (7) "We" focus vs. "You" focus — shift to reader-centric language. Show changes in [track changes style / rewritten version / side-by-side]. Explain the 3 most important edits you made and why.

Prompt 8 — Brand Voice Guide Builder

Help me create a brand voice guide for [client/brand name]. Brand personality: [3–5 adjectives] What they are NOT: [opposite adjectives — equally important] Target audience: [describe] Example copy that nails the tone: [paste 2–3 samples] Example copy that misses the tone: [paste 1–2 examples to avoid] Build: (1) Voice + Tone definition (voice is consistent, tone shifts by context — explain both), (2) Writing guidelines (6–8 specific rules with examples of right vs. wrong), (3) Word list — terms to use and terms to avoid, (4) Sentence structure guidance (length, rhythm, complexity), (5) CTA style guide. This should be usable by any writer who comes on this account.

5. Client Business

Prompt 9 — Copywriting Proposal

Write a copywriting project proposal for this client: Company: [name and what they do] Project scope: [what copy they need written] Deliverables: [list] Timeline: [project length] My fee: $[amount] / [structure: flat / retainer / royalty] Why I'm the right fit: [your specific relevant experience] Proposal structure: Executive summary (their problem + your solution) → Scope of work (detailed deliverables) → My process (phases) → Timeline → Investment (fee + payment terms) → Why me (specific proof) → Next steps. Keep it under 2 pages. Professional but personality-forward — not a corporate document.
Pro tip: Frame your fee in terms of ROI, not cost. "This sales page will likely generate 20–100x this investment in revenue" shifts the conversation entirely.

Prompt 10 — Cold Outreach for Copywriters

Write a cold outreach sequence for me to land [target client type: SaaS companies / info product creators / ecommerce brands / coaches / agencies]. My niche: [what type of copy I specialize in] My strongest proof point: [best result you've driven for a client] My offer: [what I'm pitching — audit / sample email / discovery call / intro offer] Write: (1) Initial cold email (150 words max — specific company research, lead with their problem, soft CTA), (2) Follow-up 1 (3 days later — different angle, same soft CTA), (3) Follow-up 2 (7 days later — "last touch" with value or insight). Each email must feel researched, not templated. Include how to personalize the opening line for different companies.

6. Copywriting Craft & Growth

Prompt 11 — Objection Handling Matrix

Build an objection-handling matrix for [product/service]. Price: $[price] Common objections I encounter: [list 5–7 objections the sales team or copy needs to address] For each objection: (1) Restate the objection in the customer's own emotional language, (2) The psychological root of this objection (what fear is underneath?), (3) 2–3 copy responses that address the root, not just the surface, (4) Where in the funnel this objection typically appears, (5) Which format handles it best (headline / bullet / FAQ / testimonial / guarantee). Output as a table I can use across multiple copy assets.

Prompt 12 — A/B Test Copy Variants

I want to A/B test [copy element: headline / CTA / email subject / opening paragraph / button text] for [asset type]. Current control: [paste what you have] What I'm testing for: [click rate / open rate / conversion rate] Hypothesis: [what you think will outperform and why] Write 3 challenger variants. For each: (1) The copy, (2) What psychological principle it uses (urgency / curiosity / social proof / specificity / loss aversion), (3) Why it might beat the control. Also flag: what needs to stay constant in this test (what I should NOT change to isolate the variable).
Pro tip: Test one element at a time. Running a full page redesign tells you something changed — it doesn't tell you what. Isolate variables religiously.

Prompt 13 — Copy Swipe File Builder

I want to build a personal swipe file for [niche/copy type]. Help me identify and analyze great copy. Here is a piece of copy I admire: [paste the copy] Analyze: (1) What makes the headline work — specific technique, (2) The lead — what does it do in the first paragraph to pull you in? (3) The logic structure — how does it build the argument or story? (4) The emotional journey — what does the reader feel at each stage? (5) The close — what makes the CTA irresistible? (6) What technique can I extract and apply to other projects? Format as a reusable lesson.

Prompt 14 — Portfolio Piece Critique

Critique this copywriting portfolio piece as if you're a senior direct-response copywriter reviewing a junior writer's work. [Paste the copy sample] Be honest and specific. Critique: (1) The hook — does it make me want to read on? (2) The argument structure — is the logic compelling and clear? (3) The emotional engagement — does it speak to real feelings or stay surface-level? (4) The CTA — is it clear, specific, and irresistible? (5) Overall voice — is it consistent and distinctive? Rate 1–10 and give 3 specific, actionable improvements. Don't be gentle — be useful.

Prompt 15 — Annual Copywriting Business Review

Help me review my copywriting business this year. Revenue: $[amount] from [number of clients] Copy types I wrote most: [list] Best client relationship: [why it worked] Worst client situation: [what went wrong] Skills I built: [what you got better at] Skills I neglected: [what you avoided] My goal for next year: $[amount] from [ideal client type] Analyze: (1) My most profitable copy type — should I double down? (2) The client type that creates the most friction — should I stop taking them? (3) What would need to be true to 2x my revenue? (4) The skill gap that's capping my rates, (5) My #1 priority for Q1.
