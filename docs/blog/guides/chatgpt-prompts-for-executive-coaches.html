ChatGPT Prompts for Executive Coaches

15 free prompts to prepare better sessions, develop frameworks, write compelling client resources, and grow your coaching practice.

Executive coaching is a high-touch, high-stakes practice where your preparation and frameworks determine your impact. AI can't replace your intuition, judgment, or the relationship you build with a client — but it can help you synthesize complex situations faster, generate frameworks for reflection, and handle the business side of coaching more efficiently. These 15 prompts are designed around where AI actually helps.

1. Session Preparation

Prompt 1 — Pre-Session Briefing

Help me prepare for an executive coaching session. Client context (anonymized): [role / level / industry / months into engagement] Focus from last session: [what we agreed to work on] What the client reported back: [what they tried, what happened — no identifying detail] What I know about this client's patterns: [their recurring themes, blind spots, default behaviors] My hypothesis about what's really going on: [your intuition before the session] What I want to explore today: [questions or themes you want to introduce] Suggest: (1) 3 powerful opening questions for this session, (2) a challenge I could introduce based on what they've been avoiding, (3) a reframe or model that might be useful given the patterns you described, and (4) a reflection question I should sit with myself before the session starts.
Pro tip: The best coaching preparation is not about planning what to say — it's about clearing your own mental space and arriving with full presence. Use AI to synthesize complexity, then set it aside and show up to listen.

Prompt 2 — Coaching Question Bank for a Specific Challenge

Generate a coaching question bank for a specific leadership challenge. Challenge type: [e.g., leader struggling with delegation, executive preparing for a board presentation, leader managing a team through a reorg, first-time C-suite transition, leader dealing with a difficult peer, burnout in a high-performing executive] Generate 15 coaching questions in 3 tiers: Tier 1 — Exploration (open, curious — help the client describe their reality) Tier 2 — Insight (deepen awareness — surface assumptions and patterns) Tier 3 — Forward movement (action-oriented — what they will do and by when) Include a note on which questions are highest risk (might be too challenging early) and which to use if the conversation stalls.

Prompt 3 — Debrief a Coaching Session

Help me debrief and learn from a coaching session I just completed. What happened in the session: [describe the arc — what came up, what shifted or didn't, how it ended] What I did that worked: [specific interventions, questions, moments of connection] What I did that didn't land: [where I lost the client, what fell flat] A moment I want to examine: [describe a specific moment you want to understand better] My emotional state during the session: [honest — where were you present / distracted / reactive] Write: (1) what the session suggests about this client's current edge, (2) what my intervention choice in that moment reveals about my coaching style or bias, (3) what I would do differently, and (4) the one question I wish I'd asked.
Pro tip: The sessions you debrief carefully are the ones that make you a better coach. It's not enough to write session notes — you need to examine your own process, not just the client's.

2. Client Development Frameworks

Prompt 4 — 360 Feedback Synthesis

Help me synthesize 360-degree feedback themes for a coaching client. The feedback source themes (anonymized): [describe the patterns — what multiple stakeholders said, without identifying individuals. E.g., "direct reports consistently mentioned X, peers noted Y, the manager said Z"] How the client received this feedback: [defensive / open / dismissive / deeply affected / surprised] What the client's self-assessment revealed: [how they see themselves vs. how others see them] The gaps between self-perception and stakeholder perception: [where are the biggest disconnects] Write: (1) a theme synthesis the client could work from (grouped, not just listed), (2) the one pattern that most needs to be named in coaching, (3) coaching questions to help the client sit with the feedback rather than immediately defending or over-correcting, and (4) what "success" looks like 6 months from now if the development work lands.

Prompt 5 — Leadership Development Plan

Help me design a leadership development plan for a coaching client. Client profile (anonymized): [role / level / context — e.g., VP moving to C-suite, founder transitioning out of day-to-day operations, new leader managing former peers] Primary development goals from coaching intake: [what the client said they want] What the stakeholder data says they need: [if different from above] The gap that's most limiting their effectiveness right now: [your clinical hypothesis] Engagement length: [months] Resources available beyond coaching: [assessments, cohort programs, reading, peer coaching, internal sponsorship] Write a 90-day and 6-month development plan: focus areas, how to build each, how to measure progress in a way that feels meaningful to this executive (not just checkbox activities), and the hardest moment likely to come in the first 90 days.

Prompt 6 — Reflection Framework for a Specific Leadership Situation

Help me create a reflection framework for a client dealing with a specific leadership situation. Situation type: [e.g., managing through an acquisition, inheriting a low-performing team, navigating a board conflict, deciding whether to stay or leave an organization, managing a direct report they inherited who is resistant] What the client is currently doing: [their approach] What's not working: [where the approach is breaking down] What they keep coming back to: [the recurring thought, fear, or belief] What matters most to them professionally: [values, identity, what they're protecting] Design a structured reflection exercise the client can work through between sessions: 5–7 questions that build progressively, a reframe to offer, and a decision or action question to close the reflection.
Pro tip: The best between-session work is reflection that builds on the "edge" you found in the last session. If you were nowhere near the edge, the homework will feel like homework. If you hit something real, they'll do the work without prompting.

3. Practice Building

Prompt 7 — Coaching Proposal

Help me write a coaching proposal for a prospective client or organization. Prospect type: [individual executive / HR-sponsored engagement / team coaching / group cohort / organizational contract] What they told you they need: [the presenting problem or goal] What you believe they actually need: [your hypothesis after the discovery call] Your relevant experience: [not a full bio — what specifically qualifies you for this engagement] Proposed approach: [structure, frequency, duration, assessments, stakeholder involvement] Investment: [include or exclude from proposal — your preference] Write a proposal that leads with their situation (not your credentials), frames your approach as the logical solution to their problem, addresses the unspoken concern about ROI, and closes with a clear next step.

Prompt 8 — Thought Leadership Content

Help me write thought leadership content for my coaching practice. My coaching specialty: [what you're known for / want to be known for] Target audience for my content: [HR leaders / C-suite executives / mid-market CEOs / high-potential leaders / founders] A specific leadership insight I've developed through my work: [something you've observed repeatedly that most people don't say out loud] The counterintuitive angle: [what makes this a "wait, that's different" perspective] Format: [LinkedIn post / article / newsletter / talk / podcast guest pitch] Write: (1) the core insight stated in one clear sentence, (2) a story or observation that illustrates it (anonymized), (3) the practical implication for the reader, (4) platform-specific format (if LinkedIn: hook + insight + CTA under 1,300 characters; if article: full 600-word structure).

Prompt 9 — Discovery Call Framework

Help me design a discovery call framework for prospective coaching clients. Type of engagement: [individual coaching / organizational contract / team coaching] What I want to understand from the call: [fit, goals, readiness, decision process, stakeholders, budget — list your actual priorities] What the prospect wants from the call: [what they've signaled / what they typically expect] Where discovery calls typically go wrong for me: [honest — where I lose them or they lose interest] What separates the clients I do my best work with: [the factors that predict a great engagement] Write: (1) a 45-minute discovery call structure, (2) 5 questions I should always ask (and why each one matters beyond the surface answer), (3) how to assess readiness and fit during the conversation, (4) how to handle the moment they ask about price before you've established value, and (5) how to close the call whether it's a fit or not.
Pro tip: The discovery call is also your first coaching session — how you listen, the questions you ask, and what you reflect back tells the prospect more about your capabilities than any proposal you'll ever write.

4. Client Resources

Prompt 10 — Workshop Design for a Leadership Team

Help me design a leadership team workshop. Team context: [senior team / newly formed / fractured / high-performing but siloed / dealing with a specific challenge] Workshop goal: [what needs to change for the team after this session] Duration: [half-day / full-day / multi-day offsite] What has been tried before: [so we don't repeat approaches that didn't land] The undiscussable: [the thing everyone knows but no one says — if you have a hypothesis] My role: [external facilitator only / coach-facilitator / embedded in the org] Write: (1) session design with time blocks, (2) opening activity that establishes the real purpose, (3) the pivotal conversation that needs to happen and how to create conditions for it, (4) how to handle a participant who derails the session, and (5) closing commitments structure that actually gets followed up.

Prompt 11 — Client Progress Summary

Help me write a client progress summary for a mid-engagement review. Engagement context (anonymized): [role / months in / who is sponsoring the engagement] Goals from the intake: [what we set out to achieve] What has shifted: [observable behavior change, insights, decisions made] What has not moved: [where the client is stuck or avoiding] What the sponsor or stakeholder has noticed: [any feedback from the organization] What I recommend for the next phase: [focus, pace, structure changes] Write a mid-engagement summary that: (1) celebrates real progress without overclaiming, (2) names the stuck point honestly, (3) makes a recommendation for the next phase, and (4) can be shared with a sponsor if the engagement is organizationally funded.

Prompt 12 — Reading Recommendation with Coaching Frame

Help me recommend a book or resource to a coaching client and frame it as coaching work. What the client is working on: [the development theme — e.g., executive presence, delegation, managing up, emotional regulation, strategic thinking] What they tend to do with recommended reading: [read it and apply it / skim it / not read it / intellectualize it] The specific book or resource I want to recommend: [title, or ask me to suggest options] How I want them to engage with it: [not just read — what specific questions should they bring back] Write: (1) how to introduce the resource in a session, (2) 3 reading guide questions to give the client before they start, (3) how to debrief the reading in the next session, and (4) what I'm listening for in their response — what it will tell me about their development.
Pro tip: Assigned reading without a frame is homework. Assigned reading with 3 focused questions creates a coaching conversation. The question "what surprised you?" surfaces assumption disruption. The question "what did you resist?" surfaces avoidance.

5. Professional Development

Prompt 13 — Supervision Preparation

Help me prepare for coaching supervision. Case I want to bring (anonymized): [describe the engagement — what's happening, what I'm trying, what's not working] My working hypothesis: [what I think is really going on] Where I feel stuck: [specifically — is it the intervention, the relationship, my own reactivity] What I want from supervision: [challenge / validation / new lens / specific technique] My countertransference: [how this client affects me — be honest] Write: (1) how to present this case in 5 minutes without losing the nuance, (2) the question I most need to ask my supervisor, (3) what I'm avoiding saying — and why it probably matters, and (4) a self-supervision question I can return to if supervision doesn't fully resolve the stuckness.

Prompt 14 — Coaching Practice Annual Review

Help me review my coaching practice this year. Client portfolio this year: [types of clients, industries, levels — no names] Engagements that went exceptionally well: [what made them work] Engagements that were difficult or ended poorly: [what happened and why — honest] What I learned about my coaching style: [what patterns did you notice] What I avoided or deferred: [niches, client types, business development activities] Revenue and practice metrics: [approximate — growth, retention, new business] Where I want to be in 2 years: [practice size, specialization, reputation, income] Write: (1) an honest assessment of the year — wins and gaps, (2) the one pattern in my client struggles that reflects something in my coaching I need to develop, (3) the business development investment that would most move my practice forward, and (4) the niche or specialization that would most differentiate me given my experience.

Prompt 15 — Developing a Coaching Framework or Model

Help me develop a proprietary coaching framework or model based on my experience. What I consistently observe with my clients: [patterns you see again and again] The insight that most distinguishes my approach: [what you believe about leadership development that most coaches don't emphasize] What my best client transformations have in common: [the mechanism of change] What I've named it (or working title): [if you have one] Who this framework is for: [the audience who would most benefit from understanding it] Write: (1) a framework structure with 3–5 components and a name for each, (2) a visual metaphor that makes it memorable, (3) how to explain it to a C-suite executive in 60 seconds, (4) how to write about it for LinkedIn or a keynote, and (5) what would make this framework uniquely yours vs. something that sounds generic.
