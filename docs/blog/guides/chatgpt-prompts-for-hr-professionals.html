15 ChatGPT Prompts for HR Professionals

Recruit faster, communicate better, and handle the most difficult people situations with more confidence — using AI as your behind-the-scenes thinking partner.

HR is one of the highest-stakes professions in any organization. You're writing legal documents, managing sensitive conversations, shaping culture, and representing the company to candidates — all simultaneously. These prompts help you do all of it faster and better.

These work for HR generalists, HR business partners, recruiters, talent acquisition specialists, and HR managers at companies of any size. Always review AI output before sending, especially for legally sensitive communications. Use them in ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini.

Recruiting & Talent Acquisition

1. The Job Posting Optimizer

Most job postings are written from the inside out — what the company needs, not what the candidate wants. This prompt flips it.

Write an optimized job posting for [role title] at [company name]. What the role actually does day-to-day: [describe in plain language]. Required qualifications: [list]. Nice-to-have: [list]. Salary range: $[X]-$[Y]. Company culture: [describe honestly in 2 sentences]. Write a posting that: 1) Opens with why someone would WANT this job (growth, impact, team, mission — not "exciting opportunity"), 2) Describes what success looks like in 90 days, 3) Uses inclusive language — avoid gendered terms and unnecessary credential requirements, 4) Is honest about the hard parts (reduces bad-fit applications), 5) Makes the application process clear. Also: flag any requirements in my draft that could discourage qualified applicants (degree requirements, years of experience that may be proxies for other criteria). Under 450 words.

2. The Candidate Screening Questions

Create a structured interview question set for [role title]. What this role requires: [describe the key skills, behaviors, and culture fit factors]. What we've learned from previous hiring mistakes for this role: [describe — e.g., "we hired someone who was technically strong but struggled with ambiguity" or "we need someone who can manage up"]. Write: 1) 5 behavioral interview questions (STAR format) targeting the most critical competencies, 2) 2 situational/hypothetical questions for judgment calls specific to this role, 3) 1 role-specific technical or skills question, 4) 2 culture-fit questions that reveal real values, not just what candidates think we want to hear, 5) What a strong answer looks like for each question (scoring rubric, 1-3 sentences). Avoid: questions about age, family status, national origin, religion, or anything not related to job performance.

3. The Offer Letter & Negotiation Script

Help me write and negotiate a job offer for [candidate name / role]. Offer details: Base salary: $[X]. Bonus target: [%]. Equity/options: [if applicable]. Benefits: [highlight 3-4 key benefits]. Start date: [date]. Write: 1) A warm offer call script — the words to say when extending the offer verbally (1 minute), 2) A follow-up offer summary email to send immediately after the call (not the legal contract — the human version that makes them excited to accept), 3) A response to "Can you do better on salary?" — how to negotiate without devaluing the candidate or overpromising, 4) A response to "I need a week to think about it" — keep them warm without applying pressure, 5) What to send if they accept: the "first day welcome" email that makes them feel like the decision is already the right one.

Employee Communication

4. The Performance Review Framework

Write a performance review framework for [role / team]. Review period: [annual / semi-annual / quarterly]. What we're evaluating: [list the key dimensions — performance, behaviors, development, culture contribution]. Common problems with our current reviews: [describe — e.g., "managers write vague feedback", "everything gets rated 'meets expectations'", "employees feel blindsided"]. Build: 1) A review form with rating scale and specific behavioral anchors for each rating (what does "exceeds expectations" actually look like?), 2) A manager guide: how to write specific, evidence-based feedback that holds up to scrutiny, 3) A response to "my employee disagrees with their rating" — how to facilitate that conversation, 4) A calibration process description (one page) for aligning ratings across managers. Goal: reviews that are fair, documented, and actually improve performance — not just compliance.

5. The Difficult Conversation Script

Help me script a difficult conversation with an employee. Situation: [choose — performance issue / attendance / behavior / conflict with colleague / role elimination / PIP placement / termination]. Specific issue: [describe the behavior or situation in factual terms]. What I've already tried: [previous conversations, documentation, coaching]. Desired outcome: [what do I want from this conversation — behavior change, agreement on PIP, separation?]. Write: 1) How to open the conversation — first two sentences that are direct without being combative, 2) The core message in plain language — what I need to say clearly, 3) The most likely pushback responses and how to answer each, 4) How to close the conversation with clear next steps regardless of how it goes, 5) What to document after the conversation. Note: flag if any part of this situation requires legal review before proceeding.

6. The Company-Wide Announcement

Write an all-employee announcement for [topic: leadership change / policy update / restructuring / benefit change / return to office / layoff]. Details: [describe what's changing, when, and why — be honest]. What employees will want to know most: [what does this mean for me? Is my job safe? What changes?]. Tone needed: [transparent and direct / empathetic / confident]. Write: 1) The announcement email / message (under 300 words), 2) An FAQ document addressing the 5 most likely employee questions, 3) Manager talking points — what people leaders should say when their teams ask follow-up questions in 1:1s, 4) A 24-hour follow-up message acknowledging receipt and next steps. For sensitive announcements (layoffs, leadership changes): tell me what NOT to say — common HR phrases that sound defensive or insincere.

Policy & Compliance

7. The HR Policy Plain-Language Rewrite

Rewrite this HR policy in plain language that employees will actually read and understand. Policy: [paste or describe the current policy — PTO, remote work, code of conduct, disciplinary, expense, etc.]. Current version problems: [too long / legal language / inconsistent with actual practice / employees don't know it exists]. Rewrite the policy so: 1) The main rule is stated in the first sentence — no burying the lede, 2) Common scenarios are addressed with clear "yes/no" guidance (not "it depends"), 3) The process for exceptions is clear, 4) Length is cut by at least 40% without losing substance, 5) It can pass the "new hire on day 3" test — would someone unfamiliar with company culture understand this? Also: flag anything in the current policy that may create legal exposure or is inconsistently enforced.

8. The Investigation Summary Template

Help me write a workplace investigation summary. Investigation type: [harassment / discrimination / misconduct / policy violation / safety concern]. What I can share: Complaint summary: [describe the allegation in general terms]. Parties involved: [describe roles, not names]. Evidence reviewed: [types — interviews, emails, documents, etc.]. Findings: [what was substantiated vs. unsubstantiated]. Actions taken: [describe]. Write a summary that: 1) Uses objective, factual language throughout, 2) Documents what was done to ensure a fair process, 3) States findings clearly without legal conclusions (leave those to counsel), 4) Describes next steps and monitoring plan. Note: this is a template to adapt — flag any section that should be reviewed by employment counsel before finalizing.

9. The Onboarding Experience Builder

Design a 90-day onboarding experience for [role / department]. What new hires typically struggle with in the first 90 days: [describe — information overload / unclear expectations / slow relationship building / system access delays]. What great onboarding looks like for this role: [describe]. Build a structured plan: Week 1: [what specifically should happen — not "meet the team" but who specifically, what to learn, what's the first deliverable]. Week 2-4: [structured learning + first real contributions]. Month 2: [expanding scope + relationship building]. Month 3: [full ownership + 90-day review]. For each phase: what does the manager do, what does HR own, and how do we measure "is onboarding working?" Also: write the Day 1 welcome email that sets expectations without overwhelming.

Culture & Employee Experience

10. The Employee Survey Builder

Design an employee engagement survey for [company size: under 50 / 50-200 / 200+ employees]. What we're trying to learn: [describe your specific concerns — do employees feel heard? Is the manager experience consistent? Is there a retention risk?]. What we'll do with results: [be specific — if the answer is "we'll share them but nothing changes," the survey will backfire]. Build a survey with: 1) Under 15 questions (any longer and response rate drops significantly), 2) A mix of scaled questions (1-5) and 2-3 open text questions, 3) Questions that reveal actionable information, not just sentiment, 4) Questions targeting the drivers of engagement most correlated with retention, 5) One question that directly asks: "What's the one thing we could change that would make the biggest difference for you?" Also write the launch communication that explains why this survey matters and what you'll do with results.

11. The Recognition Program Designer

Design an employee recognition program for [company name]. Company size: [X employees]. Budget: $[X/month or year, or $0]. Current recognition culture: [describe — is recognition inconsistent? Manager-dependent? Does it feel performative?]. What behaviors we want to reinforce: [list 3-4 — e.g., collaboration, going above and beyond, living our values, innovation]. Design a program that: 1) Works with the budget specified — no expensive software required, 2) Makes recognition specific (not "great job!" but recognition tied to specific behaviors), 3) Includes peer-to-peer recognition, not just top-down, 4) Has a cadence (weekly / monthly / quarterly) that keeps it alive, 5) Doesn't feel like a checkbox — it should create moments people actually remember. Also write the manager guide: how to recognize employees in a way that lands, not just technically counts.

12. The Exit Interview Framework

Create an exit interview framework that actually generates useful data. Current problem: [describe — people say "great opportunity" to avoid awkwardness / we collect data but don't act / managers find out and hold it against people]. Write: 1) 8-10 exit interview questions that get to the real reasons people leave (not the official story), 2) The format that gets the most honest answers — in-person, survey, or third party? Make a recommendation with reasoning, 3) How to categorize and analyze responses across exits to find patterns, 4) A quarterly executive summary template: "Here is what our departing employees are telling us — and what it means", 5) How to decide when exit feedback should go to a specific manager vs. stays aggregated. The goal is exit data that changes something — not data that sits in a spreadsheet.

HR Operations & Strategy

13. The HR Business Partner Prep Guide

Help me prepare for a business partner meeting with [department / business leader]. Meeting goal: [quarterly review / workforce planning / org design / resolving a specific issue]. What I know about this leader's priorities: [describe — what are they focused on, what do they worry about?]. HR data I have to bring: [headcount / turnover / open roles / engagement scores / other]. Prepare: 1) The 3 most important things to communicate proactively (don't wait for them to ask), 2) The HR question I should ask this leader that I've been afraid to ask, 3) Data storytelling: how to present a concerning metric (high turnover, low engagement) in a way that gets action rather than defensiveness, 4) How to position an HR initiative as solving a business problem they care about — not an HR priority, 5) The one "so what" for each data point: what does it mean and what should we do?

14. The Compensation Review Explainer

Help me communicate compensation review decisions to employees and managers. Context: Total budget: [%] increase pool. Methodology: [how decisions were made — merit-based, market adjustment, tenure, etc.]. Range of outcomes: [what's the range employees will see — 0% to X%]. Challenging cases: [employees who got 0% / employees who feel underpaid relative to the market / new hires who were hired above current employees]. Write: 1) The manager talking points guide — what to say when delivering each outcome, 2) A response to "My colleague makes more than me" — how to handle the comp transparency question honestly, 3) A response to "I received 0% — am I at risk?" — how to separate comp from performance messaging, 4) A response to "I received a raise but still feel underpaid for the market" — the market context conversation. For each: what TO say and what NOT to say.

15. The HR Annual Plan Builder

Help me build an annual HR plan for [company name]. Current headcount: [X]. Projected growth: [X% or X new hires]. Biggest HR challenges last year: [describe — turnover in specific departments / slow hiring / manager capability / culture issues]. Leadership priorities for this year: [describe what the business is focused on]. HR team size: [X people, including me]. Build a plan with: 1) 3 HR priorities for the year — chosen for highest business impact, not HR completeness, 2) For each priority: the business problem it solves (not the HR goal), the metric that proves success, and a rough timeline, 3) What NOT to do this year — what HR activities should we deprioritize to protect capacity, 4) The business case for one additional HR resource or tool, if applicable, 5) How to present this plan to the executive team in 10 minutes. Frame everything in terms of business outcomes — revenue, retention cost, productivity — not HR metrics alone.
Pro tip: Start with Prompt 5 (Difficult Conversation Script) — it has the highest immediate ROI. Most HR professionals know what conversation they've been avoiding. Preparing with this prompt takes 10 minutes and dramatically increases the chance the conversation goes well. Difficult conversations handled early almost always save a larger, messier situation later.
