ChatGPT Prompts for Interior Designers

15 free prompts to write better client proposals, develop design concepts faster, market your practice, and build a business that reflects your creative vision.

Interior design is visual — but the work that wins and retains clients is written: proposals, concept narratives, client communication, Instagram captions. These 15 prompts help you handle the words faster so your creative energy goes where it belongs.

1. Client Discovery & Proposals

Prompt 1 — Client Intake Questionnaire

Write a comprehensive client intake questionnaire for my interior design practice. My specialty: [residential / commercial / hospitality / full-service / e-design] Typical project scope: [full room / whole home / renovation / new build] Price point: [accessible / mid-market / luxury] Include questions covering: (1) Project scope and spaces involved, (2) Functional needs and lifestyle factors, (3) Style preferences (with follow-up probes beyond "modern vs. traditional"), (4) Color comfort (what they love, what they hate), (5) Furniture and finishes investment expectations, (6) Timeline and decision-making process, (7) Who else is involved in decisions, (8) Inspiration sources and dealbreakers. Add 2–3 questions that reveal how collaborative vs. hands-off the client prefers to be. Format as a client-facing form.
Pro tip: The client questionnaire isn't just data collection — it's the first demonstration of your professional process. Make it feel as considered as your design work.

Prompt 2 — Design Proposal Writer

Write a client design proposal for this project. Project: [describe — room type, scope, client situation] Client style vision: [what they're going for — pull from intake notes] My design approach for this project: [your concept direction] Services I'm proposing: [full-service / specify phases] Investment: $[design fee] + [procurement/FF&E estimate if applicable] Timeline: [project length] Proposal sections: (1) Project overview (restate what they told you — shows you listened), (2) Design vision (your creative direction in 150 words — evocative, specific), (3) Scope of services (what's included, phase by phase), (4) What we need from you (client responsibilities), (5) Investment and payment schedule, (6) Timeline, (7) Next step. Tone: [elevated / approachable]. Sound like a designer, not a contractor.

2. Design Concept Development

Prompt 3 — Concept Narrative Writer

Write a design concept narrative for this project. Space: [room type and size] Client profile: [who they are — lifestyle, aesthetic, how they use the space] Design direction: [style, mood, key themes — e.g., "warm minimalism with artisan craftsmanship"] Key materials and finishes I'm using: [list] Color palette: [describe] Focal point: [what the room is organized around] Write a 200-word concept narrative that: Opens with a mood or feeling (not "the room features...") → Explains the design logic and why it fits this client → Describes the sensory experience of being in the space → Connects material and color choices to the concept. This is for the client presentation — it should make them feel the space before they see it.
Pro tip: Great concept narratives are written in present tense as if the client is already in the space. "The morning light filters through linen curtains..." pulls them in; "We selected linen curtains..." keeps them at arm's length.

Prompt 4 — Mood Board Caption & Presentation Copy

Write presentation copy for my mood board and concept presentation. Project: [describe] Design concept: [name/theme of the concept] Mood board elements: [describe what's on it — textures, colors, furniture styles, materials, art references] Key design decisions to explain: [3–5 choices that need narrative context] Audience: [homeowner / developer / commercial client] Write: (1) Mood board overview (100 words — the emotional tone and why it fits this client), (2) Short captions for 4–6 mood board sections (25 words each), (3) Transition phrases between concept sections in the presentation, (4) How to verbally pitch the concept in 60 seconds if they ask "so what's the direction?". Make it visual even in text.

3. Client Communication

Prompt 5 — Client Update Email

Write a project update email for my interior design client. Project stage: [concept approval / procurement / installation / reveal] What's happened since last update: [progress, decisions made, items received] What's outstanding: [pending approvals, back-ordered items, contractor coordination] Next step they need to take: [decision needed / timeline confirmation / no action] Any issue to communicate diplomatically: [delay, over-budget item, substitution needed] Tone: [professional and warm] Email: Brief subject line → Summary of where we are → What happened this week → What they need to do or decide → Timeline reminder → Warm close. Under 250 words. Never make clients feel like they're tracking you down for updates.

Prompt 6 — Scope Creep & Budget Conversation

Help me prepare for a conversation with a client about [scope creep / budget overrun / request outside original agreement]. The situation: [what happened — they want to add rooms / changed their minds after approval / items exceed budget] Original agreement: [what was scoped] What they're asking for: [the new or additional request] Financial impact: [additional cost or fee] My goal: [add to scope with fee / reset expectations / both] Give me: (1) How to open the conversation (acknowledge their enthusiasm, don't be defensive), (2) How to explain the situation clearly and professionally, (3) The specific ask: "what I'd propose is..." (4) How to handle "but we've already paid you" or "can't you just include it?", (5) A written follow-up email version of this conversation for documentation.
Pro tip: Have this conversation on the phone first, then follow up in writing. The call preserves the relationship; the email protects you legally.

4. Portfolio & Marketing

Prompt 7 — Project Case Study Writer

Write a portfolio case study for a completed interior design project. Project name (or nickname): [private / descriptive] Type: [residential master bedroom / commercial restaurant / boutique hotel lobby / etc.] Location: [city or region — vague enough for client privacy] Design challenge: [what made this project interesting or complex] My solution: [the design approach] Key features: [3–4 memorable design moments] Client outcome: [how the client uses and feels about the space] Write: (1) Project intro (75 words — evocative, not just descriptive), (2) The challenge (100 words), (3) The design approach (150 words), (4) Key moments / design decisions (3 × 50 words), (5) The result (75 words). Keep client identity private. Make someone reading this want to hire me for something similar.

Prompt 8 — Instagram Content Calendar

Build a 30-day Instagram content calendar for my interior design practice. My aesthetic: [describe your design style and typical projects] Target client: [who I want to attract — homeowner / developer / commercial / all] Content I can post: [project photos / in-progress / product favorites / mood boards / reels / before-after] Business goal: [inquiries / brand building / collaborator relationships] 30 content ideas: For each — content type, caption hook (first line), relevant hashtags (5 design + 3 location + 2 niche), and story/reel suggestion. Mix: finished project reveals, process shots, material close-ups, designer life, product recommendations, client stories, and soft CTAs. Ratio: 80% value/inspiration, 20% business.
Pro tip: In-progress content (before, during, after) gets 2–3x more saves than final reveals. Your audience wants to understand the process, not just admire the outcome.

5. Vendor & Project Management

Prompt 9 — Vendor Introduction & Sourcing Email

Write a professional email to introduce myself to a new vendor/showroom. My practice: [name, specialty, typical project type and scale] Vendor/showroom: [name, what they carry] What I'm looking for from this relationship: [trade pricing / sample access / project collaboration / product knowledge] Project I'm working on (to show I'm actively buying): [brief description] Email: Introduce my practice specifically (not generically) → Reference something specific about their line or showroom → State what I'm looking for → Easy next step (visit / call / trade account setup). Under 150 words. Professional but personable — this is a relationship, not a transaction.

Prompt 10 — Contractor Coordination Email

Write a coordination email to a contractor on my project. Project: [describe] Contractor type: [general contractor / painter / electrician / carpenter / etc.] What I need to communicate: [schedule coordination / finish specification / design clarification / punch list item] Issue (if any): [problem with their work, scheduling conflict, specification deviation] My tone goal: [professional and direct without damaging the working relationship] Write an email that: States the need or issue clearly → Includes the specific information they need to act → Sets a clear deadline or response expectation → Is professional and direct — not apologetic or aggressive. If there's a problem: lead with the resolution needed, not the criticism.

6. Business Development

Prompt 11 — Referral Partner Outreach

Write outreach messages to build referral relationships with complementary professionals. My practice: [name, specialty, typical client] Target referral partners: [architects / real estate agents / general contractors / luxury realtors / home stagers / builders] What I offer in return: [reciprocal referrals / co-marketing / joint content] Write 2 versions: (1) Cold email (under 150 words — specific, value-led, easy next step), (2) LinkedIn connection request + message (under 200 words total). Both should: Reference something specific about their work → Explain the natural fit → Not feel like a pitch — feel like a peer introduction. Include a follow-up message if no response in 2 weeks.

Prompt 12 — Discovery Call Script

Write a discovery call script for converting inquiries into interior design clients. Call length: [20–30 minutes] My services: [describe] Starting investment: $[fee] Common objection: [cost / uncertainty about process / "we just want one room"] Script: (1) Warm opener and rapport questions (3–5 minutes), (2) Understanding their project and vision (8–10 minutes — specific questions), (3) Understanding their timeline and decision-making (2–3 minutes), (4) Presenting my approach (3–4 minutes), (5) Handling the cost conversation, (6) Next step — proposal or site visit. Include: how to identify unqualified leads early without wasting time, and how to ask for the next step without pressure.
Pro tip: The best discovery calls are 70% listening, 30% talking. The client who feels heard on the call is far more likely to say yes to the proposal.

7. Content & Thought Leadership

Prompt 13 — Design Trend Commentary

Write a design trend commentary piece for my blog or Instagram. Trend to discuss: [specific trend: biophilic design / maximalism / warm minimalism / limewash walls / arched doorways / etc.] My take: [do I love it / am I cautious about it / how to do it right] Client application: [how my typical clients can incorporate this or why they should be careful] My design philosophy: [what lens do I view trends through] Write: (1) Blog version (400 words — opinion-led, specific examples, practical advice), (2) Instagram caption version (150 words + hashtags), (3) One-sentence soundbite version (for a story or reel). My POV should be clear — not a neutral "here are the pros and cons" piece. Designers who have opinions get remembered.

Prompt 14 — E-Design Package Description

Write copy for my e-design (online interior design) service packages. Packages I offer: [describe your tiers — room package / style guide / full virtual / etc.] What's included in each: [deliverables per package] Price points: $[package 1] / $[package 2] / $[package 3] Who e-design is for: [client type — DIYers / out-of-area / smaller budget] What makes my e-design different: [your approach or specialty] Write: (1) Service overview paragraph (100 words — who this is for and what they get), (2) Package descriptions (one per tier — 75 words each, benefit-led), (3) FAQ section (5 questions: What's included? How does the process work? How long does it take? Can I ask questions? What if I don't love it?). Tone: approachable but professional. No "you'll love your new home!" promises.

Prompt 15 — Annual Practice Review

Help me review my interior design practice this year. Projects completed: [number and types] Revenue: $[amount] Client experience highlights: [what clients loved] Challenging situations: [difficult projects or clients, what you learned] Referral sources: [where new business came from] What I want more of: [project types, client profiles] What I want less of: [project types, client situations] Analyze: (1) What pattern defines my best projects this year? (2) What client type creates the most friction — and is it worth continuing to take them? (3) Where is the revenue growth opportunity — more projects, higher fees, better clients, different services? (4) My #1 priority for Q1, (5) The hard truth: what am I avoiding that would grow my practice most?
