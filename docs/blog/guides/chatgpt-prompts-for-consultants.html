15 ChatGPT Prompts for Consultants

Your expertise is the product. AI helps you package, sell, and deliver that expertise faster — so you can take on more clients without working more hours.

Consulting is simultaneously one of the most intellectually demanding and most administratively intensive businesses. Between proposals, deliverables, presentations, client communication, and business development, the writing alone can consume half your week. These prompts help you produce higher-quality work in less time.

These work for management consultants, strategy consultants, IT consultants, financial consultants, HR consultants, marketing consultants, and any professional running a solo or boutique consulting practice. Use in ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini. Always verify and adapt AI output with your professional expertise.

Business Development & Proposals

1. The Consulting Proposal Template

A consulting proposal that wins is less about what you'll do and more about demonstrating that you understand the client's problem better than they do.

Write a consulting proposal for [engagement type]. Client: [describe the organization — industry, size, situation]. The problem they've described: [describe what they told you in their own terms]. What I know about the deeper problem: [what you've diagnosed beyond what they said — the real issue]. My proposed approach: [describe your methodology or approach]. Timeline: [X weeks/months]. Investment: $[X]. Write a proposal that: 1) Opens with a sharp articulation of their problem — showing I understand it better than the RFP does, 2) Reframes the problem if needed — what's actually going on vs. what they think is going on, 3) Presents my approach as a logical answer to the real problem, 4) Shows the investment relative to the value — not "here's what it costs" but "here's what not solving this costs", 5) Makes a specific recommendation on how to proceed. Under 4 pages. No consulting jargon that clients don't use.

2. The Consulting Niche Positioning Statement

Help me define and articulate my consulting niche. My background: [industry / functional expertise / notable companies / types of problems I've solved]. Best clients I've served: [describe — industry, size, what they were struggling with, what changed]. What makes me different from other consultants in this space: [be specific — not "deep expertise" but actual differentiators]. Write: 1) My consulting niche in one sentence ("I help [specific type of organization] solve [specific problem] using [distinctive approach]"), 2) A one-paragraph positioning statement for my website that leads with client problem, not my resume, 3) How to answer "what do you do?" at a networking event in 15 seconds — no jargon, no laundry list, 4) The title to use instead of "consultant" that speaks to my specific client, 5) Three types of organizations that are a bad fit for me — and why naming this builds credibility.

3. The Discovery Call Framework

Write a discovery call framework for qualifying consulting prospects. My consulting focus: [describe]. Typical engagement size: [$X]. Common reasons prospects don't convert: [no budget / wrong problem fit / no urgency / authority issue / not ready to pay]. Write a discovery call structure that: 1) Opens by understanding their situation before any pitching — what's the current state, what would they like it to be?, 2) Uncovers urgency — why is this a priority now? What happens if they don't solve it in the next 6 months?, 3) Identifies the economic buyer — who else is involved in this decision?, 4) Surfaces budget without asking "what's your budget?" directly, 5) Tests their readiness — are they a "we've decided to do this" client or a "we're exploring" client? Also write how to close the call with a clear next step — not "let me put together a proposal" but something that creates momentum.

Client Deliverables

4. The Executive Presentation Structure

Write an executive presentation structure for [consulting deliverable: assessment findings / recommendations / strategic plan / project status / program evaluation]. Client: [describe]. Audience: [executive team / board / steering committee / department heads]. Key message: [what is the most important thing they need to understand or decide?]. Build a presentation structure with: 1) Opening slide: the "so what" — what do they need to know before anything else?, 2) Situation — what's true that they already know (set context efficiently), 3) Complication — what's changed or what's wrong (the tension), 4) Resolution — what we recommend and why, 5) Implications and next steps — what changes, who does what, when. For each section: what goes on the slides vs. what goes in the speaker notes. Also: what do consulting presentations do wrong that makes executives check out?

5. The Client Report Template

Write a consulting report template for [report type: diagnostic assessment / market analysis / process improvement / technology evaluation / organizational review]. Client type: [describe]. What this report needs to accomplish: [inform a decision / justify a budget / secure executive alignment / summarize findings / other]. Build a report structure with: 1) Executive summary (1 page max): the 3-5 most important findings and the single most important recommendation — written for someone who may only read this section, 2) Methodology section: credibility without academic heaviness, 3) Findings section structure: how to present each finding with evidence, 4) Recommendations section: how to present recommendations so they're specific enough to act on and sequenced logically, 5) Appendix guidance: what goes in appendix vs. main report. Also write the executive summary for a hypothetical diagnostic report — show the format, not just describe it.

6. The Recommendation Presentation Builder

Help me build the argument for a recommendation that may face resistance. My recommendation: [describe what you're recommending]. The resistance: [why is this recommendation likely to be challenged — cost / organizational politics / change risk / prior attempts failed / competing recommendations]. Evidence I have: [describe the data, analysis, or benchmarks that support your recommendation]. Build: 1) The core logic chain: if A is true and B is true, then C follows — the argument structure, 2) How to address the strongest objection pre-emptively (before they raise it), 3) What proof or evidence would be most persuasive to this specific audience, 4) How to frame the cost of inaction — what happens if they do nothing, 5) How to present this recommendation in a way that makes saying no feel more uncomfortable than saying yes. Also: what do consultants consistently do in recommendation meetings that undermines their own recommendation?

Client Communication

7. The Difficult Client Situation Scripts

Help me handle these difficult consulting client situations. Situation: [choose — client is questioning the value of the engagement / client changes direction mid-project / client wants to expand scope without expanding budget / client is unresponsive and blocking progress / client is unhappy with a deliverable / client asks for a discount at renewal]. Write for each: 1) How to open the conversation without being defensive, 2) The core message — what needs to be said clearly, 3) What you're offering (if anything) and what you're not, 4) How to close the conversation with the relationship intact, 5) What to document. Also: what's the most common mistake consultants make when clients push back on value? Give me direct, specific guidance — not "focus on value" advice.

8. The Client Update & Relationship Maintenance System

Build a client communication and relationship maintenance system for a consulting practice. Current situation: [how many clients, types of engagements, how you currently communicate]. What's falling through the cracks: [identify the gaps — not enough proactive updates / relationships fade between engagements / clients leave without renewal conversation]. Build a system with: 1) Active engagement updates: what to send clients weekly during an engagement (under 200 words, focused on outcomes not activities), 2) Between-engagement maintenance: how to stay top of mind without being pushy — monthly touchpoint approach, 3) Annual relationship review: the conversation to have with every long-term client once a year, 4) The early expansion conversation: how to identify and raise additional consulting opportunities without it feeling like a sales call, 5) The referral ask: when and how to ask clients for referrals — word-for-word language for the ask. Write the monthly touchpoint email template in full.

9. The Scope Creep & Engagement Expansion Management

Help me manage scope in a consulting engagement. Situation: [describe what's happening — client keeps adding requests / engagement is consuming more time than budgeted / client expects ongoing availability that wasn't in scope / additional deliverables are being added informally]. My original scope: [describe what was agreed to]. What they're asking for now: [describe the additional requests]. Write: 1) How to document the original scope in a way that makes additions clear — the language to use, 2) The conversation to have when a new request comes in — how to acknowledge the request, check scope, and respond appropriately, 3) The change order process and how to present it as serving the client (not protecting yourself), 4) How to expand an engagement commercially — pricing and framing additional work, 5) What to do if a client refuses to pay for out-of-scope work that's already been done. Be specific — word-for-word where possible.

Thought Leadership & Marketing

10. The Thought Leadership Content Plan

Create a thought leadership content plan for a consultant building a market reputation. My specialty: [describe]. Target audience: [who should know about me — industry leaders / specific function executives / mid-market companies / startups / etc.]. Platforms I'll use: [LinkedIn / newsletter / speaking / podcast / articles / all]. Goal: [attract inbound clients / build referral network / establish authority in a specific area / position for speaking invitations]. Build a plan with: 1) My distinctive point of view — what do I believe that other consultants in my space don't? (This is what builds a following.), 2) 10 content ideas for this month that demonstrate expertise without giving away the engagement, 3) How to turn one client engagement insight (anonymized) into 3 different content pieces, 4) The one platform that generates the most business opportunities for consultants in my space, 5) How to measure whether thought leadership is actually working. Also write one complete LinkedIn post that demonstrates my POV.

11. The Case Study Builder

Write a consulting case study for a past engagement. Engagement details: [describe — what you did, for what type of client, what the situation was]. Results: [describe the outcomes — quantified where possible]. What I can share: [what's public vs. what needs to be anonymized]. Build a case study with: 1) A headline that leads with the result, not the work ("How a Mid-Size Manufacturer Cut Procurement Costs 23% in 6 Months"), 2) Client situation: the problem they faced — written to be recognizable to other clients in similar situations, 3) My approach: the distinctive thing I did that mattered — not a generic methodology description, 4) Results: specific, credible, appropriately qualified, 5) Quote format (or "as a result" language if no quote is available). Also: what do consulting case studies consistently do wrong that undermines their effectiveness? What's the one thing that would make this case study twice as persuasive?

12. The Speaking Proposal Template

Write a speaking proposal for [conference / industry event / corporate event / podcast]. My topic: [describe the talk or episode concept]. My audience: [who would benefit from hearing this]. What I offer: [describe your expertise and what makes your talk distinctive]. Event or show: [describe the type of event you're pitching]. Write: 1) The talk title — provocative, specific, creates curiosity, 2) A 150-word talk description for the conference program, 3) The 3-sentence speaker bio that establishes credibility without sounding like a resume, 4) The outreach email to the conference organizer or podcast host (under 200 words, leads with audience value), 5) How to increase acceptance rate: what do conference organizers look for that most speakers don't provide? Also: what's the biggest mistake consultants make when pitching speaking opportunities?

Practice Management & Growth

13. The Pricing & Positioning Review

Help me evaluate and optimize my consulting pricing. My current model: [hourly / daily rate / project-based / retainer / value-based / combination]. Current rates: $[X]. Types of engagements: [describe]. Revenue last year: $[X]. What I want to change: [charge more / move to value-based / add a retainer model / reduce hours while maintaining income]. Run a pricing review that: 1) Calculates my effective hourly rate across all work (including non-billable), 2) Identifies where I'm leaving money on the table — which engagements are underpriced relative to value delivered, 3) Recommends a specific pricing change and how to implement it with existing clients vs. new clients, 4) Designs a retainer option for my practice — what it includes and what it costs, 5) How to have the "my rates are increasing" conversation with a long-term client who will push back. Give me specific numbers and language — not principles.

14. The Consulting Partner & Alliance Strategy

Help me build a referral and alliance network for my consulting practice. My specialty: [describe]. Who refers clients to me currently: [describe — if no one, that's the answer]. Complementary consulting services (non-competing): [describe services that clients often need alongside yours — e.g., if you do strategy, implementation consulting; if you do HR, executive coaching]. Build a plan with: 1) The 3 most valuable types of referral partners for my specific specialty, 2) An outreach approach to potential partners that creates mutual value — not "I'll send you clients if you send me clients" but a real value proposition, 3) How to structure a formal referral arrangement ethically — what to agree to, what to document, 4) The most effective way to stay top of mind with referral partners without quarterly "checking in" emails, 5) A "co-create" strategy: one type of joint content or program that would strengthen a referral relationship. Write the outreach email for the highest-value partner type.

15. The Annual Consulting Practice Review

Help me review my consulting practice for the year. Data: Revenue: $[X]. Engagements: [X number, describe types]. Average engagement value: $[X]. Average engagement length: [X months]. Revenue by client source: [referrals / inbound / outbound / repeat / other]. Highest-margin engagements: [describe]. Most time-consuming relative to fee: [describe]. New clients vs. repeat: [ratio]. Run a review that: 1) Calculates my effective hourly rate including all non-billable time, 2) Identifies the engagement type with the best margin-to-effort ratio, 3) Recommends one change to my service design that would increase revenue without increasing hours, 4) Names the single client acquisition channel worth more investment next year, 5) Identifies what I should stop doing — a type of engagement or a type of client that's consuming capacity without proportionate return. Direct assessment — what's working, what's not, and what to change.
Pro tip: Start with Prompt 1 (Consulting Proposal Template) — it has the most direct revenue impact. A proposal that opens with a sharp articulation of the client's problem wins more often than one that leads with your methodology. Demonstrating that you understand their problem better than they do is the most powerful thing a consultant can do before an engagement begins.
