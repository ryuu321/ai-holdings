15 ChatGPT Prompts for Social Media Managers

Stop staring at blank drafts. These prompts help you create faster, report smarter, and manage more accounts without burning out.

Managing social media at scale means you're constantly switching between content creation, client communication, reporting, and strategy — often for 5+ accounts at once. AI can't replace your judgment, but it can eliminate the blank-page paralysis and tedious repetitive writing that eats your best hours.

These 15 prompts work for in-house social media managers, freelance SMMs, and agency teams managing multiple client accounts. Use them in ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini.

Content Creation

1. The Brand Voice Cloner

The hardest part of managing a new account is matching the brand voice. This prompt extracts it from existing content.

Analyze the brand voice from these [X] social media posts and create a voice guide I can reference for all future content. Posts: [paste 5-10 existing posts that represent the brand at its best]. Build a voice guide that covers: 1) Tone descriptors — 5 adjectives that describe how this brand sounds (and 5 it definitely doesn't sound like), 2) Sentence structure patterns — how does this brand structure its posts?, 3) Emoji usage — does it use them? How? Which types?, 4) Hashtag style — how does this brand use hashtags?, 5) Topics it gravitates toward and ones it avoids, 6) One example of what this brand would and wouldn't say about [common topic in their industry]. This is my style guide for this account.

2. The Monthly Content Calendar

Build a 4-week content calendar for [brand name] on [platform: Instagram/LinkedIn/Twitter/TikTok]. Brand: [describe — industry, product/service, target audience, tone]. Content pillars: [list 3-5 recurring content themes]. Posting frequency: [X times per week]. Upcoming events or promotions: [list any]. Build the calendar with: 1) One post concept per day (not just topics — actual angles and formats), 2) Post type for each (carousel, single image, Reel/video, poll, etc.), 3) Brief caption direction (2-3 sentences on what the caption should accomplish), 4) One CTA type per post (follow, comment, click link, save, share). Format as a simple table: Date | Content Type | Concept | Caption Direction | CTA.

3. The Caption Batch Writer

Write 5 captions for [brand name] on [platform] for this content theme: [describe the theme or campaign]. Brand voice: [describe in 3-4 adjectives]. Audience: [describe]. Each caption should: 1) Open with a hook that stops the scroll (first line only — readers won't click "more"), 2) Deliver value or entertainment before the CTA, 3) End with a specific CTA (vary them — not all "click the link"), 4) Include relevant hashtags if appropriate for this platform. Also write one caption that's intentionally more vulnerable/personal for the brand — sometimes this performs best. Mark it clearly so I can decide whether to use it.

4. The Trending Audio/Topic Adapter

I need to jump on [trending topic/audio/format] for [brand name] in [industry]. The trend: [describe what it is — meme, audio, challenge, format]. Brand's industry: [describe]. Their tone: [describe]. Write 3 ways this brand could participate in this trend authentically: 1) A direct interpretation that fits the trend format exactly, 2) A brand-relevant twist that connects the trend to their product/service/niche, 3) A "meta" take that acknowledges the trend without forcing it. For each, write the caption and describe the visual or video concept. Flag if any of these feels forced — I'd rather skip the trend than look desperate.

Strategy & Reporting

5. The Monthly Report Narrative

Numbers without narrative don't convince clients. This prompt writes the story behind the stats.

Write the narrative section of my monthly social media report for [client name]. This month's data: Followers: [X] ([+/-] from last month). Reach: [X]. Impressions: [X]. Engagement rate: [X%]. Top performing post: [describe]. Lowest performing post: [describe]. Any notable events: [launches, campaigns, crises]. Write a report narrative that: 1) Opens with the one headline win from this month, 2) Explains what drove top performance and what we should do more of, 3) Addresses underperformance honestly without dwelling on it, 4) Gives 3 specific recommendations for next month, 5) Ends with a forward-looking statement on the trend. Tone: confident, data-informed, professional. Under 300 words.

6. The Content Audit

Help me audit [brand name]'s social content performance. Here's the data from the last 90 days by post type: [paste data — can be rough numbers like "carousels avg 4.2% ER, singles 1.8%, Reels 6.1%"]. Top 5 posts by engagement: [describe each briefly]. Run an audit that: 1) Identifies which content types are outperforming and by how much, 2) Identifies patterns in the top performers (time of day, topic, format, hook style), 3) Recommends a new content mix based on the data, 4) Names one content type we should retire or reduce, 5) Identifies one gap — what content format are we NOT testing that we should? Give me a clear recommendation, not a list of things to "consider."

7. The Competitor Analysis Framework

Build a competitor analysis for [brand name] vs [3 competitors]. For each competitor, I'll observe: posting frequency, content mix, engagement rates, follower count, and content themes. Create a framework I can fill in monthly that: 1) Shows where we're ahead and behind on each metric, 2) Identifies content gaps — what topics or formats are competitors doing well that we're not?, 3) Shows opportunities — where are competitors weak that we can own?, 4) Gives a monthly action: one thing to test based on what competitors are doing (or NOT doing). Format as a template I can reuse each month.

Client Management

8. The Client Onboarding Questionnaire

Write a client onboarding questionnaire for a new social media management client. I need to understand their brand, goals, and constraints before I can manage their accounts effectively. Include questions in these categories: 1) Brand identity (voice, values, what they love and hate about their current content), 2) Audience (who they're actually reaching vs. who they want to reach), 3) Goals (specific metrics, not "grow our following"), 4) Approval process (who approves, turnaround time, what requires legal review), 5) Past performance (what's worked, what hasn't, any past crises or sensitivities), 6) Logistics (access, scheduling tools, posting preferences). Keep it under 25 questions — long questionnaires don't get completed. Mark the 10 most critical questions.

9. The Difficult Client Email

Write an email to a client about this situation: [choose — they want to post something I think is a bad idea / they're unhappy with results but haven't given us good content to work with / they keep requesting changes after approval / they're comparing our results to a competitor using vanity metrics / they want to cancel]. Write an email that: 1) Acknowledges their concern or request without being defensive, 2) Presents my professional perspective clearly and specifically (not vague), 3) Proposes a concrete solution or next step, 4) Maintains the relationship even if we disagree. Give me the word-for-word email, not an outline. Under 150 words.

10. The Scope Expansion Pitch

I manage [platform(s)] for [client name] and want to propose expanding to [new platform/service]. Client background: [describe their business, goals, current results with me]. Why this expansion makes sense: [describe the opportunity you've spotted]. Write a pitch email that: 1) Opens with a result or insight from our current work together (establishes trust), 2) Presents the opportunity in terms of their goals (not just "more social media"), 3) Proposes a specific package with clear deliverables and pricing ($[X]/month), 4) Addresses the likely objection: "We're already spending enough on social." Keep it under 200 words — this is a conversation starter, not a proposal document.

Crisis & Community Management

11. The Crisis Response Framework

Social media crises move fast. Having a framework ready means you're not writing policy while the comments flood in.

Build a social media crisis response framework for [brand name] in [industry]. Common risks for this type of brand: [list 2-3 scenarios that could realistically happen — product issue, employee misconduct, controversial topic, viral negative review, etc.]. Build a framework that includes: 1) A decision tree: when to respond publicly vs. privately vs. not at all, 2) Response templates for each scenario (fill-in-the-blank format), 3) Escalation protocol: what requires legal/PR/leadership sign-off, 4) The 1-hour rule: what must happen in the first 60 minutes of a crisis, 5) Post-crisis review: what to assess after it's over. Keep it short enough to actually use under pressure — a 10-page document won't help at 11pm.

12. The Community Comment Response Bank

Create a comment response bank for [brand name] that I can use and adapt quickly. Brand tone: [describe]. Create response templates for: 1) Positive comments — 5 responses that feel genuine, not corporate (vary them so they don't all sound the same), 2) Product questions — 3 templates for "where can I buy" / "do you ship to X" / "what's the price" type questions, 3) Complaints — 3 templates that acknowledge, empathize, and direct to DM without being dismissive, 4) Trolls and off-topic comments — 2 templates for when to engage and when to ignore, 5) Partnership/collab requests — 1 template that's warm but doesn't commit. These are starting points — I'll personalize every response.

Efficiency & Systems

13. The Content Repurposing Machine

Take this piece of content and repurpose it for 5 different formats: [paste the original content — blog post, podcast transcript, interview, newsletter, etc.]. Repurpose into: 1) Instagram carousel (5-7 slides — give me the headline for each slide), 2) LinkedIn post (under 300 words, professional tone, specific insight as the hook), 3) Twitter/X thread (5-7 tweets — hook tweet + supporting points + CTA), 4) Short-form video script (60 seconds max — hook in first 3 seconds), 5) Email subject line + 3-sentence preview for newsletter. For each format, adapt the content for that platform's culture — don't just copy-paste.

14. The Hashtag Strategy Builder

Build a hashtag strategy for [brand name] on [platform]. Niche: [describe]. Audience: [describe]. Current follower count: [X]. Build a strategy with: 1) 3 tiers of hashtags — large (1M+ posts), medium (100K-1M), small (under 100K) — give specific examples in each tier, 2) Branded hashtag recommendation — one unique hashtag this brand should own, 3) Rules for usage — how many per post, where in the caption, when to rotate, 4) 5 hashtags to avoid for this brand (too broad, too spammy, or misaligned), 5) How to evaluate if a hashtag is performing — what to look for in analytics. Give me 30 specific hashtags organized by tier, ready to use.

15. The Client Pitch Deck Narrative

Write the narrative for a social media management pitch to [potential client type]. Their business: [describe]. Their current social presence: [describe what you've observed — platforms, frequency, engagement, what's missing]. My services: [list what you offer]. Monthly investment: $[X]. Write pitch content that: 1) Opens with a specific observation about their current social presence (shows you've done your homework), 2) Articulates the gap between where they are and where they could be, 3) Explains your approach in 3 clear phases (what happens in month 1, 2-3, and ongoing), 4) Addresses the ROI question — how will they know it's working?, 5) Closes with a specific ask (30-minute call, not "let me know if you're interested"). Under 400 words total.
Pro tip: Run Prompt 1 (Brand Voice Cloner) for every new client before you write a single post. It turns an implicit understanding of "their vibe" into a document you can reference and share with anyone who touches the account. It also makes client feedback easier to handle — you can point to the voice guide instead of arguing about what "sounds right."
