ChatGPT Prompts for Brand Strategists

15 free prompts to build sharper positioning, write compelling brand narratives, and deliver client work in half the time.

Brand strategy is 80% thinking and 20% writing — but that 20% can take forever. Positioning statements, messaging frameworks, brand voice guidelines, competitive analyses — these documents demand precision and creativity at the same time. These 15 prompts handle the writing scaffolding so you can focus on the strategic thinking that clients actually pay for.

1. Brand Discovery

Prompt 1 — Brand Discovery Interview Questions

Build a brand discovery interview guide for a new client engagement. Client type: [startup / rebranding established company / personal brand / B2B service / consumer product] Stage of business: [pre-launch / growth / maturity / repositioning] Key unknowns I need to resolve: [target audience clarity / competitive differentiation / brand personality / reason-to-believe / what to stop saying] Create: (1) 20 discovery questions organized by theme — origin story → current positioning → competitive landscape → target audience → aspirations → brand personality → what clients currently say vs. what they want to say, (2) 5 projective questions (what car would this brand be? what does it feel like to be a customer?) that surface emotional truth faster than direct questions, (3) Questions to ask employees (vs. founders) to reveal the gap between intended and actual brand, (4) How to synthesize conflicting answers into a positioning hypothesis. The best discovery reveals what clients cannot articulate on their own.
Pro tip: Ask "What do you never want someone to say about your brand?" before asking "What do you want someone to say?" Constraints often reveal positioning more clearly than aspirations.

Prompt 2 — Competitive Landscape Analysis

Help me structure a competitive brand analysis for [industry / category]. Category: [describe the market] Key competitors to analyze: [list 5–8] Dimensions to map: [positioning / messaging tone / visual identity / target audience / price point / brand promise / content strategy] Client's current position: [describe] Goal: [find white space / understand how to differentiate / identify messaging clichés to avoid] Create: (1) Competitive mapping framework (axes to use, how to plot each brand), (2) For each competitor — brand archetype, primary message, what they are saying vs. what they are not saying, (3) Category clichés — language, themes, and claims that every brand in this category overuses, (4) White space opportunity brief — what is being said by no one that could own a meaningful position. The goal is not to describe the competition; it is to find the gap.

Prompt 3 — Target Audience Persona Development

Develop target audience personas for a brand strategy project. Brand: [describe] Available data: [customer interviews / analytics / market research / founder intuition only] Segments to explore: [list 2–4 distinct audience groups] What I need to understand: [motivations / barriers / language they use / where they spend attention / what they believe that makes them open to this brand] For each persona: (1) Profile (demographics are secondary — lead with psychographics and worldview), (2) What they are trying to achieve (the job they are hiring this brand to do), (3) What they believe that makes them receptive to this category, (4) The language they actually use (not marketing language), (5) Where and how they discover brands like this, (6) What would make them choose this brand over alternatives, (7) Their biggest barrier to purchase or commitment. Do not write generic "marketing personas" — write psychologically real people.
Pro tip: The single most useful persona element is the exact language your audience uses to describe their problem. If your brand speaks that language back, everything else in the strategy gets easier.

2. Positioning

Prompt 4 — Positioning Statement Generator

Help me develop positioning statement options for a brand. Brand: [describe product/service] Target audience: [specific — not everyone] Category: [what category does this brand play in] Key insight: [what is true about this audience that most brands miss] Functional benefit: [what it does] Emotional benefit: [how it makes them feel] RTB (reason to believe): [proof point or credibility anchor] Competitors: [main alternatives] Write 5 positioning statement variations, each using a different strategic angle: (1) Category creation — reframe the category itself, (2) Target audience specificity — own a specific audience others ignore, (3) Benefit ladder — climb from functional to emotional to self-expressive, (4) Competitive repositioning — make competitor strength into a weakness, (5) Origin/mission — lead with why. For each: write the internal positioning statement AND a one-sentence consumer-facing version. Then identify which one you would stake the brand on and why.

Prompt 5 — Brand Architecture

Help me define the brand architecture for [company with multiple products/services/sub-brands]. Parent brand: [describe] Sub-brands or product lines: [list them with brief descriptions] Current confusion points: [where does the market get confused about what belongs to what] Business context: [why this architecture matters now — M&A / new product launch / repositioning / international expansion] Evaluate: (1) Which architecture model fits best — branded house / house of brands / hybrid — and why, (2) What each sub-brand should keep vs. align with the parent, (3) How to name new products/services within this architecture, (4) The naming conventions and rules to apply going forward, (5) How to explain this architecture to internal stakeholders who will be confused by it. Brand architecture decisions outlast most brand decisions — they deserve rigorous thinking.

3. Messaging

Prompt 6 — Brand Messaging Framework

Write a brand messaging framework for [brand]. Positioning: [one sentence — who is this brand for, what does it do, how is it different] Brand promise: [the core commitment to the customer] Key messages by audience: [list 2–3 audiences with different needs] Proof points: [specific evidence that makes the promise believable] What we should never say: [off-brand language, clichés to avoid] Deliver: (1) Master brand narrative (200 words — the brand story in plain language, from the customer's perspective), (2) Value proposition — one sentence for each audience segment, (3) Messaging pillars (3–4 themes every communication should reinforce), (4) Proof points for each pillar, (5) Elevator pitch (30 seconds / 2 minutes / 5 minutes — three versions for different contexts), (6) What this brand sounds like vs. what it does not sound like (with examples). This framework becomes the reference for every writer who ever touches this brand.

Prompt 7 — Brand Voice and Tone Guide

Develop a brand voice and tone guide for [brand]. Brand personality adjectives: [list 4–6 — not generic ones like "authentic" and "innovative"] Target audience: [describe — their age, sensibility, what they respond to] Communication channels: [website / social / email / packaging / sales / support] Examples I like (other brands): [list 2–3 and what specifically I like about each] What we are not: [what this brand explicitly should not sound like] Create: (1) Voice definition — what the brand personality sounds like in words, (2) 3 voice pillars with "we are this, not that" examples (e.g., "Direct, not blunt"), (3) Tone variations by context — how voice flexes across channels and situations (excited product launch vs. error message vs. customer support), (4) Before/after rewrites — take 5 generic copy examples and rewrite them in brand voice, (5) Common phrases to use and avoid, (6) How to test whether something is on-brand voice. A voice guide without examples is useless — make it concrete.
Pro tip: The most useful voice guide tells people what NOT to do. "We are confident, not arrogant" is more actionable than a list of adjectives.

4. Client Deliverables

Prompt 8 — Brand Strategy Presentation Structure

Help me structure a brand strategy presentation for client review. What I am presenting: [positioning / full brand strategy / messaging framework / rebrand rationale / brand architecture] Client audience: [CEO / marketing team / board / investors] Client personality: [analytical and data-driven / creative and visionary / skeptical and conservative / collaborative] Key strategic decisions I need them to make: [list 2–3] Time available: [30 / 60 / 90 minutes] Presentation structure: (1) Opening frame — what we are here to do and decide (not what we found), (2) The strategic insight (what we learned that changes how to think about this brand), (3) The strategic choice (usually 2–3 options with tradeoffs — not one answer), (4) Recommended direction with rationale, (5) What this means in practice (show the idea applied), (6) Next steps and decisions needed. Also: how to handle a client who wants to "move the strategy to be safer" and how to protect the work while keeping the relationship.

Prompt 9 — Naming Brief and Process

Help me run a naming project for [new brand / product / service / sub-brand]. What needs naming: [describe] Strategic criteria (in priority order): [memorability / meaningfulness / distinctiveness / global suitability / available as .com / trademarkable / easy to spell] Audience: [who will hear and use this name] Competitive context: [what naming conventions does the category use — and should we follow or break them] Examples of names I like (from any category): [list 3–5 with what you like about each] Deliver: (1) Naming brief (criteria, process, timeline, decision makers), (2) 5 naming directions with rationale (descriptive / suggestive / abstract / invented / existing word), (3) 15 name candidates organized by direction, (4) Evaluation scorecard to assess each name, (5) Questions to ask in client naming reviews to surface real preferences vs. stated ones. Naming is where strategy meets creativity — run the process, or it becomes chaos.

5. Thought Leadership

Prompt 10 — LinkedIn Article for Brand Strategist

Write a LinkedIn article on a brand strategy topic. Topic options: [the most common brand positioning mistake I see / why "authentic" is the least authentic word in branding / how to tell if your brand has a positioning problem / the difference between brand strategy and brand identity / what makes a brand name great / why most mission statements fail] Chosen topic: [paste] My perspective: [what do I actually believe about this — the contrarian or nuanced take] Proof from my experience: [general example, no client names] Target reader: [marketing leaders / startup founders / brand managers / fellow strategists] Write: Hook (the first 2 sentences — they must stop the scroll) → Setup (what most people believe) → Tension (why that is wrong or incomplete) → My perspective (the real insight) → Practical implication (what to do differently) → Close (provocative question or call to action). Under 600 words. This is thought leadership, not a textbook entry.
Pro tip: The best brand strategy LinkedIn posts disagree with something commonly accepted. Agreement is forgettable. A clear "I think this is wrong, and here is why" is what gets shared.

Prompt 11 — Case Study Write-Up

Help me write a case study for a completed brand strategy project. Project overview (anonymized if needed): [what type of brand, what the challenge was, what I did] The strategic insight: [what made this project interesting or hard] What changed: [positioning / messaging / naming / architecture / all of the above] Results (if available): [what happened after — or if results are not measured, what the client said] Write: (1) 200-word summary version (for website portfolio), (2) 500-word long version (for proposals or thought leadership), (3) 3 "sound bite" quotes the client might say about the work (so I can prompt them for a real testimonial), (4) How to anonymize the case study appropriately when client NDAs prevent full disclosure. Good case studies are not project summaries — they are strategic stories.

6. Client Management

Prompt 12 — Strategic Recommendation Email

Help me write an email recommending a strategic direction after a discovery phase. Project: [describe] My recommendation: [which direction and why] Concerns I anticipate from the client: [list 2–3] What I need from them: [a decision / a meeting / approval to move forward] Write: (1) Email that recommends a direction without being passive (do not bury the lead) → acknowledge what we learned in discovery → state the recommended direction clearly → give the rationale in 3 points → address the likely concern before they raise it → propose next step with deadline, (2) How to handle if they push back with "can we make it a little safer?" — where to hold the line and where to flex, (3) What to do if they are genuinely split between two directions. Confident recommendation emails win client trust faster than hedging.

Prompt 13 — Project Scope and Proposal

Write a project scope and proposal for a brand strategy engagement. Client: [type of company — do not use name] Project scope: [what I will deliver — discovery / positioning / messaging / naming / brand voice / full strategy] Timeline: [weeks or months] My pricing: [insert or TBD] What makes this engagement different from a cheaper alternative: [my process, my experience, my deliverables] Biggest client concern: [price / timeline / whether strategy actually leads to anything] Write: (1) Proposal intro (speak to their situation and goal — not my credentials), (2) Scope of work with clear deliverables and what is not included, (3) Process overview (what each phase involves, why it matters), (4) Timeline and milestones, (5) Investment section (how to frame the price — not "here is the cost" — what this enables), (6) FAQ section for common objections, (7) Next steps. Proposals that sell the outcome win more than proposals that list the process.

7. Professional Development

Prompt 14 — Strategy Debrief After Project

Help me run a strategic debrief after completing a brand project. Project outcome: [successful and client loved it / client pushed back on final direction / results were mixed / we ran out of time and scope crept] What I am proud of: [the thinking, the work, the relationship] What I would do differently: [honest assessment] What I learned about my process: [what worked, what did not] What I learned about this type of client or category: [generalizable insight] Structure a 30-minute self-debrief: (1) What was the real strategic problem vs. the stated problem, (2) Where did my thinking break down or where was I right earlier than I realized, (3) What would I tell myself before starting this project, (4) One process change to make before the next similar project, (5) One piece of strategic insight from this project worth keeping in my "library." The best strategists learn more from their projects than their clients do.

Prompt 15 — Annual Practice / Career Review

Help me review my brand strategy practice or career this year. Projects completed: [number and types] Most interesting strategic challenge: [describe] Revenue or compensation: [$] Client relationships that went well vs. poorly: [describe patterns] Skills I developed: [strategic thinking / writing / client management / business development] What I am moving toward: [senior IC / agency leadership / in-house / independent consultancy] Goal for next year: [specific] Review: (1) What types of work brought out my best strategic thinking — and how to get more of it, (2) The client relationship pattern that most affects my work quality — and what to do about it, (3) Where my positioning as a strategist is strong vs. where it is generic, (4) The portfolio gap that is most limiting my career or business growth, (5) The honest answer: what am I avoiding that would most accelerate where I want to go?
