ChatGPT Prompts for Cybersecurity Professionals

15 free prompts to write better security reports, communicate risk to executives, and build awareness programs that employees actually engage with.

Cybersecurity professionals are among the most technically expert and least understood people in an organization. The gap between what you know and what leadership acts on is almost always a communication problem. These 15 prompts help you close that gap — writing clearly about risk without dumbing it down, and building awareness programs that change behavior instead of generating checkbox compliance.

Important: Never input actual vulnerability details, system configurations, network architecture, credentials, incident data, or any sensitive organizational information into AI tools. Use AI for communication scaffolding only. All security content must be reviewed by qualified security personnel before use. These prompts are for documentation and communication tasks, not for offensive security or vulnerability research.

1. Executive Communication

Prompt 1 — Risk Report for Non-Technical Executives

Help me write a risk report for executive leadership or the board. Risk area: [describe in general terms — e.g., patch management backlog / third-party vendor risk / phishing susceptibility / access control gaps / cloud misconfiguration] Current state: [general level of exposure — high/medium/low — with context on what that means] Business impact: [what a breach or incident in this area could cost — downtime / regulatory fines / reputational damage / specific business process disruption] What I am recommending: [specific investment, policy change, or remediation effort] Budget or resource ask: [what you need approved] Write: (1) Executive summary (5 sentences — risk in business language, not technical language), (2) Business impact section (translate technical risk to dollar/operational terms), (3) Recommended action with cost-benefit framing, (4) What happens if nothing changes (the cost of inaction), (5) Success metrics — how leadership will know the risk was addressed. Never use technical acronyms without defining them. Write for a CEO, not an analyst.
Pro tip: "We have 847 unpatched CVEs" means nothing to a CEO. "Our most critical systems have 23 unaddressed vulnerabilities — three of them are actively exploited in attacks on companies like ours" means everything.

Prompt 2 — Board Cybersecurity Presentation

Structure a cybersecurity update presentation for the board of directors. Audience: [board members — legal / finance / ops backgrounds, not technical] Update type: [quarterly security posture review / post-incident briefing / strategic security investment request / regulatory compliance update] Key messages I need to convey: [list 3] Decisions I need from the board: [specific — approve budget / adopt policy / acknowledge risk acceptance] Structure (10 slides): (1) Current threat landscape (what is happening in our industry — 1 slide, business language), (2) Our security posture headline (simple rating or trend — improving / holding / declining), (3) Top 3 risks the board should be aware of (business impact focus), (4) What we have done this quarter (wins — brief), (5) Where we need investment (with business case), (6) Regulatory and compliance status, (7) Peer benchmarking (how we compare to similar organizations — if available), (8) Decision needed from the board, (9) Key metrics trend (simple chart concepts), (10) Q&A prep — the 5 questions boards most commonly ask. Boards are governance bodies, not technical reviewers — frame everything through the lens of: what do they need to make decisions?

Prompt 3 — Incident Communication to Leadership

Write an incident communication template for informing leadership during or after a security incident. Incident type: [general — do not describe actual incidents — e.g., phishing compromise / ransomware / data exposure / third-party breach notification] Communication stage: [initial notification (incident is active) / update (incident contained) / post-incident summary] Audience: [CISO to CEO / IT to business unit leaders / CEO to board] What they need to know right now: [what happened / what is being done / what is the impact / what do they need to do] Write: (1) Initial notification template (under 200 words — what we know, what we do not know, what we are doing, next update in X hours), (2) Status update template (what changed, current status, timeline estimate), (3) Post-incident summary for leadership (what happened, timeline, impact, root cause, what we are doing to prevent recurrence), (4) What NOT to say during an active incident (language that creates legal or reputational risk). Clear, calm communication during incidents is half the incident response.

2. Security Awareness

Prompt 4 — Security Awareness Training Content

Write security awareness training content that employees actually engage with. Topics: [phishing recognition / password hygiene / data classification / social engineering / safe remote working / vendor and third-party risks / physical security / incident reporting — choose 2–3] Audience: [general employees / executives / IT staff / customer-facing teams] Format: [email / short video script / quiz / intranet article / poster / presentation] Tone: [informative but not fear-based / engaging / practical — not compliance checkbox] For each topic: (1) The hook (why this matters to them personally, not just to the company), (2) The key concept in plain language (2–3 sentences, no jargon), (3) What an attack actually looks like (realistic scenario), (4) What to do (specific action steps), (5) One question to test understanding. Also: what makes awareness training get ignored vs. what makes it stick — and how to apply that here.

Prompt 5 — Phishing Simulation Communication

Write the communications around a phishing simulation program. Program goal: [measure click rates / improve reporting behavior / reduce susceptibility over time] Current employee awareness level: [describe — never done a simulation / had one before / ongoing program] Post-click experience: [landing page that educates / training module / just a notification] Tone: [supportive and educational — NOT punitive / shaming] Write: (1) Pre-simulation announcement (optional — some programs do not pre-announce) — frame it as a learning program, not a test with consequences, (2) Post-click training page message (when an employee clicks the simulated phish — empathetic, educational, not shaming), (3) Program result announcement email to employees (what the results showed, what it means, what to do next — not "X% of you failed"), (4) Manager briefing (how to discuss results with teams without creating blame culture), (5) The metrics that actually matter vs. click rate alone. Phishing programs that create shame do not improve security — they just go underground.
Pro tip: The goal of phishing simulation is improved reporting behavior, not zero click rates. Employees who report suspicious emails — even when they are wrong — are your best security asset.

3. Documentation

Prompt 6 — Security Policy Plain-Language Rewrite

Rewrite a security policy section in plain language for employees. Policy type: [acceptable use / password policy / data classification / incident reporting / remote work / BYOD / vendor access] Original policy excerpt: [paste the technical or legalistic language] Audience: [all employees — not just IT] Goal: [make it understandable so employees actually follow it] Rewrite: (1) Plain-language version (what does this policy actually mean in everyday terms), (2) What employees need to do differently because of this policy (specific actions), (3) What counts as a violation and what to do if they are unsure, (4) FAQ (3 questions employees will ask when they read this), (5) One-paragraph "why this matters" section — the human reason, not the legal reason. Policies that employees cannot understand are not followed.

Prompt 7 — Incident Report Write-Up

Help me structure a post-incident report. Incident type: [general — describe the category without sensitive detail, e.g., "credential-based account compromise" / "successful phishing leading to email forwarding rule" / "misconfigurated cloud storage exposure"] Report audience: [internal IT team / CISO / legal / external auditor / regulatory body] What I need to document: [timeline / impact / root cause / response actions / lessons learned / remediation] Write: (1) Report structure (sections and what to cover in each), (2) Timeline section template (how to document the sequence clearly and accurately), (3) Impact section (how to quantify when exact numbers are not known — ranges, affected system counts, data categories), (4) Root cause analysis framework (5 Whys applied to security incidents), (5) Lessons learned section that leads to action — not just what happened, but what changes. All information in the report should be verified — do not speculate in formal incident reports.

4. Vendor & Third-Party Security

Prompt 8 — Vendor Security Questionnaire Narrative

Help me write narrative sections of a vendor security questionnaire. Questionnaire type: [SIG / CAIQ / custom questionnaire from a large enterprise customer] Sections needing narrative response: [describe — e.g., incident response process / data handling practices / access control approach / business continuity] Our organization's actual posture: [describe in general terms what is in place — no sensitive configuration details] Tone: [accurate and confidence-building without overstating] Write: For each section — (1) A professional narrative response that accurately describes capabilities without creating audit risk, (2) What to avoid saying (vague assurances that auditors see through / unsupportable claims / commitments you cannot keep), (3) How to handle sections where your posture is weaker than the question implies (honest language that does not create alarm), (4) When to loop in legal before answering. Vendor questionnaire responses are contractual representations — accuracy matters as much as presentation.

Prompt 9 — Third-Party Risk Communication

Write communications around a third-party security risk finding. Situation: [a vendor has a poor security posture / we received a breach notification from a vendor / a vendor cannot meet our security requirements] Internal audience: [procurement / business owner who manages the vendor relationship / executive who approved the vendor] External audience: [the vendor themselves] What I need to communicate: [the risk / our requirements / the decision needed / the timeline] Write: (1) Internal memo to the business owner (here is what we found, here is what it means, here is the decision we need you to make), (2) Email to the vendor requesting remediation (professional, specific, deadline-driven, not adversarial), (3) Escalation memo if vendor does not remediate (to executive leadership — what is at stake, what options exist), (4) Risk acceptance memo template if the business decides to accept the risk (what was communicated, who decided, what compensating controls exist).

5. Career & Thought Leadership

Prompt 10 — Security Blog or LinkedIn Post

Write a thought leadership post on a cybersecurity topic. Topics: [why security awareness training fails / the conversation I have with every CISO about board communication / what most companies get wrong about zero trust / the human element most security programs ignore / the difference between compliance and security / why the CISO should report to the CEO, not the CIO] Chosen topic: [paste] My angle: [what do I actually believe — the contrarian or nuanced take based on real experience] Audience: [security practitioners / business leaders / general tech audience] Write: Hook (the first 2 sentences — name a common belief and challenge it) → Setup (what the conventional approach looks like) → Why it fails or is incomplete → What actually works based on real experience → One practical takeaway. Under 600 words. Security thought leadership that states an opinion and defends it with experience gets shared more than "here are 5 tips."
Pro tip: The security content that performs best on LinkedIn is not technical content — it is content that translates security into business language that non-security leaders can act on.

Prompt 11 — Certification and Career Development Plan

Build a career development plan for a cybersecurity professional. Current role and level: [SOC analyst / security engineer / pentester / GRC analyst / CISO / security architect] Certifications I have: [list] Career goal: [next role / CISO track / specialist depth / consulting / specific domain] Timeline: [1 year / 3 years / 5 years] Learning style: [self-study / structured courses / hands-on labs / mentorship] Time available: [hours per week] Create: (1) Recommended certification path with rationale (not just "get CISSP" — why and when), (2) Skills gap between current role and target role, (3) 12-month learning plan (month by month — what to study, what to build, what to demonstrate), (4) How to build visibility in the security community (speaking / blogging / open source contribution / community involvement), (5) The mentor or network investment that matters most for this career path.

6. Team & Culture

Prompt 12 — Security Culture Assessment

Help me assess and improve security culture in my organization. Current state: [employees treat security as IT's problem / security and business are adversarial / employees report suspicious activity / security is embedded in product development — describe honestly] Biggest friction point: [employees see security as slowing them down / leadership does not prioritize it / security team is seen as the "department of no" / recent incident that damaged trust] What I want to change: [specific behavior or attitude shift] Create: (1) Security culture assessment framework (10 dimensions to measure, with how to assess each), (2) The 5 behaviors that indicate healthy security culture vs. unhealthy, (3) How to shift from compliance mindset to security ownership mindset (specific interventions), (4) How to make the security team approachable without compromising rigor, (5) How to celebrate security wins visibly — what to celebrate and how. Security culture is changed by behavior, not by awareness.

Prompt 13 — Hiring and Onboarding Security Talent

Write job descriptions and onboarding materials for security roles. Role: [SOC analyst / security engineer / GRC analyst / pentester / security architect / CISO] Team size and structure: [describe] What makes this role compelling: [interesting technical challenges / mission-driven organization / good culture / specific tech stack / growth opportunity] What candidates in this space care about: [technical depth / work-life balance / remote flexibility / learning budget / impact / team quality] Write: (1) Job posting that attracts strong candidates and filters weak ones (specific about what day-to-day looks like — not vague "fast-paced environment"), (2) Interview process overview (what the candidate will experience — transparency reduces drop-off), (3) 90-day onboarding plan for a new security hire (what they should know, who they should meet, what they should own by day 90), (4) The one question in the interview that tells you the most about a security candidate's mindset.

7. Annual Review

Prompt 14 — Security Program Annual Review

Help me prepare an annual security program review. Program components: [incident response / vulnerability management / security awareness / access control / third-party risk / endpoint security / cloud security / GRC — list what you manage] Wins this year: [list 3–5 — be specific] Incidents or near-misses: [general — what happened and what you learned] Metrics trend: [what improved / what did not / what you could not measure] Budget used vs. planned: [rough] Goal for next year: [specific security objective] Structure a 10-slide annual review presentation for CISO or leadership: (1) Year-in-review headline (one sentence — better / same / harder than last year and why), (2) Threat landscape change (what changed in 2026 that affects us), (3) Program wins, (4) What we would do differently, (5) Metrics that matter (trend over 3 years if possible), (6) Incident summary and lessons, (7) Top risks going into next year, (8) Strategic priorities for next year, (9) Budget request and ROI framing, (10) Q&A prep.

Prompt 15 — Personal Security Career Annual Review

Help me review my cybersecurity career this year. Role: [current title and team] Projects or initiatives I led: [list 3–5] Most impactful contribution: [describe — what changed because of your work] Skills I developed: [technical / soft skills / leadership] Where I fell short: [honest] What I want next: [promotion / new domain / consulting / CISO track / other] Write: (1) Self-review narrative for performance review (400 words — leads with business impact, not technical activities), (2) How to articulate the value of security work in business terms (not "I patched 400 CVEs" — "I reduced our critical vulnerability exposure by X% and prevented an estimated $Xm in potential breach cost"), (3) How to make the case for what I want next year, (4) The technical or leadership gap most limiting my progression, (5) The honest question: what am I afraid of that is keeping me from the career I want in security?
