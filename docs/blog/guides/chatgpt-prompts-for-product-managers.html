ChatGPT Prompts for Product Managers

15 free prompts to write better PRDs, prioritize ruthlessly, align stakeholders, run sharper roadmap reviews, and advance your PM career.

Product management is a writing job as much as a strategy job. You're constantly translating between engineers, designers, executives, and customers — and the quality of that translation determines whether your product gets built, funded, and adopted. These 15 prompts make the writing faster so your thinking can go deeper.

1. Discovery & Research

Prompt 1 — User Interview Guide

Write a user interview guide for product discovery. Problem space I'm exploring: [what behavior, pain, or opportunity you're investigating] User segment: [who I'm interviewing] Interview goal: [understand current behavior / validate hypothesis / explore unmet need] Interview length: [30 / 45 / 60 minutes] What I already believe (to challenge): [your hypothesis or assumption] Create: (1) Warm-up questions (2–3, to build rapport and set context), (2) Core behavioral questions (8–10 — past behavior, not opinions about future), (3) Probing questions for each topic, (4) A question that directly challenges my hypothesis, (5) Wrap-up and "what else" question. Flag: any leading questions that would bias responses. The goal is to learn what's true, not confirm what I already think.
Pro tip: Ask "tell me about the last time you..." not "would you ever..." Past behavior is real data. Hypothetical responses are speculation.

Prompt 2 — Problem Statement Sharpener

Help me sharpen my problem statement. Draft problem statement: [paste yours] What we know from research: [key findings — quotes, observations, data] Who this affects: [user segment and scale] Current workarounds: [how users solve this today] Why we care (business): [what this unlocks for the company] Refine the problem statement to: (1) Be specific about who has the problem, (2) Describe the behavior gap (what they're trying to do vs. what actually happens), (3) Quantify the scope if possible, (4) Not imply a solution. Also: flag if this problem statement is actually a solution in disguise — that's the most common mistake.

2. Requirements & Documentation

Prompt 3 — PRD / Product Brief Writer

Write a Product Requirements Document (PRD) for this feature. Feature name: [name] Problem it solves: [why this matters — user and business] Target user: [who this is for] Success metrics: [how we'll know this worked] Out of scope: [what this does NOT include] Key user flows: [describe the main paths] Open questions: [what's still undecided] Dependencies: [what this relies on to ship] PRD sections: Overview → Problem + Opportunity → Goals (user + business) → Non-goals → Requirements (user stories or functional requirements) → Success metrics → Open questions → Appendix (research references). Keep it under 2 pages for a feature, 4 pages for an initiative. PMs who write shorter PRDs ship faster.
Pro tip: A PRD should answer "why are we building this?" before it answers "what are we building?" Engineers who understand the why make better technical decisions.

Prompt 4 — User Story Writer

Write user stories for [feature or flow]. Feature context: [what the feature does] User types involved: [all user roles that interact with this] Core scenarios to cover: [list 4–6 key actions users need to take] Acceptance criteria style: [Given/When/Then / bullet list / free-form] For each user story: (1) "As a [user type], I want to [action] so that [benefit]", (2) Acceptance criteria (specific, testable — not vague), (3) Edge cases to consider, (4) Dependencies or blockers. Flag any stories that are too large and should be split. A story should be completable in one sprint.

3. Prioritization & Roadmap

Prompt 5 — Prioritization Framework

Help me prioritize this backlog. Items to evaluate: [list 8–12 features, bugs, or requests] Prioritization framework I want to use: [RICE / ICE / MoSCoW / Kano / Custom — or let me decide] My product's current focus: [growth / retention / monetization / reliability / expansion] Company stage: [early / growth / scale] Constraints: [team capacity, dependencies, upcoming releases] For each item: score it on the relevant dimensions → explain the rationale → flag any where I might be overestimating impact or underestimating effort. Produce a ranked list with a brief "why this order" summary. Also: what's the #1 item I should build next that would have the most impact — and why?

Prompt 6 — Roadmap Communication

Help me communicate our product roadmap. Audience: [engineering / executive / sales / customers / all hands] Roadmap horizon: [quarterly / 6-month / annual] Key themes or bets: [what our roadmap is organized around] What NOT to say: [things that would cause problems or create expectations we can't meet] Format needed: [slide deck / doc / email / live presentation] Write: (1) The framing statement that explains our strategic focus, (2) How to present uncertainty honestly without undermining confidence, (3) How to handle the question "when will X be done?", (4) The narrative thread that connects our choices to company goals, (5) What level of detail is appropriate for this audience. Include: what to show vs. what to say for each roadmap item.
Pro tip: A roadmap is a communication tool, not a commitment. Frame it as "bets we're making" not "promises we're keeping" — especially with uncertain timelines.

4. Stakeholder Management

Prompt 7 — Stakeholder Alignment Document

I need to align multiple stakeholders on a product decision. The decision: [what needs to be decided] Stakeholders involved: [list roles and their interests] Points of disagreement: [where people don't agree] My recommendation: [what I think we should do and why] The data or evidence I have: [research, metrics, customer feedback] Write a decision document: (1) Context (1 paragraph — why this decision matters), (2) Options considered with pros/cons table, (3) Recommendation with rationale, (4) Tradeoffs being accepted, (5) How to reverse this if it's wrong, (6) Approval needed from: [list]. The goal: a document that gets us aligned in one meeting, not three.

Prompt 8 — Difficult Stakeholder Conversation Prep

Help me prepare for a difficult conversation with a stakeholder. Situation: [what's happening — they want a feature I'm deprioritizing / they're unhappy with a decision / they're pushing for a scope change / timeline dispute] Their role and influence: [describe] Their likely concern: [what's driving their position — career, customers, metrics] My position: [what I'm asking for or defending] Data I have: [evidence that supports my position] Prepare: (1) How to open the conversation in a way that acknowledges their concern, (2) The key message I need to deliver clearly, (3) How to present the tradeoff without being dismissive, (4) 3 likely pushbacks and my responses, (5) What I'm willing to negotiate vs. what I need to hold. Goal: maintain the relationship and the decision.

5. Launch & Go-to-Market

Prompt 9 — Launch Plan Builder

Help me build a product launch plan for [feature / product]. What we're launching: [describe] Target users: [who gets this first — all users / segment / cohort / beta] Launch date: [target date] Teams involved: [engineering / design / marketing / sales / support / legal] Success metric at 30 days: [what "good" looks like] Risk: [what could go wrong] Launch plan: (1) Pre-launch checklist (by team, with owner and deadline), (2) Communication plan (internal announcement / customer email / in-product messaging / blog post / social), (3) Support readiness (what support needs to know before this launches), (4) Monitoring plan (what metrics to watch in the first 48 hours), (5) Rollback criteria (when would we reverse this and how). Make it a living doc teams can actually use.

Prompt 10 — Feature Announcement Email

Write an email to announce a new feature to our users. Feature: [name and what it does] Who's getting access: [all users / paid tier / beta users] The problem it solves: [what used to be frustrating, now fixed] How to find it: [where in the product] Launch date: [when it goes live] Brand tone: [friendly / professional / technical] Write: Subject line (+ 2 alternatives) + email body. Open with the problem solved (not "We're excited to announce") → Describe the feature in one clear paragraph → Show or explain how to get started → CTA (try it now / learn more). Under 250 words. Users are busy — respect their time and lead with value.
Pro tip: Feature announcement open rates are high — users care about what's new. Don't waste this attention on a feature description. Sell the outcome.

6. Analytics & Metrics

Prompt 11 — Success Metrics Framework

Help me define success metrics for [feature / initiative]. What we're building: [describe] Business goal this connects to: [revenue / retention / activation / acquisition / efficiency] User behavior we're trying to change: [what users should do differently] Current baseline (if known): [the metric today] Design a metrics framework: (1) North star metric for this feature, (2) Leading indicators (early signals that it's working), (3) Guardrail metrics (what shouldn't get worse), (4) How long until we have enough data to evaluate, (5) The decision we'll make at 30 / 90 days based on what we see. Also: what would cause us to kill or pivot this feature?

Prompt 12 — Weekly Product Review Template

Build a weekly product review template for my team. Team type: [B2B SaaS / B2C app / marketplace / platform / other] Stakeholders in the review: [engineering / design / leadership / all] Meeting length: [30 / 45 / 60 minutes] Current initiatives: [list 2–4 things in flight] Template sections: (1) Metrics snapshot (key numbers vs. last week and goal), (2) What shipped last week (and does it need follow-up?), (3) What's shipping this week (and is there risk?), (4) Key decision needed (bring one per week — specific, bounded), (5) What I learned from customers this week. This review should drive decisions, not report status.

7. Career & Growth

Prompt 13 — PM Interview Prep

Prepare me for a product manager interview at [company type: startup / big tech / growth-stage]. Role level: [APM / PM / Senior PM / Principal / Director] My experience: [years, product types, industries] Area I'm weakest in: [metrics / strategy / execution / influencing / technical depth] Prepare me for: (1) "Tell me about a product you worked on" — structure that shows impact, not just activity, (2) A product design question — framework I can apply to any problem, (3) A metrics question — how to approach "your key metric dropped 20%, what do you do?", (4) A behavioral question — "tell me about a time you influenced without authority", (5) Questions I should ask that signal I'm thinking at the next level. Give me complete sample answers I can adapt.
Pro tip: The best PM interview answers follow STAR but go one step further — they end with "and what I'd do differently now is X." That metacognition is what separates good candidates from great ones.

Prompt 14 — PM Portfolio / Case Study

Help me write a PM portfolio case study for [product / feature / initiative]. My role: [what I specifically owned] The problem: [what we were solving and why it mattered] My process: [discovery / hypothesis / prioritization / execution / learning] Key decision I made: [a choice that defined the outcome] The result: [what happened — metrics, qualitative outcomes] What I'd do differently: [honest reflection] Write: (1) Context (75 words), (2) Problem and why it mattered (150 words), (3) My approach — focused on decisions, not process steps (250 words), (4) Outcome with specific metrics (100 words), (5) Reflection (75 words). Total: 650 words. Avoid "we did X, then Y, then Z." Focus on the judgment calls that reveal how you think.

Prompt 15 — Annual PM Self-Review

Help me write and analyze my annual PM self-review. This year's work: [features shipped, initiatives led, metrics moved] My biggest win: [with the why — what decision or approach made it work] My biggest miss: [what went wrong and what I learned] Feedback I received: [from peers, leadership, cross-functional partners] Skills I built: [what I got better at] Skills I avoided: [what I need to be honest about] Career goal: [where I want to be in 1–3 years] Analyze: (1) Where did I create the most leverage? (2) What's my actual superpower as a PM? (3) What gap is most likely to limit my growth? (4) Three specific things to do in Q1 to close that gap, (5) The honest assessment of whether I'm on track for my career goal.
