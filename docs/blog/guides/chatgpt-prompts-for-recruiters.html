ChatGPT Prompts for Recruiters

15 free prompts to write better job descriptions, source more effectively, and create a candidate experience that converts top talent.

Recruiting is a writing-heavy job — job descriptions, outreach messages, interview feedback, rejection notes, hiring manager updates, and employer brand content all compete for your time. These 15 prompts handle the writing so you can focus on the relationships and judgment calls that actually fill roles.

1. Job Descriptions

Prompt 1 — Job Description Rewrite

Rewrite this job description to attract stronger candidates and reduce drop-off. Original JD: [paste the existing job description] Role level: [IC / manager / director / VP / executive] Key skills required (non-negotiable): [list 3–5] Skills that are nice to have but not required: [list 3–5] What this role actually does day-to-day: [describe in plain language — not the corporate version] What makes this role or company compelling: [the real reason a great candidate should join] Avoid: [corporate jargon / "rockstar" / "ninja" / passive voice / requirements lists that screen out qualified women / inflated years-of-experience requirements] Rewrite with: Hook opening (why this role is interesting — not "we are looking for") → What you will do (active, specific) → What you need (only true must-haves) → What we offer (real benefits, not "fast-paced environment") → Company description (2 sentences, not boilerplate).
Pro tip: Every extra requirement in a job description costs you candidates — especially women, who apply only when they meet 100% of criteria. Cut everything that is not truly required.

Prompt 2 — Inclusive Job Description Audit

Audit my job description for language that may deter underrepresented candidates. Job description: [paste] Review for: (1) Gendered language (words associated with masculine or feminine traits — research shows this affects who applies), (2) Unnecessarily exclusive experience requirements (years-of-experience proxies for skills / credentials that screen out non-traditional backgrounds), (3) Cultural fit language that can mask bias, (4) Jargon that favors insiders over strong external candidates, (5) Benefits and perks that signal who is welcome (and who is not). Output: (1) Flag specific phrases with explanation of why they may exclude, (2) Suggested rewrites for each flagged phrase, (3) An overall assessment: is this JD likely to attract a diverse candidate pool? What is the single most important change to make?

2. Sourcing & Outreach

Prompt 3 — LinkedIn Outreach Message

Write a LinkedIn outreach message for passive candidate sourcing. Role I am hiring for: [title and 2-sentence description of what makes it compelling] Candidate I am reaching out to: [their current title, company, and 1–2 specific things about their profile that caught my eye] What makes this opportunity relevant to them: [why would they specifically care about this role] Tone: [conversational and genuine — not a template blast] Length: [LinkedIn InMail limit — under 300 characters for connection request / under 2000 for InMail] Write: (1) Connection request message (under 300 characters — no "I'm a recruiter with an amazing opportunity"), (2) InMail version (under 200 words — lead with something specific about them, then the opportunity, then an easy out), (3) Follow-up after no response (1 week — shorter, different angle, still not pushy). The goal is a conversation, not an application.

Prompt 4 — Boolean Search String Builder

Help me build a Boolean search string for sourcing candidates on LinkedIn. Role: [job title] Must-have skills or keywords: [list 5–8] Nice-to-have skills: [list 3–5] Target companies (if applicable): [list competitors or target employers] Exclude: [staffing agencies / certain titles / certain companies — explain why] Location: [remote / specific cities or regions] Build: (1) Primary Boolean string (combines must-haves with OR for title variations), (2) Alternative string (if the primary is returning too few results — broaden the skills), (3) Company-focused string (for poaching from specific competitors), (4) Brief explanation of how to modify each string for different platforms (LinkedIn / GitHub / portfolio sites).
Pro tip: Build title OR strings first — the same person may be listed as "Software Engineer," "SWE," or "Full Stack Developer." You miss them if you search for only one.

Prompt 5 — Sourcing Strategy for Hard-to-Fill Role

Build a sourcing strategy for a role that is hard to fill through standard channels. Role: [title and description] Why it is hard to fill: [niche skill set / low supply / competitive market / location / low brand recognition / compensation constraints] What we have already tried: [job boards / LinkedIn / employee referrals / etc.] What I can and cannot do: [budget / tools available / time to fill deadline] Strategy: (1) Non-obvious sourcing channels for this specific role (beyond LinkedIn), (2) Communities, meetups, conferences, or publications where these candidates gather, (3) Referral program optimization — how to get more referrals for this specific role, (4) Employer brand content that would attract passive candidates, (5) How to build a talent community for this role type for future openings. Specific and actionable — not generic job board advice.

3. Candidate Experience

Prompt 6 — Interview Process Communication Sequence

Write a candidate communication sequence for my interview process. Interview stages: [list your stages — e.g., phone screen / hiring manager call / technical assessment / panel interview / final round / offer] Timeline between stages: [typical time] What candidates most often complain about: [not knowing where they stand / too many rounds / unclear expectations / ghosting] Write email templates for each stage: (1) Scheduling confirmation (what to expect, how to prepare, who they will meet), (2) Between stages — status update (even if nothing has changed — silence is the enemy), (3) Moving forward to next stage (enthusiasm, specific timeline), (4) Post-final round — decision still pending (realistic timeline, not "we will be in touch"), (5) Rejection after first screen, (6) Rejection after final round (they invested significant time — they deserve more than a form email).

Prompt 7 — Rejection Message That Does Not Burn Bridges

Write rejection messages that treat candidates with respect and leave the door open. Stage: [after resume review / after phone screen / after technical / after final round] Reason for rejection (if I can share it): [not a fit for this role / another candidate was a stronger match for this specific need / role was filled internally / [specific skill gap]] My goal: [leave them with a positive impression / keep them warm for future roles / get them to refer others] Write: (1) Standard rejection (respectful, specific enough to feel human, no false promises of "we will keep your resume on file"), (2) Version for strong candidates I want to keep warm ("not the right fit for this role, but I would love to stay connected for X type of role in the future"), (3) Version for a finalist who made it to the final round (they earned more — acknowledge it). No form letters that start with "After careful consideration..."
Pro tip: Rejected finalists talk to other candidates. A respectful, specific rejection email is worth more than any employer brand campaign — treat it as marketing.

4. Hiring Manager Partnership

Prompt 8 — Hiring Manager Kickoff Meeting Agenda

Build a hiring manager kickoff meeting agenda for a new role opening. Role: [title and level] Hiring manager: [their style — hands-off / micromanager / first time hiring / experienced / unclear on what they need] What I need from this meeting: [clear intake / aligned on must-haves / realistic salary expectations / agreed-on process and timeline] Common problems with this HM (if any): [list — e.g., keeps changing requirements / unrealistic timeline / unclear on compensation range] Meeting agenda: (1) Role brief — what problem does this hire solve (make them articulate it, not just restate the title), (2) Ideal candidate — must-have vs. nice-to-have (force prioritization), (3) Realistic market briefing — what the candidate market looks like for this role, (4) Interview process design, (5) Timeline and SLAs (what I commit to / what I need from them), (6) How we will communicate on candidates. Also write: the 5 questions I should ask even if they are uncomfortable.

Prompt 9 — Candidate Presentation to Hiring Manager

Write a candidate presentation summary for the hiring manager. Candidate: [general profile — title, years of experience, key skills — no PII beyond role context] Role they are being considered for: [title] Why I am excited about them: [specific to the role requirements] Potential concerns or gaps: [honest — do not hide them] What I need from the hiring manager: [a decision / specific feedback / interview scheduling] Write: (1) Candidate summary (150 words — lead with the hook, support with specifics, flag concerns honestly), (2) "Why I am recommending this candidate" in 3 bullets, (3) Questions I want the HM to probe in the interview (based on the gaps I see), (4) Recommended next step. Hiring managers who trust your judgment move faster — that trust comes from honest, specific presentations.

5. Employer Brand

Prompt 10 — LinkedIn Company Page Content

Write LinkedIn content for employer brand and talent attraction. Company: [name / industry / size / stage — startup / scaleup / enterprise] What makes this company a great place to work: [be specific — not "great culture" — specific to real programs, team dynamics, mission] Target candidates: [who I want to attract] What candidates in this space care about: [growth / flexibility / mission / comp / tech stack / work-life balance — be honest about where you shine] 10 LinkedIn post ideas: For each — format, topic, hook sentence, and key points. Content mix: day-in-the-life posts → team spotlights → company mission stories → transparency about how hiring works → behind-the-scenes culture moments → specific employee achievements → "what we look for" educational content. Authentic, not corporate marketing copy.

Prompt 11 — Glassdoor Review Response

Write responses to Glassdoor reviews — positive and critical. Write responses for: (1) 5-star enthusiastic review — genuine acknowledgment, specific to what they mentioned, (2) 4-star review with specific praise and one concern — acknowledge the concern, describe what is being done (only if true), (3) 3-star review about culture or management — empathetic, honest, not defensive, invites offline conversation, (4) 2-star review with a specific complaint about the hiring process — take accountability if warranted, (5) 1-star review that appears to be from a disgruntled candidate (not employee) — professional, factual, brief. Principle for all responses: Candidates and employees read your responses more carefully than the reviews. Never get defensive. The response is your public interview.
Pro tip: The best Glassdoor responses are short, specific, and acknowledge imperfection without being defensive. Candidates trust a company that can say "you are right, we are working on this" more than one that argues with every negative review.

6. Screening & Interviews

Prompt 12 — Phone Screen Question Guide

Build a structured phone screen guide for [role]. Role: [title and key responsibilities] Must-have signals to identify in a 30-minute screen: [list 5 — what you need to know to advance or pass] Nice-to-have signals: [list 3] Common reasons candidates fail at the next stage: [be specific — what does the HM reject people for] Create: (1) Opening (how to set expectations for the call and put the candidate at ease), (2) 8 screen questions with what a strong answer sounds like and what a weak answer sounds like, (3) How to probe deeper when an answer is vague, (4) Candidate questions for me — what strong candidates typically ask (and what it signals if they do not ask anything), (5) How to close the call regardless of outcome. Structured screens reduce bias and improve prediction.

Prompt 13 — Interview Debrief Facilitation

Help me facilitate a structured interview debrief with a hiring team. Role: [title] Interviewers: [list their roles — not names] Common debrief problems: [interviewers share impressions not evidence / first impression bias / loudest person dominates / no structured framework / everyone waited to see what the HM thinks] Build: (1) Debrief agenda (20–45 minutes depending on finalists), (2) Framework for structured feedback — "hire / no hire / more data needed" with evidence-based reasoning (not "I just didn't click with them"), (3) How to solicit independent opinions before group discussion (reduce anchoring), (4) How to handle disagreement between interviewers, (5) Decision criteria — how to break ties. Also write: the debrief questions I should ask to pull out evidence, not just impressions.

7. Productivity & Process

Prompt 14 — Recruiter Weekly Planning Template

Build a weekly planning template for a recruiter managing multiple open roles. Roles I am currently working: [list 3–8 by title and stage] My biggest time wasters: [reactive scheduling / re-explaining the same process / chasing HM feedback / redundant status updates] My goal for the week: [specific — e.g., advance 3 finalists / open 2 new roles / reduce time-to-feedback] Template: (1) Monday focus block — what to prioritize first, (2) Role-by-role status snapshot (where each role is, what I need to move it forward), (3) Stakeholder touchpoints to schedule this week, (4) Candidate touchpoints in flight (who I owe a response to), (5) Metrics to track weekly (time-to-fill / candidate pipeline stage / offer acceptance rate). A recruiter who works a plan moves faster than one who reacts to their inbox.

Prompt 15 — Annual Recruiting Retrospective

Help me run an annual recruiting retrospective. Roles filled this year: [number] | Time-to-fill average: [days] | Offer acceptance rate: [%] Top referral source: [job board / LinkedIn / referrals / agencies] Hardest roles to fill: [list 3 — and why] Biggest win: [describe] Where the process broke down most often: [sourcing / screening / HM alignment / offer stage / candidate drops] Goal for next year: [specific] Retrospective analysis: (1) What worked — which sourcing channels, processes, or partnerships delivered best results, (2) Where we lose candidates — and at what stage, (3) Hiring manager relationships that accelerated or slowed down fills, (4) Employer brand gaps that cost us candidates, (5) The honest answer: what one change would have the biggest impact on next year's results?
