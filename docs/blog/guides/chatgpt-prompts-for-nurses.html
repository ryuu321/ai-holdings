15 ChatGPT Prompts for Nurses

You already do the hardest work. Let AI handle the writing, studying, career planning, and communication tasks so you have energy left for what matters most.

Nurses are among the most educated, overworked, and underserved professionals when it comes to tools that actually help. These prompts are designed for the real demands of nursing life: clinical writing, career advancement, patient education, managing burnout, and building income beyond the bedside.

These work for RNs, LPNs, NPs, nurse educators, charge nurses, travel nurses, and nursing students. Never use AI for actual clinical decisions or patient care. These prompts are for professional communication, career development, and personal productivity. Use in ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini.

⚠️ Important: Never input patient information or PHI into AI tools. These prompts are for professional development and communication only — not clinical decision support. Always follow your facility's policies on AI use.

Patient Education & Communication

1. The Patient Education Sheet

Patients who understand their condition, medications, and discharge instructions have better outcomes. AI helps you create clear materials faster.

Write a patient education sheet for [condition or procedure: diabetes management / post-surgical wound care / hypertension medication / COPD management / heart failure diet / etc.]. Reading level: [6th grade — accessible for most patients]. What patients need to understand: [key concepts, what to watch for, when to call the doctor, what to do and not do]. Format: 1) What is [condition/procedure] — one plain-language paragraph, 2) What you need to do — 5-7 numbered steps, 3) Warning signs to watch for — bullet list, 4) When to call us — specific, not just "if you feel worse", 5) Resources for more information — type of resources (websites, apps — without specific URLs that may change). Write at a 6th-grade reading level. No medical jargon without immediate plain translation. Also write a 3-sentence verbal summary for the bedside handoff.

2. The Medication Explanation Template

Write a patient explanation for [medication: metformin / lisinopril / atorvastatin / metoprolol / warfarin / insulin / etc.]. What patients most commonly misunderstand about this medication: [list common misconceptions or fears]. Key teaching points: [what they need to know to take it correctly and safely]. Write an explanation that: 1) Explains what this medication does in one plain-language sentence, 2) Covers how and when to take it — specific and clear, 3) Explains the most common side effects and what to do about each, 4) Addresses what to do if they miss a dose (general framework — remind them to check with their pharmacist for specifics), 5) Lists red-flag symptoms that mean call 911 or go to the ER. Under 200 words. Also write a short version for a patient who has limited time or literacy barriers.

3. The Family Meeting Preparation Guide

Help me prepare for a family meeting about [situation: end-of-life care discussion / transition to long-term care / prognosis communication / care plan goals / discharge planning]. What the family knows so far: [describe]. What needs to be communicated: [describe the key information]. Common concerns families raise in this situation: [list]. Prepare: 1) An opening statement that sets a compassionate, honest tone, 2) Key points to communicate — in plain language, organized logically, 3) The top 3 questions families ask in this situation and how to answer them honestly, 4) How to handle a family member who is in denial or resistant, 5) What to document after the meeting. Also: what resources to have ready — chaplain, social work, palliative care. This is a preparation guide only — actual conversations require clinical judgment and team collaboration.

Career Development

4. The Nursing Resume & Cover Letter

Write a nursing resume summary and cover letter for [target position: ICU / ED / home health / nurse educator / charge nurse / travel nurse / NP / leadership role]. My background: [years of experience, specialties, certifications, key accomplishments]. What I'm applying for: [describe the role and what you know about the employer]. Write: 1) A 3-4 sentence resume summary that leads with my specialty and impact — not "motivated nursing professional", 2) A cover letter that: opens with why this specific role and employer appeal to me, highlights 2-3 specific accomplishments with numbers where possible, ends with a confident CTA. Under 300 words for the letter. Also: what to emphasize in a nursing resume that most nurses undersell? (certifications, charge nurse hours, preceptor experience, quality improvement projects)

5. The Nursing Interview Prep

Help me prepare for a nursing interview for [type of unit/role]. Role: [describe]. Write: 1) 8 behavioral interview questions I'm likely to be asked — STAR format (tell me about a time when...), 2) What a strong answer looks like for each — the elements to include without scripting it word-for-word, 3) 2 clinical scenario questions specific to this unit and how to approach them, 4) 5 thoughtful questions I should ask the interviewer (shows I researched the unit and care about the culture), 5) How to answer "what's your biggest weakness?" — in a way that's honest but strategic. Also: what nursing interviewers actually look for that candidates don't realize (beyond clinical skills)?

6. The NCLEX & Certification Exam Study Plan

Build a study plan for [exam: NCLEX-RN / NCLEX-PN / CCRN / CEN / CNOR / NP board exam / etc.]. My situation: [exam date, hours available per week for studying, areas I know are weak, what I've already tried]. Build a plan with: 1) Weekly hour allocation — what to study each week leading up to the exam, 2) The priority topics for this exam — what's highest yield based on how the test is weighted, 3) Active study strategies that work better than re-reading notes, 4) How to use practice questions effectively — not just doing them but actually learning from wrong answers, 5) The week before the exam: what to do and what NOT to do. Also: the top 3 reasons nurses fail this exam on their first attempt (and how to avoid each).

Professional Writing

7. The SBAR Communication Template

Help me write a clear SBAR communication for a clinical situation. Situation: [describe what's happening with the patient — use HYPOTHETICAL details only, never real patient info]. Write a model SBAR structure with: Situation: [1-2 sentences — who, what, what's happening now], Background: [2-3 sentences — relevant history, medications, recent events], Assessment: [1-2 sentences — your clinical impression of what's going on], Recommendation: [what you're asking for — specific ask to the provider]. Also write: a version for a non-urgent situation (scheduled callback), a version for an urgent situation (this needs to happen now), and 2-3 phrases to use if the provider seems dismissive. Reminder: practice this with hypothetical scenarios — never input real patient data.

8. The Policy or Procedure Summary

Summarize this nursing policy or procedure in plain language for staff training. Policy/procedure to summarize: [paste or describe the key points — e.g., fall prevention protocol / pressure injury staging / blood transfusion verification / central line care]. The problem: the official policy is too long and detailed for staff to actually remember at the bedside. Create: 1) A one-page quick reference card with only what's essential at the bedside, 2) The 3 most critical compliance points that staff most often miss, 3) A 5-question quiz to verify staff understanding, 4) A 2-minute verbal overview for new staff orientation. Format the quick reference for scanning (bullet points, bold critical steps, visual hierarchy). This is a training aid — the official policy is still authoritative.

9. The Incident Report & Documentation Guide

Help me write a factual, professional incident report for [type of incident: patient fall / medication error / near-miss / equipment malfunction / patient complaint]. Principles for documentation: 1) What factual, objective language looks like vs. language that implies fault, 2) What to include vs. what to leave out of an incident report, 3) How to describe observations vs. interpretations, 4) What the documentation chain should look like — who to notify and in what order, 5) How to document in the medical record after an incident (separate from the incident report — what goes where). Write a model template for this type of incident using generic/hypothetical details. Note: follow your facility's specific incident reporting system and consult your risk management or charge nurse for actual incidents.

Nursing Side Hustles & Income

10. The Nurse Entrepreneur Business Plan

Help me plan a nursing-based side business. Business idea: [legal nurse consulting / nurse coaching / health writing / CPR/first aid instruction / telehealth / nursing tutoring / medical aesthetics / wellness coaching / healthcare staffing / other]. My background: [specialty, years of experience, certifications]. Time available: [X hours/week]. Investment budget: [$X]. Build a plan with: 1) What license, certification, or legal structure I may need before starting, 2) How to get my first client in 30 days — specific, actionable steps, 3) What to charge — how nurses in this space typically price their services, 4) How to market without needing a large following, 5) The biggest mistake nurses make when starting this type of business. Also: what aspects of my nursing background are most valuable to this business that I might be underselling?

11. The Legal Nurse Consulting Introduction

Help me position myself as a legal nurse consultant and reach my first attorney client. My background: [specialty, years of experience, certifications]. What LNCs do that I can offer: [case screening / medical record review / expert witness preparation / standard of care research]. Write: 1) A one-page introduction letter to personal injury or medical malpractice attorneys — what I offer and why it's valuable to them, 2) My LNC bio for a website or LinkedIn (positions me credibly for attorneys), 3) What to say when an attorney asks "what's your hourly rate?" — how to price LNC services, 4) The elevator pitch for a networking event with attorneys, 5) How to get on plaintiff attorney referral lists — the practical steps. Note: consult AALNC (American Association of Legal Nurse Consultants) for professional guidelines before starting.

12. The Nursing Blog or Content Strategy

Build a content strategy for a nursing professional who wants to build an audience or income through writing/content. My focus: [nursing career advice / specialty-specific content / patient advocacy / nurse wellness / NCLEX prep / travel nursing / etc.]. Platforms: [Instagram / TikTok / YouTube / blog / newsletter / LinkedIn]. Goal: [build an audience / create a side income / establish expertise / attract job opportunities]. Build a plan with: 1) My unique angle — what do I know that other nurses or patients need?, 2) 10 content ideas for this month that would resonate with my target audience, 3) How to handle HIPAA concerns when creating nursing content (general framework), 4) Monetization options at different audience sizes — when can I start earning?, 5) The one platform to start with given my content style and goal. Be realistic — this takes 6-12 months to build meaningful income.

Wellbeing & Career Longevity

13. The Burnout Recovery Plan

Help me assess my nursing burnout and build a recovery plan. My situation: [describe — years in nursing, current unit, what's feeling unsustainable, what I've already tried]. What I still love about nursing: [describe — what keeps you]. What I'd change if I could: [describe]. Build a plan with: 1) An honest assessment of whether I'm burned out vs. in the wrong specialty vs. in the wrong organization, 2) 3 changes I can make in the next 30 days that don't require quitting (schedule, unit transfer request, boundary changes, etc.), 3) What a sustainable nursing career looks like — what does the evidence say about longevity in this profession?, 4) How to talk to my manager about unsustainable conditions — word-for-word approach, 5) A realistic evaluation: is this unit salvageable, or do I need to move? Give me an honest answer, not just encouragement.

14. The Specialty Transition Plan

Help me plan a transition to [new nursing specialty or role: ICU / ED / OR / home health / case management / informatics / leadership / travel nursing / NP program]. My current background: [specialty, years of experience]. What attracts me to this new role: [describe]. Concerns or gaps: [what I'm worried about — skills gap, age, schedule, competition]. Build a plan with: 1) What skills and experience from my background transfer better than I realize, 2) What I need to develop or obtain before making the move (certifications, shadow shifts, network contacts), 3) How to position my resume for this transition — what to emphasize, 4) The realistic timeline — how long does this transition typically take?, 5) Whether to apply externally or transfer internally — and the pros/cons of each path. Be specific about this specialty — not generic career advice.

15. The Nurse's Personal Finance Plan

Help me build a personal finance plan as a nurse. My situation: Total income: $[X/year]. Student loan debt: $[X]. Other debt: $[X]. Savings: $[X]. Goals: [pay off loans / buy a house / retire early / build an emergency fund / start a side business]. Special nursing context: [shift differentials / travel nursing income variability / public service loan forgiveness eligibility / overtime income]. Build a plan with: 1) Whether I might qualify for PSLF or other nurse loan forgiveness programs — what to check, 2) How to build an emergency fund on a nursing schedule when income varies by shifts, 3) The highest-priority financial move given my situation — what to do first, 4) Whether travel nursing would meaningfully accelerate my financial goals, 5) The one financial habit nurses most commonly neglect that I should start now. Give me direct recommendations, not a list of options to "consider."
Pro tip: Start with Prompt 1 (Patient Education Sheet) — it has the most immediate professional impact. Better patient education materials mean better patient outcomes, fewer repeat questions during a shift, and documentation that protects you. If you create reusable education sheets for your most common patient populations, you'll save hours every week and your colleagues will thank you.
