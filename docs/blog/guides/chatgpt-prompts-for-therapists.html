15 ChatGPT Prompts for Therapists & Counselors

The therapeutic work is irreplaceable. But the emails, intake paperwork, marketing, and documentation? AI can help you handle those faster — so you can save your energy for the sessions that matter.

Private practice therapists and counselors carry a double burden: delivering emotionally demanding clinical work, and running a small business entirely on their own. These prompts address the business side — so you can spend more energy on the clinical side.

These work for licensed therapists (LCSW, LPC, MFT, psychologists, counselors), group practice owners, and mental health professionals in private practice. None of these prompts are for clinical use. Always review AI content before using it professionally. Use in ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini.

⚠️ Critical: Never input client information, case details, or any PHI into AI tools. These prompts are for practice administration and marketing only — not for clinical documentation, treatment planning, or anything involving actual clients. Consult your licensing board's guidance on AI use in mental health practice.

Practice Communication & Intake

1. The Initial Inquiry Response

How you respond to the first contact shapes whether someone in distress takes the next step toward help.

Write a response template for initial therapy inquiries. My practice: [specialty: anxiety / depression / trauma / relationships / LGBTQ+ / ADHD / grief / eating disorders / other]. Modality: [individual / couples / family / group]. Format: [in-person / telehealth / hybrid]. Availability: [describe — accepting new clients / waitlist / limited openings]. Write a response that: 1) Acknowledges what they reached out about without making assumptions (they may not have shared details), 2) Lets them know what happens next — what the consultation process looks like, 3) Sets expectations about timeline and availability honestly, 4) Is warm without being performatively effusive, 5) Includes one question that helps me understand what they're looking for. Under 200 words. The tone should feel like it comes from a real human being, not a practice management system. Also write a waitlist message if you're full.

2. The Intake Paperwork Explanation

Write a plain-language explanation of intake paperwork to send to new clients before their first session. What I send: [informed consent / HIPAA notice / intake questionnaire / cancellation policy / fee agreement]. The problem: clients don't read it, then are confused or surprised. Write an email that: 1) Explains why they're receiving this paperwork in a way that doesn't feel bureaucratic, 2) Highlights the 3-4 most important things to know before signing, 3) Specifically explains the cancellation policy — the most common source of conflict, 4) Addresses the HIPAA notice in one plain sentence (not a legal recitation), 5) Tells them what to do if they have questions before the first session. Under 250 words. This email should reduce first-session anxiety, not add to it.

3. The Cancellation & No-Show Policy Communication

Write communications around cancellation and no-show policies for my therapy practice. My policy: [describe — 24/48-hour notice required / fee charged for late cancellations / how many no-shows before discharge / exceptions]. Write: 1) The policy explanation in my informed consent document — clear and complete but not threatening, 2) The first cancellation fee communication (for a client who cancels late for the first time — firm, educational, not punitive), 3) The second late cancellation message (firmer, references the agreement they signed), 4) How to handle a client who says they can't afford the cancellation fee — the conversation that protects the policy while preserving the relationship, 5) The clinical decision framework: when does a pattern of cancellations warrant a clinical conversation vs. just a policy reminder? Note: always ensure your policy is clearly explained in your informed consent.

Practice Marketing

4. The Psychology Today Profile Rewrite

Rewrite my Psychology Today profile to attract the clients I work best with. Current profile (paste or describe): [describe what you currently have or what you'd like to say]. My ideal client: [describe who you help best — not every presenting problem, but the specific person]. My approach: [describe your modality and style in plain language — not "evidence-based" but what that feels like in a session]. What makes me different: [specific things — not "compassionate" but actual differentiators]. Rewrite the profile with: 1) An opening that speaks directly to the person I help (not "I am a licensed therapist..."), 2) A description of what working with me is like — what they'll actually experience, 3) My specialties in plain language — what problems, populations, and life situations I work with best, 4) One sentence about my approach that differentiates me, 5) A clear invitation to reach out. Under 400 words. No jargon, no credentials-first, no laundry list of conditions treated.

5. The Specialty Page Website Copy

Write a specialty page for my therapy website. Specialty: [anxiety / depression / trauma / relationship issues / ADHD / grief / life transitions / eating disorders / OCD / other]. What people searching for this feel: [describe their experience — what does living with this feel like? what have they tried? what are they afraid of?]. My approach to this specialty: [describe how you actually work with this — not clinical theory but what clients notice]. Write a page with: 1) An opening that makes someone recognize themselves — they should feel "this therapist understands this", 2) A description of common experiences without clinical labeling (not "you may have GAD" but the felt experience), 3) How therapy for this specialty actually works — what changes, what to expect, 4) A brief description of my approach specific to this issue, 5) A CTA that makes the next step feel manageable. Under 500 words. No clinical jargon without immediate plain-language translation.

6. The Referral Source Outreach

Write outreach to build referral relationships with [referral source: primary care physicians / psychiatrists / schools / pediatricians / OBGYNs / employee assistance programs / HR departments / community organizations]. My specialty: [describe]. What I offer referral sources: [describe — easy referral process, prompt communication, coordination, specific expertise]. Write: 1) An introduction email that establishes my specialty and how I can serve their clients/patients — specific, not generic, 2) A one-page referral guide I can send: who I work with, what I treat, how to refer, what happens next, 3) A follow-up email for when a referral hasn't come in after 30 days — keeps the relationship warm without being pushy, 4) How to communicate progress back to referral sources (with appropriate consent) in a way that strengthens the relationship. Under 150 words for the intro email.

Business Operations

7. The Fee & Insurance Communication

Write fee and insurance communication for my practice. My fees: [$X per session / sliding scale from $X-$Y / insurance accepted: [list] / superbill available]. The problem: fee conversations are awkward, clients are confused about insurance, and I lose potential clients over unclear communication. Write: 1) The fee section of my website — clear, complete, no burying the price, 2) How to present my fee on the phone when someone asks (word-for-word), 3) The explanation of what a superbill is and how to submit it (for clients with out-of-network benefits), 4) How to present a sliding scale without it feeling arbitrary or charity-like, 5) How to respond when someone says "your fee is too high" — affirm the concern without immediately discounting. Also: the one thing most therapists get wrong in fee conversations.

8. The Group Practice Operations Templates

Write operational communication templates for a group therapy practice. Templates needed: 1) The intro email for a new group practice therapist joining the team — what they need to know about culture, expectations, and administrative responsibilities, 2) Peer consultation meeting agenda template — keeps discussions productive and ethical, 3) Client transition email when a therapist leaves the practice — how to communicate this sensitively, 4) New referral distribution process — how to describe to referrers which therapist to match with which client type, 5) End-of-year check-in template to send to therapists in the practice — what's working, what needs adjustment. Each under 200 words. These are the communications that keep a group practice running without constant hand-holding.

9. The Self-Care & Preventing Burnout Plan

Help me build a sustainable private practice structure that prevents therapist burnout. My current situation: [number of client hours/week, admin hours, no-shows, clinical challenges you're dealing with]. What sustainability looks like for me: [describe — financial security, clinical variety, personal energy, time off]. Build a practice audit with: 1) My ideal caseload: what number and type of clients would be energizing rather than depleting, 2) What to change about my scheduling that would make Fridays feel different from Mondays, 3) The one type of case I should stop taking (not because I can't help, but because the cost to me is too high), 4) How to structure my week so admin doesn't bleed into clinical recovery time, 5) A check-in question to ask myself quarterly to notice burnout before it's a crisis. This isn't about productivity — it's about longevity in a field I love.

Content & Education

10. The Mental Health Blog Post Plan

Create a 6-month blog content plan to attract ideal therapy clients through SEO. My specialty: [describe]. My location: [city or telehealth/nationwide]. Target: [someone searching for help with their specific issue — not mental health professionals]. Build a plan with: 1) 12 blog topics targeting search terms my ideal clients use (the language they use, not clinical language), 2) For each post: the search intent (what are they really looking for?) and what the post should help them do, 3) Which post to publish first for fastest search traction, 4) 2 posts that are specifically shareable by referral sources (GPs, schools, HR), 5) How to end each post in a way that makes reaching out feel like a natural next step — not a forced CTA. Note: avoid anything that could be construed as providing therapy or diagnosis in a blog format.

11. The Social Media Content Calendar

Create a monthly social media content plan for my therapy practice. Platforms: [Instagram / LinkedIn / Facebook / TikTok / Pinterest]. Goal: [attract new clients / build community / establish thought leadership / all of above]. My comfort with personal sharing: [low / medium / high — adjust accordingly]. Create 20 post ideas for the month that: 1) Psychoeducational posts: what clients need to know about [anxiety / depression / relationships / ADHD / etc.] in plain, accessible language, 2) "What therapy is like" posts: demystifying the process for people who've never tried it, 3) Myth-busting posts: common misconceptions about therapy or mental health, 4) "Signs you might benefit from therapy" posts — without fear-mongering, 5) Reachable CTA posts: what to do if they're ready to take the next step. For each: the hook (first line) and the call to action. Avoid: content that could be construed as clinical advice.

12. The Workshop or Group Program Description

Write a description for a [workshop / group therapy / skills group / online course] I'm launching. Program: [describe — topic, format, length, who it's for, what they'll learn or experience]. What participants typically struggle with: [describe their situation before the program]. What they'll be able to do or feel after: [describe the transformation]. Write: 1) A program description for my website (under 300 words) that makes the right people feel "this is for me", 2) An email to my existing clients and contacts announcing it, 3) A social media post announcing it (under 150 words, shareable), 4) An FAQ addressing: who it's for / is it right for me / how is this different from individual therapy / what's the commitment. Avoid overclaiming outcomes — use "you'll explore" and "you'll practice" language rather than guaranteed results.

Professional Development

13. The Consultation Note & Case Conceptualization Aid

Help me prepare for peer consultation or supervision. What I want to discuss: [describe the clinical challenge — a stuck case, an ethical dilemma, a countertransference issue, a termination concern]. What I've already tried or observed: [describe]. What I'm hoping to get from consultation: [a new framework / different perspective / permission to feel what I'm feeling / guidance on next steps]. Help me: 1) Organize my thoughts before the consultation meeting — what's the core of what I want to bring?, 2) Identify what I might be missing or avoiding in my thinking about this case, 3) Generate 3 questions to bring to consultation, 4) Reflect on what this case might be activating in me personally. Note: use only hypothetical or de-identified information. This is a thinking aid — not clinical advice.

14. The CE and Continuing Education Plan

Help me plan my continuing education for the next year. My license: [LCSW / LPC / MFT / psychologist / etc.]. CE requirement: [X hours by date]. My clinical interests: [what I want to learn or expand in], Current gaps: [what I know I need more training in], Budget: [$X for CE this year]. Build a plan with: 1) The highest-priority CE areas given my current caseload and goals, 2) How to get CE credits efficiently (live workshops vs. online vs. publications), 3) 3 specific training programs or workshops to look at this year (give me search terms or categories, not specific links), 4) How to turn the CE I take into content for my practice — blog posts, Instagram posts, or informed conversations with clients, 5) One training that would most expand my ability to help clients I currently struggle to serve.

15. The Private Practice Financial Review

Help me review the finances and sustainability of my private practice. Data: Sessions per week: [X]. Average fee: $[X]. No-show rate: [X%]. Time spent on admin/marketing per week: [X hours]. Annual revenue: $[X]. What I want to change: [earn more / work fewer hours / reduce insurance dependency / raise fees / build a group practice]. Run a review that: 1) Calculates my effective hourly rate (revenue ÷ total hours including admin), 2) Identifies the fee adjustment that would have the biggest impact without pricing me out of my market, 3) Calculates what eliminating one no-show per week would be worth annually, 4) Evaluates whether transitioning from insurance to private pay makes sense — what's the actual math?, 5) Tells me the single most financially impactful change I could make in the next 90 days. Give me the actual numbers where possible, not just advice.
Pro tip: Start with Prompt 4 (Psychology Today Profile Rewrite) — it's the fastest path to attracting more of the clients you want. Most therapist profiles lead with credentials when potential clients care most about whether you understand them. A profile that describes their experience before listing your degrees converts significantly better.
