ChatGPT Prompts for Athletic Coaches

15 free prompts to build better practice plans, communicate with athletes and parents, and develop your program year-round.

Coaching is about relationships and decisions under pressure — but a huge chunk of a coach's time disappears into practice planning, parent communication, scouting, and administrative work. These 15 prompts help you handle the off-field work faster so you can focus on what actually wins games: developing athletes.

1. Practice Planning

Prompt 1 — Weekly Practice Plan

Help me build a weekly practice plan for my team. Sport: [your sport] Level: [youth rec / high school JV / high school varsity / college / adult amateur / elite club] Phase of season: [preseason / early season / mid-season / playoff prep / off-season] Key focus areas this week: [e.g., transition defense, set piece execution, pitching mechanics, shooting under pressure] Team weaknesses identified from last game/practice: [specific — what broke down] Time per practice: [minutes] Number of practices this week: [number] Equipment/facility constraints: [if any] Write a day-by-day plan with time blocks, drill names, objectives per drill, coaching points for each, and how to modify for different skill levels within the group.
Pro tip: The best practice plans work backward from your next opponent or the skill that most needs improving. Don't plan time — plan outcomes, then reverse-engineer the time.

Prompt 2 — Drill Design

Help me design a new drill for a specific skill. Sport: [your sport] Skill being trained: [specific — e.g., defensive footwork, bat-to-ball contact, first-touch control, foul shooting under fatigue] Current level of athlete performance on this skill: [what they do well vs. where they break down] Constraints I want to build into the drill: [time pressure / opposition / fatigue / small space / consequence for error] How many athletes can participate at once: [number] Equipment available: [list] Design a drill with: setup diagram description, step-by-step instructions, what good execution looks like, common errors and how to correct them, how to make it easier (regression) or harder (progression), and a competitive variant to add intensity.

Prompt 3 — Season Periodization Plan

Help me plan the training progression for an entire season. Sport: [your sport] Season length: [weeks] Key dates: [first game / championship / evaluation periods / scheduled breaks] Primary performance goals: [what you want the team to peak at — fitness, technical, tactical] Areas where the team is typically undertrained by season's end: [common collapse points] Write a season periodization overview: pre-season block (what to build), early season block (what to establish), mid-season block (what to maintain and sharpen), and championship phase (what to peak). Include load management principles to reduce injury risk in the final 4 weeks.
Pro tip: Most youth and high school teams peak in week 3 and decline by the playoffs. Building in a deliberate reduction in volume (not intensity) in the final 2 weeks before championships is one of the highest-leverage coaching decisions you can make.

2. Athlete Communication

Prompt 4 — Individual Athlete Feedback

Help me write performance feedback for an individual athlete. Athlete profile: [position / experience level / general personality — competitive, sensitive, coachable, resistant] What the athlete is doing well: [specific behaviors — not generic praise] Primary development area: [one specific thing to improve — technical, tactical, mental, effort] How I want them to approach fixing it: [specific behavioral change] Their emotional state about their performance right now: [confident / frustrated / plateaued / overconfident] Write individual feedback I can deliver in a 5-minute one-on-one conversation. Lead with specific recognition, give one clear development focus, explain why it matters to their game, and close with a concrete next step they can take in the next practice.

Prompt 5 — Team Speech for a Difficult Moment

Help me write a team speech for a specific situation. Situation: [after a bad loss / before a must-win game / mid-season slump / after a team conflict / after an unexpected win over a top opponent / season-ending loss] Team's emotional state: [describe — deflated, overconfident, divided, unfocused] What I need them to take from this moment: [the core message — one idea] My coaching style: [intense motivator / calm and analytical / relationship-first / high standards] What I've already said to them about this: [so I don't repeat myself] Write a 2–3 minute speech. Not a generic pep talk — something that acknowledges the actual situation and gives them one thing to hold onto.

Prompt 6 — Parent Communication

Help me write a communication to parents about a sensitive topic. Topic: [playing time decisions / athlete behavior / team expectations / travel logistics / early season struggles / cutting a player from the roster / end of season] What I need to communicate clearly: [the core message] What I anticipate pushback on: [the part that will upset some parents] My position on that pushback: [your honest stance] Tone I want: [direct / warm / professional / firm] Write a parent communication (email or team meeting talking points). Be clear about what is and is not negotiable. Anticipate the objection and address it before it's raised.
Pro tip: Parent communication problems are almost always caused by unclear expectations set early. Write your playing time philosophy and team expectations at the start of the season — before anyone is upset — and distribute it in writing.

3. Game Preparation

Prompt 7 — Opponent Scouting Report

Help me write a scouting report and game plan for an upcoming opponent. Sport: [your sport] What I know about the opponent: [their typical formation, system, key players by position/role, tendencies, weaknesses] Our team's strengths: [what we do well] Our team's current vulnerabilities: [what we need to protect] Key matchups: [which of our players vs. which of their players] Write: (1) a 1-page scouting summary I can share with athletes, (2) 3 tactical adjustments we should make for this specific opponent, (3) set piece or situational adjustments (if applicable), and (4) the one thing we need to get right in the first 10 minutes / first quarter / first inning.

Prompt 8 — Pre-Game Mental Preparation

Help me design a pre-game mental preparation routine for my team. Sport and level: [your sport / level] Typical pre-game issue: [team gets too tight / too loose / distracted by opponent reputation / individual anxiety / inconsistent focus] Time available before game: [warm-up window] What has worked in the past: [any rituals or approaches] What hasn't worked: [what to avoid] Design a pre-game routine: physical warm-up integration, team meeting talking points (5 minutes max), individual mental preparation options, and what to do differently if we're playing a highly ranked opponent vs. a team we're supposed to beat easily.

Prompt 9 — Post-Game Review

Help me structure a post-game review for my athletes. Result: [win / loss / tie] Overall performance vs. game plan: [did we execute what we prepared?] Key moments: [turning points — what went right or wrong and why] What I want athletes to understand: [the core learning from this game] Emotional temperature of the team: [deflated / relieved / overconfident / focused] Write a post-game communication structure: (1) immediate post-game (right after the final whistle — 2 minutes), (2) cool-down talk (locker room — 5 minutes), (3) next-day film or practice debrief structure, and (4) the 3 things to put on film review or practice plan for the following week.
Pro tip: Post-game conversations immediately after a loss are rarely productive — adrenaline and emotion cloud everything. The best coaching conversations happen 24 hours later, when athletes can actually hear you.

4. Program Building

Prompt 10 — Recruiting Materials

Help me write recruiting materials for my program. Sport and level: [your sport / high school / college / club] What makes our program different: [genuine differentiators — coaching philosophy, development track record, culture, facilities, academic support] Ideal recruit profile: [the athlete and person you're looking for] Common objections recruits or their families have: [what makes them hesitate] Recruiting platform or format: [email outreach / info packet / social media bio / campus visit script] Write: (1) a program overview (200 words) that leads with what we offer, not just our record, (2) an initial outreach email to a prospective recruit, (3) 3 talking points for a family campus visit conversation, and (4) what to say when a recruit is choosing between us and a competitor.

Prompt 11 — Program Philosophy Statement

Help me write a coaching philosophy statement for my program. My sport and level: [your sport / level] What I believe about athlete development: [how athletes grow — what you prioritize] What winning means in my program: [is it everything / one of several goals / secondary to development] What I expect from athletes: [commitment, character, growth mindset — be specific] What athletes can expect from me: [what I commit to in return] Who I've been influenced by: [coaches, mentors, books — optional] Write a 400-word coaching philosophy statement. It should sound like a real person, not a mission statement generator. It should make the right athletes want to join and the wrong athletes self-select out.

Prompt 12 — Off-Season Development Plan

Help me design an off-season development program for my athletes. Sport: [your sport] Off-season duration: [weeks or months] Primary goals: [fitness base / technical skill development / strength / speed / mental reset] Resources available: [facility access / whether athletes are on their own vs. supervised / budget] Biggest gaps from last season: [what most limited performance] Write a monthly off-season plan with: Phase 1 (recovery and base building), Phase 2 (skill development), Phase 3 (pre-season preparation). Include recommended training frequency, focus areas per phase, and how to track athlete development over the off-season without burning them out.
Pro tip: The off-season athletes who improve the most aren't always the ones who train the most hours — they're the ones who train with the most intentional focus on their specific weaknesses. Help them identify the 1–2 things that would most change their game, then build the off-season around that.

5. Professional Development

Prompt 13 — Coaching Certification Study Plan

Help me prepare for a coaching certification or license. Certification target: [e.g., NSCAA / USYSA license level / NSCA-CSCS / First Aid/CPR / state athletic director certification / other] Current knowledge gaps: [what areas I'm weakest in] Time until exam or assessment: [weeks or months] Time available to study: [hours per week] Write a study plan: what to focus on first, what resources to use, how to practice practical application (not just theory), and how to prepare for any observed coaching component or written exam.

Prompt 14 — Grant or Funding Application for a Program

Help me write a grant application or funding request for my athletic program. What I need funding for: [equipment / travel / facility / coaching staff / scholarships / community program] Who I'm applying to: [local business / foundation / school district / sports governing body / community organization] What the program does for athletes: [beyond wins — development, academics, community] Number of athletes served: [approximate] What the funding would specifically enable: [that currently cannot happen without it] Write a 400–600 word funding request. Lead with athlete impact, not program statistics. Include a specific ask and what the funder's contribution would make possible.

Prompt 15 — Coaching Career Reflection

Help me reflect on my coaching career this year. Wins I'm proud of: [not just on the scoreboard — developmental, relational, cultural] Moments I handled poorly: [be honest — a communication failure, a wrong decision, a missed athlete] What I'd do differently: [specific] How my athletes grew this year: [beyond statistics] What kind of coach I want to be next year: [one concrete change in approach] Write: (1) a personal reflection I could share with my staff or mentor (not for public use), (2) the one coaching belief I want to challenge next season, and (3) a letter I could write to my team at the end of the year that they would actually want to read.
