15 ChatGPT Prompts for Teachers

Lesson plans in minutes. Differentiated activities on demand. Parent emails that don't drain you. Use AI to take back your evenings without cutting corners on quality.

The average teacher spends 10+ hours per week on administrative tasks outside the classroom. That's time spent writing feedback, adapting materials, responding to emails, and creating plans from scratch — work that AI can do in seconds if you know how to ask.

These 15 prompts are designed for classroom teachers at any level (K-12, college, ESL) who want to use ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini as a planning assistant. They save you time without sacrificing the professional judgment that only you can bring.

Lesson Planning

1. The Instant Lesson Plan Generator

Build a complete lesson plan in the time it used to take to open your planner.

Create a complete lesson plan for teaching [topic/concept] to [grade level] students. Include: 1) Learning objective (what students will be able to do by the end), 2) Materials needed, 3) Warm-up activity (5-8 minutes), 4) Direct instruction section with key points, 5) Guided practice activity with clear instructions, 6) Independent practice or exit ticket, 7) Differentiation suggestions for advanced and struggling students, 8) Extension activity for early finishers. Format it so I can use it directly tomorrow.

2. The Week-at-a-Glance Planner

I'm teaching [subject] to [grade] students next week. The unit focus is [unit topic]. We've already covered [what students know]. Standards to address: [list 2-3 standards]. Create a 5-day lesson sequence that: 1) Builds logically from Monday to Friday, 2) Varies between teacher-led, small group, and independent work, 3) Includes one formative assessment moment, 4) Leaves Friday for review/catch-up. Give me just the overview — I'll flesh each day out separately.

3. The Essential Question Maker

A good essential question anchors a unit and gives students a reason to care. This prompt writes them for you.

I'm teaching a unit on [topic] to [grade level]. The key learning goals are [describe 2-3]. Write 5 essential questions that: 1) Are open-ended (no yes/no answers), 2) Connect the content to students' real lives, 3) Could be debated differently by different students, 4) Would still matter to students 10 years from now, 5) Are written in age-appropriate language. Then recommend which one is strongest for anchoring the whole unit and why.

Student Feedback & Assessment

4. The Feedback Batch Generator

This is the one that saves the most time. Instead of writing 30 individual comments, batch-generate personalized feedback in one prompt.

I need to give written feedback on student work for a [grade level] [subject] assignment. The assignment was: [describe what students had to do]. Here are my notes on each student's strengths and areas for improvement: Student 1: [your quick notes] Student 2: [your quick notes] [continue for each student] Write complete feedback comments for each student (3-4 sentences each) that: are specific to what they did, acknowledge their effort, identify one clear area to improve, and use encouraging but honest language. Use first person ("Your essay...") not third person.

5. The Rubric Builder

Build a rubric for assessing [assignment type] in [grade level] [subject]. The assignment requires students to [describe the task]. Create a rubric with: 1) 4-5 criteria that reflect what matters most in this assignment, 2) 4 performance levels per criterion (Excellent / Proficient / Developing / Beginning), 3) Specific, observable descriptors for each cell — not vague phrases like "shows understanding," 4) Point values if I give 100 points total. Format it as a table I can print or paste into a Google Doc.

6. The Progress Report Comment Bank

Write a comment bank for [grade level] [subject] progress reports or report cards. Include comments for: 1) Academically strong students who need more challenge, 2) Students performing at grade level, 3) Students who are struggling but trying hard, 4) Students whose behavior affects their learning, 5) Students who have shown significant growth this marking period. Write 3 different comment options for each category. Keep each under 60 words. Avoid generic phrases like "works well with others" — be specific about skills and behaviors.

Differentiation & Student Support

7. The Differentiation Adapter

The hardest part of differentiation isn't knowing you should do it — it's having time to create multiple versions of the same activity.

I have this [activity/assignment] for my [grade level] [subject] class: [describe or paste the original activity]. Adapt it into 3 versions: 1) Modified version for students 2+ years below grade level — simplify language and reduce complexity while keeping the core skill, 2) On-grade-level version (keep essentially as-is, just clean up formatting), 3) Extended version for students who need more challenge — add complexity, require deeper thinking, or add an extension layer. Keep the same learning objective across all three versions.

8. The IEP Accommodation Translator

A student in my class has these IEP accommodations: [list the accommodations]. I'm teaching a lesson on [topic] using [describe your teaching format/activity]. Tell me: 1) Which of my planned activities might create barriers for this student and why, 2) How to modify each problematic activity without lowering expectations, 3) Any additional support strategies I could build into the lesson proactively, 4) How to include this student in whole-class discussions in a way that supports their confidence. Be practical — I have 25 other students and 50 minutes.

9. The English Language Learner Support Prompt

I have ELL students in my [grade level] [subject] class at [proficiency levels: beginning/intermediate/advanced]. I'm teaching a lesson on [topic]. Suggest specific scaffolding strategies for this lesson: 1) Vocabulary pre-teaching approach (which words, how to teach them in 5 minutes), 2) Visual or graphic organizer I could use during instruction, 3) Modified speaking/writing tasks for each proficiency level, 4) A sentence frame bank students can use for the exit activity, 5) How to pair ELL students for group work in a way that supports language development. Be specific — not general ESL theory.

Parent & Guardian Communication

10. The Parent Email Writer

Parent communication emails are often the most emotionally taxing writing teachers do. This prompt takes the weight off.

Write a parent email about [situation: academic concern / behavioral issue / positive achievement / conference request]. Key facts: Student: [first name only]. Situation: [describe what happened or needs addressing]. Action already taken: [what you've already done]. What you need from the parent: [next step]. Tone should be: professional, collaborative, non-accusatory, and focused on the student's success — not on assigning blame. Keep it under 200 words. Include a subject line.

11. The Conference Preparation Script

I have a parent conference coming up for a student who is [describe the situation: struggling academically / having behavioral issues / thriving but parents concerned about challenge level]. I want to: 1) Start with something genuine and positive, 2) Present the concern clearly without alarming the parents, 3) Share 2-3 specific examples from class, 4) Propose a specific plan with clear responsibilities for the teacher, student, and parents, 5) End with a follow-up date. Write a structured script I can use as a guide for a 15-minute conference. Include transition phrases between sections.

Classroom Management & Routines

12. The Classroom Procedure Writer

Write clear, step-by-step classroom procedures for [grade level] students for: [choose one or more: entering the classroom / turning in assignments / asking for help / transitioning between activities / bathroom break protocol / group work roles / end of class routine]. Write each procedure as a numbered list in simple, direct language that students can read and remember. Also write a brief script I can use to introduce and practice this procedure on the first day.

13. The Behavior Intervention Script

I'm dealing with a recurring behavior issue: [describe the behavior — e.g., constant talking during instruction, refusing to start work, conflict with peers]. I've already tried: [list what hasn't worked]. The student is in [grade]. Write: 1) A script for a private conversation I can have with this student (without shame or ultimatums), 2) 2-3 low-profile in-class strategies I can try without disrupting the lesson, 3) A behavior-specific positive reinforcement plan, 4) How to document this if I need to escalate to administration. Focus on relationship-first approaches.

14. The Student Engagement Idea Generator

When the class energy is low, you need new engagement strategies fast. This prompt generates them on demand.

My [grade level] [subject] class is losing engagement during [part of class: lectures / independent work / transitions / a specific type of activity]. Suggest 8 evidence-based engagement strategies I could try, specifically for [describe the situation]. For each strategy: 1) Name it, 2) Explain how to implement it in under 60 seconds, 3) Estimate the energy level it creates (low/medium/high), 4) Note any prep required. Include at least 2 strategies that require no prep at all.

15. The Professional Email to Admin

Advocating for yourself or your students in writing is a skill that AI can help you do more confidently.

Write a professional email to my [principal / department head / counselor / special ed coordinator] about [topic: requesting resources / reporting a student concern / proposing a program change / requesting coverage / following up on an unresolved issue]. Key points I want to make: [list 2-3 main points]. What I need from them: [specific ask]. Tone: professional, collegial, assertive but not confrontational. Include a subject line. Keep it under 150 words unless more is needed for context.
Pro tip: The prompt that saves most teachers the most time is #4 (Feedback Batch Generator). Try it on your next set of papers — write your quick notes on each student, paste them in, and get complete feedback comments in one go. It's not replacing your judgment; it's doing the drafting so you can focus on reviewing.
