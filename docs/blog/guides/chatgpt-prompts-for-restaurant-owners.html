ChatGPT Prompts for Restaurant Owners

15 free prompts to write better menus, handle reviews professionally, market your restaurant, train staff, and improve profitability — with less admin overwhelm.

Running a restaurant means handling a hundred things at once — and most of them require writing: menus, job posts, review responses, social captions, training materials, supplier emails. These 15 prompts handle the words so you can focus on the food and your team.

1. Marketing & Promotion

Prompt 1 — Social Media Content Calendar

Build a 30-day social media content calendar for my restaurant. Restaurant: [name] | Type: [cuisine / concept] | Location: [city] Platforms: [Instagram / Facebook / TikTok] Posting frequency: [X times/week] Upcoming events or promotions: [weekly specials, seasonal menu, holiday, event] Content I can create: [photos of food / behind-the-scenes / staff stories / video] 30 post ideas: For each — platform, content type, caption hook (first line), relevant hashtags. Mix: food photography, behind-the-scenes kitchen, staff spotlights, customer stories, local community content, limited-time offers, and seasonal items. Ratio: 70% value/story, 30% promotional.
Pro tip: Behind-the-scenes kitchen content consistently outperforms staged food photography for engagement. Show the craft, not just the plate.

Prompt 2 — Google Business Profile Posts

Write 8 Google Business Profile posts for my restaurant. Restaurant: [name] | Cuisine: [type] | Location: [city] Current promotion or news: [weekly special / new menu item / event / milestone] Post types to include: (1) Weekly special announcement, (2) New menu item spotlight, (3) "Why locals love us" — community angle, (4) Behind-the-scenes story, (5) Seasonal limited item, (6) Private dining / event booking CTA, (7) 5-star review highlight (with permission), (8) Holiday message + holiday hours. Each post: under 300 words, includes a CTA, and ends with a call to book or visit. Google Posts expire after 7 days — write these to be relevant for 1 week each.

2. Menu Writing

Prompt 3 — Menu Item Descriptions

Write menu descriptions for these dishes at my restaurant. Restaurant concept: [describe your vibe — fine dining / casual / fast casual / farm-to-table / etc.] Tone: [elevated / casual / playful / minimal] Dishes to describe: 1. [Dish name] — ingredients: [list] 2. [Dish name] — ingredients: [list] 3. [Dish name] — ingredients: [list] For each: Write a 25–40 word description that: evokes the experience (not just lists ingredients) → uses sensory language → matches our concept's tone → helps guests who are undecided choose this dish. Avoid: "delicious," "amazing," "mouth-watering" — show, don't tell.
Pro tip: Menu items with descriptive names sell 27% more than those with plain names. "Slow-braised short rib with crispy shallots" outperforms "Beef Short Rib" every time.

Prompt 4 — Seasonal Menu Concept

Help me build a seasonal menu for [Spring / Summer / Fall / Winter]. My restaurant: [concept and cuisine type] Price range: [$] / [$$] / [$$$] Ingredients available this season: [list local or seasonal ingredients in your region] Current bestsellers I want to keep: [list] Number of new items I'm adding: [number] Suggest: (1) 3 new appetizers, (2) 4 new entrees, (3) 2 new desserts. For each: name, key ingredients, brief description, estimated food cost range. Highlight: which items showcase seasonal ingredients → which would appeal to dietary preferences (vegetarian, gluten-free, etc.) → which have the best margin potential.

3. Review Management

Prompt 5 — Positive Review Response Templates

Write 8 response templates for positive Google/Yelp reviews. Restaurant: [name] | Cuisine: [type] | Tone: [warm / professional / friendly] Templates for: (1) Reviewer mentions specific dish, (2) First-time visitor, (3) Regular customer, (4) Reviewer mentions a staff member by name, (5) Family dining experience, (6) Date night / special occasion, (7) Reviewer mentions value for money, (8) Out-of-town visitor. Each template: personalized feel (reference what they mentioned) → thank specific element → invite them back / mention something upcoming. 50–80 words each. Avoid starting every response with "Thank you for your review."

Prompt 6 — Negative Review Response

A customer left this negative review: Review: "[paste the full review]" Stars: [number] Was the complaint valid? [yes / partially / no — your honest assessment] Did something go wrong that day? [context if relevant] Write a public response (Google/Yelp — this is visible to everyone): (1) Acknowledge without being defensive, (2) Take responsibility for what went wrong (if appropriate), (3) Explain what you've done or will do differently, (4) Invite them to return / make it right, (5) Keep it under 150 words. The goal: impress the 50 people reading this, not just this reviewer. Future guests decide based on how you handle complaints.
Pro tip: Never argue, over-explain, or blame staff publicly. A gracious, accountable response converts skeptical future customers far more effectively than a defensive one.

4. Staff & Operations

Prompt 7 — Job Posting Writer

Write a job posting for a [position: line cook / server / host / bartender / manager / dishwasher]. Restaurant: [name] | Type: [concept and cuisine] Location: [city] Hours: [full-time / part-time, typical shift times] Pay: $[range] + [tips if applicable] What makes working here great: [culture, food quality, growth opportunity, team] Requirements: [experience level, skills, certifications] Write a posting that: Leads with what makes us a great place to work (not just the duties list) → Describes the role honestly → Lists requirements without over-filtering great candidates → Ends with an easy application CTA. Tone: [professional / energetic / down-to-earth]. Make it stand out from generic hospitality job posts.

Prompt 8 — Staff Training Guide Section

Write a training guide section for new [servers / hosts / bartenders / kitchen staff] at my restaurant. Topic: [table service steps / upselling techniques / opening procedures / closing procedures / allergy handling / difficult customer situations / POS system basics] My restaurant: [concept, price point] Experience level of new staff: [entry-level / experienced] Write: (1) Why this matters (brief — connect to guest experience and tips/reviews), (2) Step-by-step process (numbered, clear), (3) Examples of what to do and what NOT to do, (4) Common mistakes and how to avoid them, (5) How this gets checked or measured. Under 500 words. Practical and direct — not corporate HR language.

5. Customer Communication

Prompt 9 — Event Booking Response Templates

Write response templates for private event and reservation inquiries. Restaurant: [name] | Private dining capacity: [number] | Cuisine: [type] Minimum spend or deposit policy: [describe] What we offer for events: [private room / exclusive buyout / custom menu / AV / etc.] Templates for: (1) Initial response to a private event inquiry, (2) Follow-up when they haven't responded, (3) Confirming a booking with details, (4) Handling a guest who wants to negotiate price, (5) Gracious decline when we can't accommodate the request. Each: professional, warm, and makes us sound like the obvious choice for their event. Under 150 words each.

Prompt 10 — Email Newsletter for Regulars

Write a monthly email newsletter for our restaurant's customer list. Restaurant: [name] | Cuisine: [type] This month's news: [new menu items / upcoming event / seasonal special / staff news / milestone] Reservation link: [URL] Tone: [personal and warm — as if the owner is writing directly] Newsletter sections: (1) Personal note from the owner/chef (150 words — authentic, not marketing-speak), (2) What's new this month (menu / events), (3) "In season" feature — one ingredient or dish spotlight, (4) A table reservation CTA that doesn't feel pushy. Under 400 words total. This is a relationship, not a promotion.
Pro tip: Email newsletters from restaurants have some of the highest open rates in the industry — 35–50% — because subscribers are genuinely fans. Write like it, not like a mass-market blast.

6. Finance & Profitability

Prompt 11 — Menu Engineering Analysis

Help me analyze my menu for profitability. My dishes with their approximate food cost % and popularity: [Paste or list: Dish Name → food cost % → approx sales per week] Using menu engineering principles (Stars / Plowhorses / Puzzles / Dogs), categorize each item: (1) Stars (high profit, high popularity) — how to leverage these, (2) Plowhorses (low profit, high popularity) — how to improve margin, (3) Puzzles (high profit, low popularity) — how to promote these, (4) Dogs (low profit, low popularity) — should they be cut? Give me specific action recommendations for each category.
Pro tip: Stars are your superstars — feature them prominently in photos and descriptions. Puzzles often just need better menu placement or photography to become Stars.

Prompt 12 — Supplier Negotiation Prep

Help me prepare to negotiate with my food supplier / produce vendor / beverage distributor. Current situation: [what I'm buying, current prices, volume] Issue I want to address: [price increase / quality issue / delivery reliability / minimum order size] My leverage: [payment history, volume, relationship length, alternatives I'm considering] What I want: [price reduction / better terms / more flexibility / priority delivery] Prepare me: (1) Opening framing for the conversation, (2) The specific ask with justification, (3) What to offer in return (commitment, volume, payment timing), (4) How to handle "this is our best price", (5) When to walk away and what my alternative is. This is a long-term relationship — help me be firm without being adversarial.

7. Strategy & Growth

Prompt 13 — Slow Season Strategy

My restaurant faces a [winter / summer / post-holiday] slow season. Help me build a strategy to maintain revenue. Restaurant: [concept, capacity, average check] My slow season: [months, typical drop in covers/revenue] Current promotions I run: [describe or "none"] What's worked before: [list or "nothing specific"] Resources: [what staff, budget, partnerships I have access to] Strategy: (1) Revenue tactics that drive covers during slow period, (2) Marketing campaigns with specific messaging, (3) Slow season menu or pricing strategy, (4) Community partnerships or events to generate traffic, (5) What to do with the slower time to improve operations. Be specific — not generic "run a promotion" advice.

Prompt 14 — Online Ordering & Delivery Strategy

Help me optimize my restaurant's online ordering and delivery strategy. Current setup: [delivery platforms: DoorDash / Uber Eats / GrubHub / own website / none] Delivery percentage of total revenue: [%] Main challenge: [high commission fees / low average order value / packaging issues / quality on delivery] Goal: [increase delivery revenue / reduce platform dependency / improve margins] Recommend: (1) Which platforms are worth staying on and which to reconsider, (2) How to build direct ordering to reduce commissions, (3) How to design the delivery menu differently from the dine-in menu, (4) Packaging investment that protects quality, (5) How to use delivery data to understand customer behavior. Calculate the margin impact of shifting 10% of orders to direct ordering.

Prompt 15 — Annual Restaurant Review

Help me review my restaurant's year. Year summary: [revenue, covers served, avg check, best months, worst months] Wins: [what went well — dishes, staff, marketing, events] Challenges: [what was hard — costs, turnover, slow periods, supply issues] Customer sentiment: [what reviews said most often, positive and negative] Where I am vs. where I wanted to be: [honest gap] Analyze: (1) What drove my best months — can I replicate this? (2) My biggest cost leak and what to do about it, (3) The #1 reason we lose customers (from reviews and patterns), (4) My priorities for next year: operations, menu, marketing, team, (5) The honest answer to: what am I avoiding that could make the biggest difference?
