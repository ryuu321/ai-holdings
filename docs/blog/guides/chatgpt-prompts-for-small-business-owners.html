15 ChatGPT Prompts for Small Business Owners

Stop wearing all the hats at once. Use AI to handle the writing, planning, and communication that eats your time — so you can focus on what actually grows the business.

Big companies have entire departments for marketing, HR, and operations. As a small business owner, that's you — all of it, all the time. These 15 prompts give you the leverage of a full team without the overhead.

These work for retail shops, service businesses, restaurants, online stores, local services, and any small business with under 50 employees. Use them in ChatGPT (free or Plus), Claude, or Gemini. Always review before sending to customers.

Marketing & Customer Acquisition

1. The Google Business Profile Optimizer

Your Google Business Profile is your most valuable free marketing tool. Most small businesses waste it with a generic description.

Write an optimized Google Business Profile description for my business. Business name: [name]. Type: [restaurant / salon / plumber / retail store / etc.]. Location: [city/neighborhood]. What makes us different: [3-4 specific differentiators — not "great service" but actual specifics: years in business, specialty, awards, unique offerings]. Target customer: [describe your ideal customer]. Services/products: [main offerings]. Write a 750-character description that: 1) Leads with the most important keyword naturally, 2) Describes exactly what we do and for whom, 3) Highlights what makes us genuinely different, 4) Includes a subtle location signal, 5) Ends with a CTA. Also suggest 5 Google Business categories and 10 attributes to enable.

2. The Local SEO Content Plan

Create a 90-day local SEO content plan for [business name] in [city]. Business type: [describe]. My 3 main services/products: [list]. Current online presence: [website yes/no, social media platforms]. Target keywords I want to rank for: [list 3-5 local search terms like "best [service] in [city]"]. Build a plan that: 1) Lists 12 blog or website page topics (one per week) targeting local search terms, 2) Identifies 3 "near me" or local intent keywords I'm missing, 3) Suggests 2 types of content that earn local backlinks (local press, community involvement), 4) Prioritizes which page to create first for fastest ranking impact. Be specific to my industry — not generic "create good content" advice.

3. The Customer Review Response System

Write a response system for Google/Yelp reviews for [business name]. Create response templates for: 1) 5-star reviews — 4 different responses that feel genuine and varied (not the same reply to everyone), 2) 4-star reviews — acknowledge the positive, address the "almost 5 stars" gap, 3) 3-star reviews — thank them, acknowledge the specific issue, explain what changed or what we'll do differently, 4) 1-2 star reviews — de-escalate, offer to make it right, move the conversation offline without being defensive. Also write a response to this specific review: [paste a real review you've received]. Guidelines: under 100 words each, professional but warm, always sign with a real name.

Customer Communication

4. The Email Newsletter Template

Write a monthly email newsletter template for [business name]. Industry: [describe]. Subscriber list: [customers who bought from us / leads who expressed interest / mix]. Goal of newsletter: [keep customers returning / promote seasonal offers / build community / all of the above]. Create a newsletter format with: 1) Subject line formula (give me 3 options for the first newsletter), 2) Opening section: personal update from the owner (2-3 sentences — what's happening at the business this month), 3) Feature section: one helpful tip, recipe, how-to, or insight relevant to our customers, 4) Offer section: current promotion or new product (not every email — show me when to include vs. skip), 5) Community section: customer story or local connection, 6) Closing CTA. Keep total reading time under 3 minutes.

5. The Difficult Customer Handler

Help me respond to this difficult customer situation: [describe the situation — refund request / aggressive complaint / leaving negative review / disputing a charge / making unreasonable demands]. My policy on this: [describe your refund/returns/service policy]. The customer's perspective (steelman it): [try to understand why they feel this way]. Write: 1) A response that acknowledges their frustration without admitting fault if I'm not at fault, 2) A clear statement of my policy in plain language, 3) What I'm willing to offer (if anything), 4) A closing that preserves the relationship even if we disagree. Also tell me: is this worth fighting or should I just resolve it to protect my reputation? Give me a direct recommendation.

6. The Referral Program Designer

Design a word-of-mouth referral program for [business name]. Average transaction value: $[X]. Average customer lifetime value: $[X]. How customers currently hear about us: [describe]. Design a program that: 1) The incentive structure (what to give referring customers and new customers — make it specific to my business type), 2) The ask — exact words to say in person, by text, and by email when asking for referrals, 3) How to track referrals without expensive software, 4) When in the customer journey to make the ask (first visit? after great service? etc.), 5) A simple "refer a friend" card or text template. Budget: $0 beyond the discount/incentive itself. Make it simple enough that my staff can execute it.

Operations & Team

7. The Job Posting Writer

Write a job posting for [role] at [business name]. What this person will actually do day-to-day: [describe the real work]. Must-have skills: [list]. Nice-to-have skills: [list]. Pay range: $[X]-$[Y] [hourly/salary]. Benefits: [list what you offer]. Company culture in 2 sentences: [describe honestly]. Write a job posting that: 1) Opens with why someone would WANT this job (not just what we need), 2) Describes the role in plain language — no corporate buzzwords, 3) Is honest about the hard parts of the job (reduces bad-fit applications), 4) Makes clear what growth opportunities exist, 5) Has a simple application process. Under 400 words. Also write a shorter version for social media (under 150 words).

8. The Standard Operating Procedure Builder

Help me write a standard operating procedure (SOP) for [task]. The task: [describe what needs to happen, when, and who currently does it]. Current pain points: [what goes wrong or inconsistently when someone else does this]. Write an SOP that: 1) States the purpose in one sentence, 2) Lists required materials/access/tools, 3) Gives step-by-step instructions in plain language (numbered, not prose), 4) Includes checkpoints — how to verify each step was done correctly, 5) Lists common mistakes and how to avoid them, 6) States who to ask if something goes wrong. Format it so a new employee could follow it on day one without asking me. No business jargon.

9. The Vendor Negotiation Script

Write a negotiation script for renewing/renegotiating my contract with [vendor type: supplier / landlord / service provider]. Current terms: [describe — monthly cost, contract length, what's included]. What I want to change: [lower price / better terms / more included / longer payment terms]. My leverage: [how long I've been a customer, payment history, volume, alternatives I've researched]. Write: 1) An opening that positions this as a conversation, not a demand, 2) The specific ask with justification, 3) A response to "we can't lower prices right now", 4) An alternative ask if they won't move on price (what else has value?), 5) A graceful exit if they won't budge (how to leave the door open). Also: what should I research before this conversation?

Strategy & Growth

10. The Competitor Analysis

Help me analyze my 3 main competitors: [name each one and describe them briefly — size, location, what you know about their pricing and approach]. My business: [describe]. Run an analysis that: 1) Identifies where each competitor is stronger than me, 2) Identifies their visible weaknesses (based on reviews, social media, what customers say), 3) Finds gaps in the market none of us is serving well, 4) Recommends one thing I could do differently that would be genuinely hard for them to copy, 5) Identifies one segment of customers they're probably not serving well that I could win. Give me actionable insight, not a SWOT table.

11. The Price Increase Communication

Help me communicate a price increase to my customers. Background: I'm raising prices by [X%] effective [date]. Reason (honest): [inflation / increased costs / haven't raised prices in X years / investing in quality]. Customer types: [describe your main customer groups]. Write: 1) An email to existing customers announcing the increase (explain the why without over-apologizing), 2) A short script for telling customers in person, 3) How to handle "I'll find someone cheaper" — a response that doesn't grovel but doesn't lose the relationship, 4) Updated FAQ text for my website about pricing. Tone: confident and transparent, not defensive.

12. The 90-Day Growth Plan

Build a 90-day growth plan for [business name]. Current situation: Monthly revenue: $[X]. Main revenue sources: [list]. Biggest constraint right now: [too few customers / not enough repeat business / can't deliver faster / staff issues / cash flow]. Growth target: [X% increase in revenue or X new customers]. Resources available: Time: [X hours/week for business development]. Budget: $[X/month for marketing]. Build a plan with: 1) One primary growth lever for these 90 days (pick the highest-leverage action, not 10 things), 2) Week-by-week milestones, 3) The metrics to track weekly, 4) What I should STOP doing to make room for the growth work. Be specific to my business — not a template I have to translate.

Financial & Administrative

13. The Grant Application Writer

Help me write a small business grant application. Grant: [name of grant and what it's for]. My business: [describe — what you do, how long you've been in business, location, team size]. What I'd use the money for: [specific, not "grow my business"]. Why my business fits the grant criteria: [explain the connection]. My impact on the community or industry: [describe]. Write: 1) The business description section (under 200 words, compelling), 2) The use of funds section (specific breakdown with dollar amounts), 3) The community impact section (concrete, not vague), 4) A closing paragraph that explains why my business specifically deserves this grant. Review what I've written for grant language that sounds weak and help me strengthen it.

14. The Invoice Follow-Up Email Sequence

Write a 3-email sequence for following up on unpaid invoices. Context: Invoice amount: $[X]. Due date: [X days ago]. Client relationship: [new client / long-term client / one-time project]. Write: Email 1 (1 day after due): Friendly reminder — assume it was overlooked, not intentional. Email 2 (7 days after due): Firmer but still professional — acknowledge we haven't heard back, state what happens next if unpaid. Email 3 (14 days after due): Final notice — clear about consequences (late fees, stopping work, collections) without being hostile. For each email: subject line + body under 80 words. Also write a text message version of Email 1 for clients I normally text.

15. The Business Plan Executive Summary

Write an executive summary for [business name] to share with [a bank for a loan / a potential investor / a landlord for a commercial lease]. Business overview: [what you do and for whom]. Years in operation: [X]. Annual revenue: $[X] (or projected: $[X]). What makes us profitable/viable: [key differentiators and demand signals]. How this funding will be used: [specific purpose]. Projected outcome in 12 months: [what the money will make possible]. Write a 300-word executive summary that: 1) Opens with the business opportunity, not the company history, 2) Demonstrates market demand with specifics, 3) Shows the financial ask is reasonable relative to the opportunity, 4) Ends with why we're the team/business to execute this. Professional but plain language — no MBA buzzwords.
