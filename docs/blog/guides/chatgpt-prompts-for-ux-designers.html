ChatGPT Prompts for UX Designers

15 free prompts for UX and product designers. Write better research plans, present design rationale, handle stakeholders, and advance your career.

UX design work is 50% craft and 50% communication — and most designers underinvest in the latter. These 15 prompts help you write better research plans, articulate design decisions in business language, navigate stakeholder dynamics, and build the kind of portfolio that lands senior roles.

1. User Research

Prompt 1 — Research Plan Builder

Help me build a user research plan. Research question I'm trying to answer: [what do you need to learn?] Product/feature being designed: [describe] Timeline: [how long do I have?] Resources: [team size, budget, participant access] Constraints: [remote only / enterprise users / regulated industry / etc.] Create: (1) Research objectives (3–5 specific, measurable questions), (2) Recommended method(s) and why (interviews / usability tests / surveys / diary studies / etc.), (3) Participant screener criteria, (4) Session guide outline (for qualitative) or survey structure (for quantitative), (5) How to analyze and synthesize findings, (6) What outputs to deliver and to whom. Keep it lean — the right research done quickly beats the perfect research done too late.
Pro tip: 5–8 user interviews reveal most major usability issues. You rarely need more than 10 participants to see patterns in qualitative research.

Prompt 2 — Interview Questions Generator

Write a user interview guide for a study about [topic/behavior/product area]. Research goal: [what I need to understand] User type: [who I'm interviewing] Interview length: [45 / 60 / 90 minutes] Experience level with the product: [new user / existing user / power user] Structure: (1) Warm-up questions (2–3 to build rapport and gather context), (2) Core questions (8–12 open-ended, behavioral questions — avoid leading), (3) Probing questions for each core topic, (4) Task scenarios (if usability component), (5) Wrap-up / anything I missed. Flag any questions that are leading or hypothetical (those are usability test mistakes). Include a "why" for each section.

2. Design Communication

Prompt 3 — Design Rationale Writer

Help me write the design rationale for this decision. Design decision: [what I chose to do — be specific] What I considered but didn't do: [alternatives you evaluated] The constraint that shaped this decision: [technical / business / user need / brand] Evidence or data informing the decision: [research findings, usability data, analytics] Who needs to understand this: [stakeholder type — PM / engineer / executive] Write a rationale that: States the decision clearly → Explains the user problem it solves → Shows the tradeoffs considered → Connects to a business or product goal → Anticipates the "why not X" question. Length: 150–250 words. No design jargon that a PM wouldn't know.
Pro tip: Write design rationale in terms of the user problem and business outcome, not the design solution. "We simplified the nav because users were failing to find checkout in 40% of sessions" lands better than "we improved the UX."

Prompt 4 — Stakeholder Presentation Structure

Help me structure a design presentation for [context: design review / exec review / user research readout / design critique]. Audience: [who will be in the room — title/role] Time available: [minutes] Goal: [decision needed / awareness / feedback / approval] Design maturity: [early concept / mid-fidelity / final proposal] Anticipated pushback: [what objections or questions I expect] Structure every slide with: Slide topic → Key message (one sentence the audience must walk away with) → What to show (visuals / data / quotes) → Presenter notes (what to say vs. what's on screen). Start with the insight or problem — not the design. End with a clear ask.

3. Stakeholder & Team Collaboration

Prompt 5 — Handling Stakeholder Pushback

A stakeholder is pushing back on my design decision. Here's the situation: My design recommendation: [what I proposed] Their pushback: [exactly what they said or are insisting on] Their role/influence: [PM / executive / engineer / marketing] Data or evidence I have: [what supports my decision] Is there validity to their concern? [yes / partially / no — explain] Help me: (1) Understand what's really driving their concern (user, business, or personal), (2) Reframe the conversation toward shared goals, (3) Script a response that's collaborative, not defensive, (4) Know when to stand firm vs. when to find a middle path, (5) Propose a test or data-driven resolution. I want to solve this without damaging the relationship or shipping a bad design.

Prompt 6 — Design Critique Facilitation

Help me facilitate a productive design critique session. What's being reviewed: [design artifact type, stage] Team involved: [designers / PMs / engineers / researchers — how many] Time available: [minutes] Goal: [feedback for iteration / decision to ship / learning exercise] Potential issues I anticipate: [senior person dominates / too abstract / no time for next steps] Create: (1) Session agenda (timed), (2) Framing statement to open the session (set expectations and ground rules), (3) Critique prompt questions that generate specific, useful feedback (not "looks good"), (4) How to capture and prioritize feedback, (5) Closing framework — how to turn critique into clear next steps. Include how to handle the person who says "I don't like it" without specifics.
Pro tip: Start critique with "How well does this design solve the stated problem?" — not "what do you think?" The former generates useful feedback. The latter generates opinions.

4. UX Writing & Content

Prompt 7 — UX Copy Generator

Write UX copy for the following interface elements. Product: [describe the product]. User: [who is using it]. Brand tone: [friendly / professional / minimal / warm]. Elements needed: 1. Empty state: [what the screen shows when there's no data yet] 2. Error message: [what went wrong and what they should do] 3. Success message: [what just completed successfully] 4. Onboarding tooltip: [explain [feature] to a new user] 5. Confirmation dialog: [asking the user to confirm a destructive action — [what action]] 6. Loading state: [what's happening while they wait] 7. Placeholder text: [for [input field type]] For each: Write 2 variations. Label: [clear/direct] and [warm/conversational]. Flag any that risk confusing or alarming users.

Prompt 8 — Content Audit Framework

Help me audit the content in [product area / user flow: onboarding / checkout / settings / error states / etc.]. Product: [describe] The user task I'm auditing: [what the user is trying to do] Known pain points: [where users get confused or drop off] Audit framework: (1) Inventory — what content exists (labels, headers, body copy, tooltips, errors), (2) Consistency check — same concepts named differently across screens, (3) Clarity check — would a new user understand each piece without context?, (4) Tone check — does the copy match the brand voice?, (5) Action orientation — does every screen tell the user what to do next? Output as a prioritized list of content problems with severity rating (critical / high / medium / low).

5. Portfolio & Career

Prompt 9 — Case Study Writer

Help me write a UX portfolio case study for [project name]. Context: [what the product is, who the users are, what organization/context] My role: [what I specifically did on this project] The problem: [what we were trying to solve and why it mattered] My process: [methods used — research, synthesis, ideation, prototyping, testing, iteration] Key decisions and tradeoffs: [2–3 important choices you made and why] Outcome: [what shipped, what the results were — metrics if available] Write: (1) Project intro (150 words), (2) Problem framing section (200 words), (3) Process narrative (400 words — focused on decisions, not just activities), (4) Key design decisions section (250 words), (5) Outcome + reflection (150 words). Avoid: "I used a design thinking process" (everyone says this). Focus on the specific decisions that show your judgment.
Pro tip: The best portfolio case studies show how you think, not just what you made. The "why did you choose this approach?" question is your strongest content.

Prompt 10 — Interview Answer Prep

Help me prepare for a UX design interview at [company type: startup / enterprise / agency / product company]. Role I'm interviewing for: [title and seniority level] My background: [years of experience, specialties] My strongest project: [brief description] The question I struggle with most: [describe] Prepare me for: (1) "Walk me through your design process" — a 4-minute answer that shows strategic thinking, (2) "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a stakeholder" — situation, action, result, (3) "How do you know when a design is good enough?" — shows business sense, (4) Design challenge approach (if they give you a live problem to solve), (5) Questions I should ask them that signal senior-level thinking. Write full sample answers I can adapt.

6. Strategy & Growth

Prompt 11 — Design System Proposal

Help me write a proposal to invest in a design system for [company/product]. Current state: [describe: inconsistent UI, no shared components, multiple platforms, team size] Problem this causes: [describe the pain — dev rework, slow design, inconsistent UX] Audience for this proposal: [who needs to approve] Resources needed: [estimated time, people, tools] Write a proposal that: Quantifies the current cost (dev rework, design inconsistency) → Describes what a design system would include → States the business case (speed, consistency, scale) → Proposes a phased approach (quick win → foundation → mature system) → Addresses "what about our current deadlines?" → Ends with a clear ask. Under 500 words. No design jargon.

Prompt 12 — Measuring UX Impact

Help me define metrics to measure the impact of my UX work. Project: [what you designed] Business goal this supports: [revenue / retention / activation / support cost / conversion] What we changed: [the specific design intervention] User behavior we're trying to influence: [what you want users to do differently] Design a measurement framework: (1) Primary metric (the number that matters most to the business), (2) Secondary metrics (supporting signals), (3) Guardrail metrics (what shouldn't get worse), (4) How to isolate the design's contribution vs. other changes, (5) What "success" looks like at 30 / 90 days. Also: how to communicate this to non-designers in a way they find credible.
Pro tip: Frame UX metrics in business language: "reduced checkout drop-off by 12% = $X additional monthly revenue" wins more credibility and budget than "improved usability."

Prompt 13 — UX Audit of a Competitor

Help me conduct a UX audit of a competitor product. Product I'm auditing: [name, URL if available] Audit scope: [specific flow or area: onboarding / checkout / dashboard / search] My product for comparison: [name] Audit framework: (1) Task completion — can a new user accomplish [core task] without confusion? (2) Information architecture — is it clear what's where and why? (3) Visual clarity — does the UI support or fight the user's task? (4) Error prevention and recovery, (5) Mobile responsiveness, (6) Accessibility basics. For each category: rate 1–5, describe what I observed, and note what my product does better or worse. Use this as a competitive intelligence input, not a copy guide.

Prompt 14 — Design Leadership Progression

I'm a [mid-level / senior] UX designer wanting to move to [senior / lead / principal / manager] level. Help me understand what the transition requires. My current work: [describe what you do day-to-day] What I'm good at: [design craft, research, facilitation, etc.] What I avoid or struggle with: [be honest] Company size/type: [startup / scale-up / enterprise] My goal timeline: [6 months / 1 year / 2 years] Map out: (1) What the next level actually does differently (scope, influence, impact), (2) Skills I need to develop — with specific, concrete ways to build them, (3) What I should start doing now that demonstrates senior-level judgment, (4) How to make my impact visible to leadership, (5) The honest assessment of what's holding me back.

Prompt 15 — Annual UX Design Review

Help me run my annual career review as a UX designer. Projects shipped this year: [list] Biggest impact I had: [describe with numbers if possible] Skills I grew: [specific] Skills I avoided building: [honest] What I wish had gone differently: [describe] Where I am vs. where I want to be: [describe the gap] Goals for next year: [career / craft / impact / income] Analyze: (1) What pattern does my best work this year share? (2) What type of work do I do that has the highest leverage? (3) What skill, if I had it, would make the biggest difference? (4) What should I stop doing to free up capacity for growth? (5) My #1 priority for Q1.
