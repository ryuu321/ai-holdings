15 ChatGPT Prompts for Lawyers & Attorneys

AI doesn't practice law — but it can handle the writing, communication, and business tasks that aren't billable, so you can focus on the legal work that is.

The legal profession generates enormous amounts of non-billable writing: client intake emails, status updates, engagement letters, marketing content, billing disputes, and more. These prompts help you draft faster, communicate more clearly, and run a better practice — without replacing your legal judgment.

These work for solo practitioners, small firm attorneys, in-house counsel, law firm partners, and legal professionals in any practice area. Always review AI output carefully — never send AI-generated legal content without professional review. Use in ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini.

⚠️ Critical reminder: AI is a drafting tool, not a lawyer. Never input client confidential information into AI tools. Never use AI output as legal advice without professional review. Check your jurisdiction's bar rules on AI use before deploying these prompts in client matters.

Client Communication

1. The Plain-Language Legal Explanation

Clients who understand what you're doing for them are more satisfied, more likely to refer, and less likely to call with anxiety-driven questions.

Explain [legal concept or process] in plain language for a client with no legal background. Concept: [discovery / statute of limitations / motion to dismiss / deposition / escrow / easement / fiduciary duty / pleading / etc.]. Client's situation: [describe what they're going through — buying a home / in a dispute / starting a business / going through divorce / estate planning]. Write an explanation that: 1) Uses one real-world analogy to make the concept concrete, 2) Explains what this means FOR THEM specifically in their situation, 3) Answers the unstated question: "should I be worried about this?", 4) States clearly what they need to do (if anything), 5) Is under 200 words — no legal jargon without immediate plain-language translation. Also: what's the single most important thing they should understand about this?

2. The Client Status Update Template

Write client status update email templates for [practice area: litigation / real estate / estate planning / business / family law / criminal / immigration / employment]. Create templates for: 1) Routine update when nothing has changed — clients need to hear from you even when there's no news, 2) Update delivering good news — a favorable ruling, settlement offer, or milestone reached, 3) Update delivering a delay or setback — how to communicate bad news without alarming the client unnecessarily, 4) Pre-hearing or pre-closing preparation email — what the client needs to know and do, 5) Matter completion email — closing the file with next steps clear. For each: under 150 words, professional but human, no legalese. Leave [BRACKETS] for specifics. Also write the subject line for each — subject lines that get opened.

3. The Difficult Client Conversation Script

Help me script a difficult client conversation. Situation: [client has unrealistic expectations about outcome / client is not following your advice / client disputes your invoice / client wants to take a position you advise against / client is hostile or abusive / client wants to settle when you think they should fight / you need to withdraw from representation]. Write: 1) How to open the conversation professionally and directly, 2) The core message without jargon or hedging, 3) How to respond if the client gets emotional or aggressive, 4) How to document the conversation (what to put in the file note), 5) For the "I need to withdraw" scenario: the professional steps to withdraw ethically and the communication to the client. Also write a follow-up email confirming what was discussed.

Legal Writing & Documents

4. The Demand Letter Framework

Help me structure a demand letter for [type of matter: breach of contract / personal injury / debt collection / cease and desist / employment dispute / property damage]. Facts of the case: [describe the key facts — what happened, what the client is owed or seeking, what the other party has or hasn't done]. Legal basis: [the relevant claims or rights at issue — describe without needing to be exhaustive]. What I want: [specific demand — payment of $X by date / cessation of activity / specific performance]. Write a letter structure with: 1) Opening: establish the professional relationship and purpose in the first sentence, 2) Fact section: present the facts in chronological order — factual only, no characterization, 3) Legal basis: state the claims clearly without excessive citation, 4) Demand: specific, with a response deadline, 5) Consequences of non-compliance: stated professionally, not threateningly. Also: what tone strikes the right balance between firm and not burning bridges?

5. The Client FAQ for Your Practice Area

Write an FAQ section for my law firm website. Practice area: [family law / estate planning / business / real estate / criminal defense / employment / personal injury / immigration]. Write answers to the 10 most common questions my potential clients ask — including "How much does it cost?" and "How long will this take?". For each question: 1) A plain-language answer under 100 words, 2) A phrase that acknowledges the answer depends on circumstances (to set expectations without being evasive), 3) A CTA: what should they do next — call, fill out a form, download a guide? Write answers that are genuinely helpful, not just designed to get them to call. Clients who feel informed convert better AND make better clients.

6. The Engagement Letter Plain-Language Summary

Write a plain-language cover email to accompany my engagement letter. What the engagement letter covers: [scope of representation, fee structure, billing practices, retainer terms, communication expectations, file handling]. The problem: clients sign without reading it, then are surprised by things that are in it. Write a cover email that: 1) Explains WHY they're receiving this document, 2) Highlights the 4-5 most important things to know in plain language, 3) Specifically calls out the fee structure and billing cycle (clients most frequently misunderstand this), 4) Tells them what to do if they have questions before signing, 5) Gives a clear signing deadline. Under 250 words. Goal: reduce the "I didn't know that" conversations by getting clients to actually engage with the terms before signing.

Practice Marketing & Business Development

7. The Law Firm Website Rewrite

Rewrite the homepage copy for my law firm website. My practice: [describe — practice areas, types of clients, location]. What makes me different: [specific differentiators — not "aggressive advocacy" but real specifics: years of experience, case results, client service model, specialized knowledge]. Who I serve: [describe your ideal client — not everyone]. Current homepage problem: [too formal / too generic / focused on me not the client / doesn't convert visitors]. Rewrite the homepage with: 1) A headline that speaks to what the client needs — not "experienced legal counsel," but what that means for them, 2) A subheading that explains specifically who you help and how, 3) A "why us" section with 3 concrete differentiators, 4) Social proof: how to present case results, years of experience, or reviews without violating bar rules, 5) A clear CTA: what to do next. Under 400 words. No legal jargon in the copy.

8. The Referral Thank-You System

Build a referral appreciation system for my law practice. Who refers clients to me: [other attorneys / financial advisors / CPAs / former clients / real estate agents / etc.]. Current approach: [nothing formal / thank-you note / occasional lunch]. Build a system that: 1) The thank-you email to send within 24 hours of receiving a referral (before you know if they'll become a client), 2) The update to send the referral source when the referred person becomes a client, 3) The year-end appreciation note to your top 5 referral sources, 4) How to check in with referral sources without seeming transactional, 5) What to offer in return — be careful about fee-splitting rules; flag what's ethically permissible in general terms. Also: the one sentence to say in person when a referral walks in that will make the referral source feel appreciated when they hear it back.

9. The LinkedIn Thought Leadership Plan

Create a 30-day LinkedIn content plan for an attorney building a practice reputation. My practice area: [describe]. Target audience: [potential clients / referral sources / other attorneys]. What I'm comfortable sharing: [legal insights / case studies (anonymized) / opinion pieces / process explanations / personal stories]. Build a plan with: 1) 12 post ideas (3/week), 2) For each: topic, hook (first sentence), and why it would resonate with my target audience, 3) 2 posts specifically targeting referral sources (what do financial advisors or CPAs need to know that I can provide?), 4) How to comment on other professionals' posts to build visibility — what makes a comment worth leaving, 5) The line between educational content and anything that could be considered solicitation in your state. Also write one complete post from the plan as an example.

Practice Operations & Team

10. The New Client Intake System

Design a new client intake process for [practice area]. Current intake problems: [clients call before they're ready / intake takes too long / mismatched expectations / staff inconsistency]. Build an intake process with: 1) Pre-screening: what to put on your website to qualify leads before they call (the right clients self-select, the wrong ones don't call), 2) Initial call script (15-20 minutes): what to ask to determine fit before taking the matter, 3) Conflict check reminder: when and how to communicate this step without losing momentum, 4) Intake questionnaire: 8-10 questions clients complete before the first meeting, 5) Engagement onboarding: the first email after signing that sets the relationship on the right foundation. Goal: less time on unqualified leads, better-prepared clients from day one.

11. The Billing & Collections Communication

Write billing and collections communication templates for a law firm. Create templates for: 1) Monthly invoice cover email (what to say when sending an invoice — not just "please see attached"), 2) 30-day overdue reminder (friendly — assume it was an oversight), 3) 60-day overdue (firmer — clear about consequences without threatening), 4) Response to "your bill seems high" — how to handle billing disputes professionally, 5) End-of-matter billing conversation: how to discuss the final bill when it's larger than estimated. For each: professional tone, under 100 words. Also: what to say when a client calls to dispute a specific line item on the bill — the word-for-word response that defends the work without being defensive.

12. The Associate Training Guide Template

Write a training guide for new associates or paralegals on [task or skill: client communication / billing best practices / drafting standards / file management / deadline tracking / client intake]. What we do: [describe your firm's approach — not generic but how you actually do it]. Common mistakes new hires make: [describe]. Write a guide that: 1) States the standard in the first sentence ("at this firm, we always/never..."), 2) Explains the rationale — why this standard matters to clients and the firm, 3) Gives a specific example of right vs. wrong, 4) Lists the top 3 mistakes to avoid, 5) Tells them who to ask if they're unsure. Format: one page, readable in under 5 minutes.

Strategy & Growth

13. The Practice Area Expansion Brief

Help me evaluate expanding into [new practice area or sub-specialty]. My current practice: [describe]. Why I'm considering this: [client demand / market opportunity / personal interest / revenue diversification]. What I know about this area: [describe your current knowledge level]. Build an evaluation framework covering: 1) Market opportunity: who needs this service and how often, 2) Competitive landscape: who else does this in my market, 3) Investment required: CLE hours, referral network development, marketing, 4) Time to revenue: realistic timeline to first matter, 5) Biggest risk: what could go wrong with this expansion. Give me an honest assessment — not just reasons to do it. End with a recommendation: do it, don't do it, or test it with one matter first.

14. The Client Survey & Feedback System

Build a client satisfaction system for my law firm. When to survey: [after matter closes / at key milestones / annually for ongoing clients]. What I want to learn: [communication quality, value perception, likelihood to refer, what I could do better]. Current approach: [nothing / verbal at the end of matters / occasional email]. Build a system with: 1) A 5-question post-matter survey that clients will actually complete, 2) The email to send with the survey that gets a high response rate, 3) What to do with a negative response — immediate acknowledgment and process, 4) How to use positive feedback: turning it into a review request or testimonial ask, 5) How to share client feedback with your team without making it a performance review. Also: the single question that best predicts whether a client will refer you.

15. The Annual Business Review for Attorneys

Help me review my legal practice for the year. Data: Total revenue: $[X]. Matters opened: [X]. Average matter value: $[X]. Revenue by practice area: [describe]. Best client source: [referrals / website / networking / bar association / other]. Most profitable matter types: [describe]. Most time-consuming matter types relative to fee: [describe]. Run a review that: 1) Calculates my effective hourly rate (total hours worked including non-billable ÷ revenue), 2) Identifies the most profitable client type relative to effort, 3) Recommends one pricing change (rate increase, retainer conversion, minimum matter size), 4) Names the one referral source to invest more in next year, 5) Identifies one type of matter or client to stop taking. Give me a direct assessment — what should change and what should stay the same.
Pro tip: Start with Prompt 1 (Plain-Language Legal Explanation) — it has the highest immediate impact on client satisfaction. Clients who understand what you're doing for them are 3x more likely to refer and significantly less likely to call with status anxiety. In a referral-driven profession, that compounds over years.
