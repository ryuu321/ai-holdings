ChatGPT Prompts for Occupational Therapists

15 free prompts to write treatment plans, patient education materials, and insurance justifications faster — so you can spend more time with patients.

Important: Never enter real patient names, case numbers, or protected health information (PHI) into AI tools. Use role descriptions and anonymized scenarios only. All AI-generated clinical content must be reviewed and adapted by a licensed OT before use. Clinical judgment cannot be delegated to AI.

Occupational therapists spend a disproportionate amount of their day on documentation, justification letters, and caregiver education — time that could go toward patients. These 15 prompts help you write faster without sacrificing quality or accuracy.

1. Treatment Planning

Prompt 1 — Functional Goal Writing

Help me write functional, measurable OT goals for a patient with the following profile. Diagnosis/condition: [general — e.g., CVA with right hemiplegia, pediatric fine motor delay, hand injury post-surgery, cognitive impairment] Functional baseline: [what the patient can currently do — describe in functional terms, not just scores] Patient priorities: [what the patient says they want to be able to do] Caregiver involvement: [who helps, what they can support] Timeframe: [4 weeks / 8 weeks / discharge-focused] Setting: [inpatient / outpatient / home health / school] Write 3 long-term goals and 2 short-term objectives per goal, using functional language the patient would recognize. Include measurable criteria (assist level, repetitions, percentage of trials, etc.).
Pro tip: Goals written in the patient's own language ("return to cooking independently") are more motivating than clinical language ("demonstrate bilateral upper extremity coordination"). Use both — clinical language for the chart, patient language for the session.

Prompt 2 — Evaluation Summary

Help me write an OT evaluation summary. Referral reason: [general — what the referral source stated] Occupational profile: [patient's roles, routines, environment — no identifying detail] Standardized assessments used: [list — e.g., FIM, AMPS, Barthel, COPM, PDMS-2] Assessment results: [general findings — functional level, areas of deficit, areas of strength] Participation restrictions: [ADLs, work, school, leisure affected] Clinical impression: [your overall clinical reasoning] Recommended frequency and duration: [e.g., 3x/week for 6 weeks] Write a structured OT evaluation summary in SOAP or narrative format. Include clinical reasoning linking assessment findings to participation restrictions and the rationale for your recommended frequency.

Prompt 3 — Intervention Plan

Help me design a treatment intervention plan for a specific OT case. Population: [adult rehab / pediatric / mental health / hand therapy / community-based] Primary deficit area: [ADL performance / fine motor / cognitive / upper extremity / sensory processing / work rehabilitation] Evidence-based approaches applicable: [list what you know — e.g., CIMT, task-oriented training, sensory integration, occupation-based practice] Session length available: [30 / 45 / 60 minutes] Number of sessions: [total expected] Barriers to progress: [patient motivation, insurance limits, home environment, caregiver capacity] Write a session-by-session progression outline for the first 4 weeks, including activity ideas, grading strategies, and how to measure progress at each phase.
Pro tip: Always anchor your intervention to a meaningful occupation. "Practicing grip" is less motivating than "practicing grip so you can open your medication bottles again."

2. Insurance and Justification

Prompt 4 — Prior Authorization Letter

Help me write an OT prior authorization letter to a payer. Diagnosis: [general category] Requested services: [frequency, duration, type] Functional deficits justifying skilled OT: [describe in functional terms — what the patient cannot do that requires skilled intervention] Why this cannot be delegated to a COTA or caregiver: [clinical complexity requiring OT-level skill] Risk if services are denied: [functional consequences — falls, hospitalization, inability to return to work, caregiver breakdown] Progress to date (if continuing auth): [what has changed since the last auth period] Write a compelling, evidence-based prior authorization letter in professional format. Focus on medical necessity and functional impact — not therapy jargon.

Prompt 5 — Discharge Summary

Help me write an OT discharge summary. Patient role: [adult / pediatric — no identifying detail] Initial functional status: [what the patient could do at evaluation] Goals set: [list the goals from the treatment plan] Goals met: [which goals were achieved and to what level] Remaining deficits: [what was not resolved] Discharge disposition: [home with caregiver / home alone / SNF / assisted living / return to school] Home program provided: [general description] Follow-up recommendations: [outpatient / home health / reassessment in X months / community resources] Write a complete discharge summary with clinical reasoning for the discharge decision and clear handoff instructions for the next care provider.

Prompt 6 — Medical Necessity Statement

Help me write a medical necessity statement for OT services to appeal a payer denial. Denial reason stated: [what the payer said] Why the denial is clinically incorrect: [your professional reasoning] Functional deficits that require skilled intervention: [specific — not just "patient needs therapy"] Evidence base for the intervention: [any clinical guidelines, research, or professional standards that support the approach] Risk of non-treatment: [clinical consequences] Write a 300–400 word medical necessity appeal statement. Use clinical language that demonstrates skilled OT reasoning, not just describing what the patient can't do.
Pro tip: Payers deny claims when the documentation sounds like maintenance therapy. Your language must show why OT-level skill is required at this moment — not just that the patient has functional limitations.

3. Patient and Caregiver Education

Prompt 7 — Home Exercise Program

Help me write a home exercise program (HEP) for a patient. Diagnosis/condition: [general] Goals of the HEP: [link to functional goals — e.g., improve grip strength for cooking, improve shoulder ROM for dressing] Exercises to include: [list the activities or exercises] Frequency and repetitions: [sets, reps, times per day] Patient literacy level: [simple language / standard / can handle diagrams] Caregiver involvement needed: [none / some / extensive] Write the HEP in patient-friendly language. Include: exercise name, clear written instructions without jargon, precautions, how to know if they are doing it correctly, and when to stop and contact their OT.

Prompt 8 — Caregiver Training Script

Help me write a caregiver training script for a specific skill. Skill to teach: [e.g., safe transfers, cueing for ADLs, hand-over-hand assistance, splint application, compensatory strategies] Caregiver relationship: [spouse / adult child / paid caregiver / parent of child patient] Patient's level of participation in the task: [fully dependent / needs cues / can do parts independently] Key safety points: [what could go wrong if done incorrectly] Common mistakes caregivers make: [what you see most often] Write a training script the caregiver can follow step-by-step. Include: how to prepare the environment, step-by-step instructions, what to say to the patient during the task, safety checkpoints, and what to do if the patient resists or the task goes wrong.

Prompt 9 — Patient Education Handout

Create a patient education handout on the following topic. Topic: [e.g., joint protection principles for arthritis, energy conservation, sensory diet strategies, adaptive equipment for one-handed cooking, sleep hygiene for pain management] Reading level: [6th grade / 8th grade / adult professional] Format preference: [bullet points / short paragraphs / numbered steps] Key points to include: [list 4–5 must-include concepts] What to avoid: [clinical jargon the patient won't understand] Write a one-page patient handout with a clear header, 4–6 main points, one specific example for each point, and a section for patient questions at the end.
Pro tip: Always health literacy check your patient education materials. If your handout needs a dictionary, your patient won't read it — they'll nod and put it in a drawer.

4. Professional Communication

Prompt 10 — Referral to Another Provider

Help me write a referral letter to another healthcare provider. Who I'm referring to: [physician / PT / SLP / psychologist / social worker / dietitian / specialist] Reason for referral: [what I observed that falls outside OT scope or requires collaboration] What I've already addressed in OT: [to avoid duplication] What I need the provider to evaluate or address: [specific ask] Urgency: [routine / within 2 weeks / urgent] Write a professional, concise referral letter. One paragraph per section: what the patient's situation is, what I've done, what I'm asking for, and what follow-up communication I'd like.

Prompt 11 — Team Communication After Evaluation

Help me communicate OT evaluation findings to an interdisciplinary team. Team members present: [physician / nursing / PT / SLP / social work / case manager / family] Key findings in plain language: [what the patient can and cannot do — avoid OT jargon that non-OTs won't understand] OT's recommended role in the care plan: [what OT will address vs. defer to others] Discharge planning implications: [what OT findings mean for the patient's next setting] What I need from the team: [decisions, information, coordination] Write a 3–4 minute verbal team communication summary. Include a brief patient occupational profile, key functional deficits, OT's plan, and one clear ask of the team.

Prompt 12 — OT Student Supervision Feedback

Help me write structured feedback for an OT student I'm supervising. Student level: [Level I / Level II / first rotation / final rotation] Observation: [describe what happened — a session, documentation, clinical reasoning discussion — no patient detail] What the student did well: [specific behaviors] Area for development: [specific gap — not a personal judgment] How I want them to approach it differently: [concrete behavioral change] Write feedback in the "situation-behavior-impact" format. Be specific enough that the student knows exactly what to do differently, but frame it in a way that preserves their confidence and learning mindset.
Pro tip: Vague feedback ("you need to be more confident") teaches nothing. Specific feedback ("in your next session, try stating your clinical reasoning out loud before choosing an activity — here's what that sounds like...") builds skills.

5. Professional Development

Prompt 13 — CEU Learning Plan

Help me build a continuing education plan for the next 12 months. Current specialty: [pediatrics / adult rehab / hand therapy / mental health / school-based / community] Areas I want to develop: [list 2–3 specific skills or specializations] Areas I'm required to complete (state/employer): [mandatory topics] Time available for learning: [hours per month] Learning preferences: [online courses / live conferences / journal reading / mentorship / hands-on training] Create a 12-month CE plan: what to prioritize first, why, what specific certification or course types to look for, how to integrate learning into a busy clinical schedule, and how to demonstrate new skills to employers or payers.

Prompt 14 — OT Advocacy Writing

Help me write an advocacy piece for OT services. Audience: [hospital administration / insurance payer / school district / community organization / state legislature / the public] What I'm advocating for: [OT coverage / program funding / scope of practice / new OT position / patient access] The core argument: [why this matters in terms of patient outcomes and cost] Data or outcomes I can reference: [any numbers — even general research findings] What I want the reader to do: [approve / fund / reconsider / learn more] Write a 300–500 word advocacy letter or op-ed. Lead with a patient impact story (hypothetical/anonymized), build to the evidence, and close with a specific ask.

Prompt 15 — Specialty Certification Study Plan

Help me prepare for an OT specialty certification exam. Certification target: [CHT / BCPR / SCLV / SCSS / FAOTA / other specialty] My current knowledge level: [strong in some areas, weak in others — list] Time until exam: [months] Study time available: [hours per week] Preferred study methods: [practice questions / reading / study groups / mnemonics / clinical case review] Write a week-by-week study schedule for the first 8 weeks, prioritized by highest-weight exam domains. Include what to study, how to study it, how to track my readiness, and how to manage exam anxiety in the final 2 weeks.
