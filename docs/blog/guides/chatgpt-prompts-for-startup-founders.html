ChatGPT Prompts for Startup Founders

15 free prompts to write better pitch decks, investor communications, and customer outreach when every hour counts.

Startup founders are some of the most overextended writers in the world — pitch decks, investor updates, customer emails, job postings, press releases, and product copy all land on one person's plate. These 15 prompts help you communicate with the precision and confidence that converts — whether you are pitching investors or onboarding your first 100 customers.

1. Fundraising

Prompt 1 — Pitch Deck Narrative

Help me write the narrative for my startup pitch deck. Company: [one sentence what you do] Problem: [the specific pain point you solve — with concrete evidence of why it is real] Solution: [what you built and why it is the right approach] Market: [TAM/SAM/SOM — or if early, how you are thinking about market size] Traction: [what you have — revenue / users / LOIs / pilots / growth rate] Team: [why you and your team are the right people for this] Ask: [how much / what you are using it for] Write: Slide-by-slide narrative for a 10-slide deck. For each slide: the headline (one statement, not a question or topic label) → 3 bullet points of what the slide should convey → what "great" looks like for this slide vs. what kills deals on it. Include: how to open the pitch (the first 60 seconds before the deck) and how to close (the last 2 minutes). A pitch is not a presentation — it is a story with a specific ending.
Pro tip: Investors see 500 decks a year. Your problem slide needs to make them feel the pain, not understand it. Show the cost of the problem, not just the existence of it.

Prompt 2 — Investor Outreach Email

Write cold outreach emails to investors. My startup: [1-sentence description] Stage: [pre-seed / seed / Series A] Traction: [the most compelling metric or milestone I have] Why this investor specifically: [their thesis, portfolio companies relevant to me, something they have written] Warm connection (if any): [mutual contact / they follow me / I met them at X event] What I am asking for: [30-minute call / intro call / to send deck] Write: (1) Cold email version (under 150 words — lead with the specific reason I am reaching out to them, not a generic intro), (2) LinkedIn message version (under 200 words), (3) Warm intro request to a mutual connection (how to ask someone to make the intro — brief, gives them everything they need to forward), (4) Follow-up after no response at 1 week and 2 weeks. No pitch in the first message — get the meeting first.

Prompt 3 — Investor Update Email

Write a monthly investor update email. Company: [name] Month: [current month] Key metrics this month: [revenue / ARR / MRR / users / growth rate / runway — whatever is most relevant] Progress vs. last month: [up / down / flat — with context] Key wins: [list 2–3] Key challenges / what I am working through: [be honest — investors who know problems early can help] What I need from investors: [intros / advice on X / nothing this month] Next milestone: [what comes next and when] Write: Subject line (metrics-forward — e.g., "$42K MRR | +18% MoM | May Update") → Metrics snapshot → Wins (short, specific) → Challenges (honest, with your plan) → Asks → What is next. Under 300 words. Consistent monthly updates build more investor trust than irregular ones with better news.
Pro tip: The founders who share bad news in investor updates get more help than founders who go silent when things get hard. Investors can only help with problems they know about.

2. Customer Growth

Prompt 4 — Landing Page Copy

Write landing page copy for my startup. Product: [what it does in plain language] Target customer: [who it is for — specific, not everyone] Their biggest pain point: [the thing that keeps them up at night that this solves] What makes this different from alternatives: [including "do nothing" and "build your own"] Social proof available: [testimonials / number of customers / logos / specific results] CTA goal: [sign up / book a demo / start free trial / join waitlist] Write: (1) Hero section — headline (under 10 words, leads with outcome not feature), sub-headline (one sentence on why this works), CTA button text, (2) Problem section (agitate the pain before presenting the solution), (3) How it works (3-step simplification), (4) Social proof section (how to present what you have), (5) FAQ section (6 questions that reduce friction), (6) Final CTA section. Also: mobile-first considerations — what gets cut if only 50% of the page is visible.

Prompt 5 — Customer Discovery Interview Guide

Build a customer discovery interview guide for my startup. Stage: [idea / pre-product / post-launch / scaling] What I am trying to learn: [whether the problem is real / whether my solution is right / why users churn / what drives conversion / what the ICP really is] Customer type: [B2B buyer / end user / prosumer / consumer] My current hypothesis: [what I believe is true that I want to test] Create: (1) 15 open-ended interview questions organized by theme (background → current behavior → problem → attempted solutions → reaction to my approach), (2) Questions to absolutely avoid (leading questions that confirm your hypothesis), (3) How to handle "your product sounds great" (it means nothing — here is how to dig deeper), (4) How to end every interview with a referral ask, (5) Note-taking template to capture signal vs. noise, (6) How to synthesize 10 interviews into actionable insights. Discovery interviews are the highest-ROI 30 minutes a founder can spend.

3. Operations & Team

Prompt 6 — Job Posting for Early Hire

Write a job posting for my startup's first or second hire. Role: [title and description] Stage: [pre-seed / seed / Series A] What this person will actually do: [day-to-day specifics — not a generic list] What makes this opportunity compelling: [the real reasons a great candidate should join a startup at this stage] What I am not offering: [probably a high salary — how to be honest about the tradeoffs] Who thrives vs. who struggles in early-stage roles: [the honest profile] Write: (1) Job posting that attracts the right people and repels the wrong ones, (2) The section most job postings get wrong — how to describe the startup-stage opportunity honestly without underselling or overselling, (3) Salary / equity framing (how to talk about compensation when you are pre-revenue or early), (4) How to write the application question that tells you immediately if someone is right, (5) Where to post this beyond LinkedIn for early-stage startup hires.

Prompt 7 — Advisor Outreach and Agreement

Help me identify, pitch, and structure advisor relationships for my startup. My startup: [description and stage] What I need advisors for: [specific gaps — enterprise sales / specific market / regulatory / product / fundraising / specific network] Target advisors: [the profile of who would be most valuable] What I can offer: [equity / access to my network / co-investment rights / just a great problem to work on] Write: (1) Outreach message to a target advisor (personalized, specific about why I want them, specific ask), (2) What to cover in a first advisor conversation (what you need, how you would work together, what success looks like), (3) Simple advisor agreement terms to propose (SAFE note / equity cliff / time commitment expectations), (4) How to tell if an advisor is actually adding value vs. just having the title, (5) How to gracefully end an advisor relationship that is not working.
Pro tip: The best advisor outreach explains specifically what you need from them — not just "advice." "I need someone who has sold to mid-market HR teams" wins more advisor relationships than "looking for mentors."

4. Press & Visibility

Prompt 8 — Press Release and Media Outreach

Write press outreach for my startup announcement. What I am announcing: [funding round / product launch / major partnership / milestone / new market entry] The real news: [what makes this genuinely interesting — not "we are excited to announce"] Proof of relevance: [why does this matter to readers who do not know us] Target outlets: [tech press / industry press / local business / specific beat journalists] Write: (1) Press release (300 words — inverted pyramid: news → context → quotes → boilerplate), (2) Pitch email to a journalist (under 150 words — lead with why this is interesting to their readers, not to you), (3) Subject line options (3 variations — one data-forward, one narrative, one question), (4) How to follow up without being annoying, (5) What makes a startup story actually newsworthy vs. what founders think is newsworthy. PR is not about getting coverage — it is about giving journalists a story their readers will share.

Prompt 9 — Founder Personal Brand Content

Build a 30-day LinkedIn content plan for a startup founder. My startup: [describe] My personal angle: [what I have been through / what I believe about this market / what I have learned building this] Target audience: [potential customers / investors / talent / other founders] Content goal: [customer pipeline / investor visibility / talent attraction / thought leadership] 30 post ideas: For each — format (post / carousel / short video script), topic/angle, hook sentence. Mix: founder lessons learned → market insights → behind-the-scenes building → customer stories (with permission) → data or research in your space → contrarian takes → milestone announcements → failures and what they taught me. 70% authentic founder experience, 30% market/product insight. No promotional posts that lead with "buy our product."

5. Customer Communication

Prompt 10 — Customer Onboarding Sequence

Write a customer onboarding email sequence for my product. Product: [describe] Customer type: [B2B / consumer / prosumer] What "activated" means: [the action that proves they have gotten value — used the core feature / connected first integration / invited a teammate / completed first project] Common reasons customers do not activate: [list 3 — confusion / overwhelm / forgot / competing priorities] Tone: [warm and helpful / professional / conversational] Write a 5-email onboarding sequence: Email 1 (day 0 — immediately after sign-up): welcome + single most important next step + why it matters → Email 2 (day 1): how to get the first win (whatever activation looks like) → Email 3 (day 3): the feature or use case they probably have not discovered yet → Email 4 (day 7): social proof — what other customers are doing with this → Email 5 (day 14, if not activated): "are we a fit?" check-in — honest, not passive-aggressive. Each email under 200 words.

Prompt 11 — Churn Interview Script

Write a script for interviewing customers who cancelled or stopped using my product. Product: [describe] Typical reason I hear for cancellation: [too expensive / not using it / found something better / no longer need it] What I actually need to understand: [whether the problem is product / positioning / onboarding / customer fit / pricing] Tone: [curious and non-defensive — this is the most important] Write: (1) Outreach message to a churned customer (genuine, not scripted — ask for 15 minutes, explain what I am trying to learn), (2) Interview guide (10 questions — start with their situation, not your product), (3) Questions that often reveal the real reason (not the stated reason) for cancellation, (4) How to avoid getting defensive when they criticize the product, (5) How to synthesize 10 churn interviews into a product or positioning action. Every churned customer is a lesson — most founders never ask.
Pro tip: Never ask "why did you cancel?" first. Ask "walk me through how you were using the product." The usage story reveals the real reason long before they give you the official one.

6. Metrics & Strategy

Prompt 12 — Board or Advisor Update Memo

Write a monthly board or advisor memo. Company: [name] Period: [month/quarter] Key metrics: [revenue / ARR / burn / runway / headcount / key growth metrics] Strategic progress: [what major initiatives moved forward] What is working: [specific, with evidence] What is not working: [honest — what is not going as planned and your current thinking] Upcoming decisions: [what you need input or approval on] Asks: [specific — intros / expertise / vote] Write: Executive summary (5 sentences — the most important things to know) → Metrics (with context vs. prior period and vs. plan) → Wins → Challenges and current plan → Upcoming decisions → Asks → Appendix structure (detailed metrics). Under 500 words for the main body. Board memos that name problems early build more confidence than memos that only share wins.

7. Resilience & Growth

Prompt 13 — Difficult Stakeholder Communication

Help me communicate difficult news to stakeholders. Situation: [missed a milestone / major customer churned / key team member leaving / pivot announcement / running out of runway / missed fundraise / product delay] Audience: [investors / team / customers / co-founder / board] What I need them to know: [the full honest picture] What I want them to do: [stay the course / help / make a decision / give time] Write: (1) The opening sentence that does not bury the lead — name the situation directly, (2) Context that helps them understand without making excuses, (3) What you are doing about it (specific plan, not vague reassurance), (4) What you need from them, (5) How to close so they feel informed but not panicked. Difficult news delivered with honesty and a plan is always better received than difficult news discovered later.

Prompt 14 — Co-Founder Alignment Framework

Help me build a co-founder alignment document or run a co-founder alignment conversation. What I want to align on: [roles / decision-making authority / equity and vesting / what we will and will not do / exit philosophy / conflict resolution / what happens if one of us wants to leave] Create: (1) 20 questions every co-founding team should answer before building (the ones that kill companies when left unresolved), (2) How to have the equity conversation without damaging the relationship, (3) Decision-making framework — who has final say on what (engineering decisions vs. hiring vs. product direction vs. fundraising), (4) How to run a quarterly co-founder alignment check-in (30 minutes, structured), (5) What to do when you fundamentally disagree on something strategic. Co-founder conflicts are the number one startup killer — they are also almost entirely predictable.

Prompt 15 — Quarterly Founder Review

Help me run a quarterly personal and business review as a founder. Quarter: [Q1/Q2/Q3/Q4, year] Key metrics vs. last quarter: [growth / revenue / burn / team size] What I said I would do this quarter: [list your Q-start priorities] What actually happened: [honest recap] Where I spent time vs. where I should have spent time: [time allocation honesty] Personal state: [energy level / mental health / relationship with building right now] Review: (1) Wins to acknowledge (founders are bad at this — you need to do it deliberately), (2) Where strategy diverged from execution — was that right or wrong, (3) The thing that consumed time but generated little value — what to stop, (4) The constraint most limiting the company right now — is it capital / team / product / distribution / founder clarity, (5) The single most important thing to focus on next quarter — and what that means I should deprioritize. Quarterly reviews are how founders stay ahead of their company instead of behind it.
