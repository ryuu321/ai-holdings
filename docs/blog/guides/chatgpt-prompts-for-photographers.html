15 ChatGPT Prompts for Photographers

The photos are the hard part — and you've got that. Use AI to handle the writing, pricing, and business tasks so you spend more time shooting and less time at your inbox.

Photography is one of the most business-intensive creative fields. Between client emails, contracts, gallery delivery, invoicing, and marketing, the administrative work can easily take as much time as the actual shooting and editing. These prompts help you reclaim that time.

These work for wedding photographers, portrait photographers, commercial photographers, real estate photographers, newborn photographers, and any photographer running a solo or small business. Use them in ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini.

Client Communication

1. The Inquiry Response Template

How you respond to the first inquiry sets the tone for the entire client relationship — and determines whether they book or keep looking.

Write a response template for a new photography inquiry. My photography type: [wedding / portrait / commercial / newborn / real estate / etc.]. Inquiry details: [paste the inquiry or describe — what they asked, their event date, their budget if mentioned]. Write a response that: 1) Acknowledges specifically what they mentioned in their inquiry (shows I read it), 2) Confirms availability and gives a clear next step, 3) Asks 2-3 qualifying questions that help me understand if we're a good fit, 4) Provides just enough information about pricing to pre-qualify without overwhelming, 5) Ends with a clear CTA (schedule a call, view portfolio, etc.). Under 200 words. Warm and professional — not a form letter, not overly formal.

2. The Booking Confirmation & Prep Email

Write a booking confirmation email that gets clients excited and prepares them for a great session. Session details: [type of session, date, location, duration]. What clients typically need to know: [what to wear, what to bring, parking, timeline, when they'll receive photos]. Write an email that: 1) Leads with genuine excitement about their session, 2) Provides all logistics clearly (no buried information), 3) Includes one "what to expect" section that reduces anxiety, 4) Gives 3 specific tips for getting the best photos (tailored to this session type), 5) States what happens next and when. Also write a shorter reminder email to send 48 hours before the session (under 100 words).

3. The Gallery Delivery Email

Write a gallery delivery email that turns photo delivery into a memorable experience. My style: [describe your photography style briefly]. Session: [describe what the session was — wedding, family portrait, newborn, etc.]. How long they waited: [delivery time]. Write an email that: 1) Opens with a sentence that builds anticipation before giving the link, 2) Shares one thing you specifically love about their gallery, 3) Explains how to download, share, and print (don't assume they know), 4) States the gallery expiration date clearly, 5) Includes a soft ask for a review or referral — after they've just seen their photos is the best moment. Also write a follow-up email to send 3 days later if they haven't opened the gallery.

Pricing & Packages

4. The Pricing Guide Copy

Write copy for my photography pricing guide. My packages: [describe each package — what's included, price, typical use case]. My style: [describe briefly]. Target clients: [who you photograph best]. What makes me different: [1-3 genuine differentiators]. Write pricing guide copy that: 1) Opens with the experience clients are buying, not the deliverables (you're not selling "300 edited images" — what are you really selling?), 2) Presents each package with a name, price, what's included, and who it's for, 3) Addresses the "why does photography cost this much?" question directly, 4) Includes a FAQ for the 3 questions that slow down bookings most, 5) Ends with a clear CTA. Tone: confident, not apologetic about pricing. Under 600 words total.

5. The Price Increase Announcement

Write an email announcing a price increase to past clients and leads in my pipeline. Current pricing: $[X] for [service]. New pricing: $[Y] effective [date]. Reason: [be honest — investment in equipment, rising costs, demand, haven't raised prices in X years]. Write: 1) An email to past clients announcing the change — includes a "book now at current rates before [date]" offer, 2) A response to "why are you raising your prices?" that's honest and confident, 3) Updated language for my website about pricing. Also write a version of the announcement for social media (under 150 words, Instagram-appropriate). Don't over-apologize — communicate the value confidently.

6. The Upsell Email Sequence

Write an upsell email sequence for clients who've booked a basic package. Package they booked: [describe]. Upsell options I offer: [list add-ons — extra coverage time, album, prints, engagement session, second shooter, etc.]. Write: Email 1 (sent 2 months before session): Introduce the album or main add-on — focus on why they'll regret not having it later. Email 2 (sent 1 month before): Second add-on option with social proof from a past client who added it. Email 3 (sent 1 week before): Final reminder — what they can still add before the session. Each email: under 150 words, specific not generic, includes a real example or story where possible.

Portfolio & Website

7. The Portfolio Gallery Description

Write descriptions for [3-5] portfolio galleries on my photography website. Gallery 1: [describe — type of session, setting, mood, what makes it special]. Gallery 2: [describe]. Gallery 3: [describe]. For each gallery write: 1) A headline that captures the emotion, not just the category (not "Wedding Gallery" — something that makes visitors want to click), 2) A 2-3 sentence description that describes the experience of viewing this gallery, 3) A sentence about the clients or context (if appropriate), 4) A CTA. Also write my main portfolio page intro (50 words): what my photography feels like, who I photograph, what they walk away with. No clichés like "capturing memories" or "timeless moments."

8. The About Page Rewrite

Rewrite my photography website About page. Current version (or description of me): [paste or describe — your background, style, what you love about photography, personal details you're comfortable sharing]. The problem with most about pages: they're about the photographer, not the client. Write an About page that: 1) Opens with the client and what they're looking for (not "My name is X and I love photography"), 2) Describes my approach in a way that tells clients what it's like to work with me, 3) Includes 2-3 personal details that make me human without being irrelevant, 4) States clearly who I photograph best and why (not everyone), 5) Ends with an invitation to connect. Under 350 words. First-person, warm, specific.

9. The SEO Blog Post Plan

Create a 6-month blog content plan to rank my photography website locally. My location: [city/region]. Specialization: [wedding / portrait / commercial / etc.]. Build a plan with: 1) 12 blog post titles targeting local search terms (e.g., "Best [city] wedding venues for photography"), 2) For each post: target keyword, what the article should cover, why someone would search for it, 3) 3 posts that would attract vendor relationships (venues, wedding planners, florists who might refer clients), 4) The first post to publish — which has the highest chance of ranking quickly? Each post should serve a reader who is actively planning and might need a photographer. Not "5 photography tips for beginners."

Marketing & Social Media

10. The Instagram Caption Writer

Write 5 Instagram captions for [types of photos I'm posting this week: wedding details / couple portraits / newborn session / behind the scenes / etc.]. My brand voice: [describe — romantic and poetic / warm and personal / clean and minimal / fun and candid]. Target audience: [who follows you and who you want to attract]. For each caption: 1) An opening line that stops the scroll — not "I love this couple" but something unexpected or emotionally resonant, 2) Body that adds context without over-explaining the photo, 3) A question or CTA to drive engagement, 4) Relevant hashtag sets (mix niche + local + community). Also: which of these 5 would you A/B test as a Reel cover text? Explain briefly.

11. The Venue Partnership Pitch

Write an email to [venue type: wedding venue / portrait studio / hotel / event space] proposing a referral partnership. My photography: [describe your style and ideal clients]. Why this venue: [what specifically attracted you to them — be specific, show you've looked at their work]. What I'm proposing: [preferred vendor listing / cross-referrals / styled shoot collaboration / social media features]. Write an email that: 1) References something specific about their venue (shows research), 2) Proposes a clear mutual benefit — what's in it for them, not just me, 3) Makes a specific low-commitment ask (not "let's partner" but "would you be open to a 20-minute call?"), 4) Attaches or links to my portfolio as evidence. Under 150 words.

12. The Referral Ask Script

Write a referral request system for past photography clients. Timing: [right after gallery delivery / 6 months after session / at the 1-year anniversary]. Write: 1) Email version — thanks them for being a client, asks for a referral in a way that feels natural not transactional, 2) Text version (under 60 words — for clients I have that kind of relationship with), 3) What to offer as a referral incentive — make it specific to photography (print credit, discount on next session, etc.), 4) How to make the ask in person when you see them, 5) Follow-up message if they say "I'll pass your name along" — how to make it easy to actually follow through. Most referrals die because clients don't know what to say — fix that.

Business Operations

13. The Contract Explainer Email

Write an email to send with my photography contract that makes clients actually read it. Key points in my contract: [list the 5-7 most important clauses — cancellation policy, delivery timeline, usage rights, weather policy, payment terms]. Write an email that: 1) Frames the contract as protecting both parties (not just CYA for me), 2) Highlights the 3-4 most important things to know in plain language, 3) Answers the most common question clients ask about the contract, 4) Tells them exactly how to sign and return, 5) Reassures them that questions are welcome. Goal: reduce the "this contract seems really strict" responses and speed up the signing process. Under 250 words.

14. The Bad Weather Contingency Script

Write communication templates for weather-related issues. My photography type: [outdoor wedding / outdoor portrait / real estate / etc.]. Scenarios I need templates for: 1) Email to client the day before a shoot when weather looks bad — do we proceed or reschedule?, 2) Morning-of message when the weather is borderline, 3) Rescheduling confirmation email after a weather cancellation, 4) Refund/credit policy explanation if rescheduling isn't possible. Write each template with a calm, solution-focused tone. Clients in weather situations are often anxious — the tone of your communication affects how they remember the experience, even if the shoot gets canceled.

15. The Annual Business Review Template

Help me review my photography business year. Data I have: Total revenue: $[X]. Sessions booked: [X]. Average booking value: $[X]. Most popular package: [describe]. Referral source breakdown: [where did clients come from?]. Highest-paying sessions: [describe]. Lowest-converting inquiries: [what types of leads rarely book?]. Run a business review that: 1) Calculates my effective hourly rate (help me think through the math), 2) Identifies which sessions or clients are most profitable relative to time, 3) Recommends one pricing change for next year, 4) Names the single marketing activity that generated the most revenue (so I do more of it), 5) Tells me one thing I should stop offering or doing. Give me a direct assessment, not a list of things to "consider."
Pro tip: Start with Prompt 1 (Inquiry Response Template) — it pays off immediately. Most photographers lose bookings at the inquiry stage, not because of their pricing or portfolio. A response that arrives quickly, feels personal, and has a clear next step converts at 2-3x the rate of a generic reply.
