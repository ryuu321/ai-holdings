15 ChatGPT Prompts for Event Planners

You're managing vendors, clients, timelines, and crises — all at once. Use AI to handle the writing and communication so you can focus on creating events people remember.

Event planning is one of the most detail-intensive businesses in the world. Between proposals, contracts, vendor emails, client updates, day-of timelines, and post-event follow-ups, the administrative writing alone can consume hours that should go toward creative planning. These prompts help you reclaim that time.

These work for wedding planners, corporate event planners, social event coordinators, conference producers, party planners, and any event professional running a solo or small business. Use them in ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini.

Client Proposals & Pitching

1. The Event Proposal Template

Your proposal is often the first real work product the client sees. It should feel like the event itself — thoughtful, detailed, and exciting.

Write an event planning proposal for [event type: wedding / corporate conference / birthday / gala / product launch / team offsite / etc.]. Client details: [what they shared — guest count, date, venue preference, budget if known, style/vibe they described]. My services: [describe what's included — full planning / day-of coordination / partial planning / specific services]. My differentiators: [what makes my approach or results different]. Write a proposal that: 1) Opens with a paragraph that captures the vision they described back to them — shows I listened and understood, 2) Presents my services in packages if applicable (good / better / best), 3) Includes a tentative timeline showing my process from now to event day, 4) Addresses the budget professionally — how I help them get the most value, 5) Ends with a clear next step and deadline. Also write a shorter "quick quote" email version for clients who just want a number fast.

2. The Discovery Meeting Script

Write a discovery meeting script for an initial client consultation. Event type: [describe what type of events you plan]. What I need to learn: [budget / guest count / vision / timeline / decision makers / past events / what's most important to them]. Write a script that: 1) Opens with a question that gets them talking about the experience they want — not the logistics, 2) Uncovers budget range without asking "what's your budget?" directly (the question that makes clients low-ball), 3) Identifies who the real decision-makers are (especially for weddings and corporate events), 4) Surfaces potential challenges early — venue limitations, complicated families, budget/expectation gaps, 5) Closes with a clear next step — not "I'll send something over" but a specific timeline and action. Also write 3 follow-up questions for when a client seems excited but vague.

3. The Venue Pitch Email

Write an email to [venue type: hotel / outdoor space / restaurant / museum / event center / private estate] to inquire about holding a client event. Event details: [event type, estimated guest count, date or date range, budget range if appropriate to share, any special requirements]. What I need from them: [availability, pricing, site visit, catering options, capacity, etc.]. Write an email that: 1) Is professional and specific — not a generic inquiry they can tell was sent to 20 venues, 2) Mentions one specific thing I know about their venue that makes it a fit for this event, 3) Asks the 3-4 most important questions to qualify them quickly, 4) Makes it easy to respond — not a 15-question email, 5) Includes my contact and availability for a site visit. Under 200 words. Also write a follow-up email if I don't hear back in 5 days.

Client Communication

4. The Client Update Email System

Write a client update email system for event planning. Create templates for: 1) Monthly planning update (for events 3+ months out): what's been done, what's next, any decisions needed from the client, 2) 60-days-out check-in: final vendor confirmations needed, guest count due date, timeline draft review, 3) 2-week countdown email: final details, payments, day-of logistics, what to have ready, 4) Day-before message: weather plan if applicable, parking and arrival info, timeline reminder, your contact number, 5) Post-event follow-up (sent 1 week after): how it went, photo sharing info, testimonial ask. For each: under 200 words, warm and excited (not bureaucratic), organized so the client knows exactly what needs their attention vs. what I'm handling.

5. The Difficult Client Scenario Scripts

Help me handle these difficult event planning client situations: Scenario 1: Client wants to add elements outside the budget. Scenario 2: Client changes the guest count significantly after vendor contracts are signed. Scenario 3: Client is unresponsive and the deadline for a vendor decision is passing. Scenario 4: Client is unhappy with a vendor I selected. Scenario 5: Client disputes your invoice after the event. For each scenario write: 1) How to open the conversation or email — direct but not combative, 2) The core message — what needs to be communicated clearly, 3) What you're offering (if anything) and what you're not, 4) How to end with the relationship intact (if possible), 5) What to document. Word-for-word language where possible — not advice about what to say.

6. The Day-of Event Timeline Template

Create a day-of event timeline template for [event type: wedding / corporate conference / gala / birthday party]. Event duration: [X hours]. Guest count: [X]. Venue setup time: [X hours before guests arrive]. Key moments: [describe the major elements — ceremony, speeches, dinner service, entertainment, etc.]. Build a timeline with: 1) Setup timeline (venue + vendor arrival, setup completion, final walkthrough), 2) Guest arrival and pre-event, 3) Main event flow — timed to the minute for key transitions, 4) Buffer time built in at critical moments (not everything runs on time), 5) Breakdown and vendor departure. Also: write the version I share with vendors (includes their specific calls), and the version I share with the client (big picture, no operational minutiae). What time gaps do event planners consistently underestimate?

Vendor Management

7. The Vendor Negotiation Script

Write a negotiation script for vendor contracts. Vendor type: [catering / photography / florist / venue / entertainment / AV / transportation]. What I'm negotiating: [price reduction / additional services / flexible payment terms / cancellation policy / exclusivity waiver]. My leverage: [booking multiple dates / referral relationship / off-peak date / larger event than they typically do]. Write a negotiation script that: 1) Opens by establishing the relationship — this is a long-term partnership conversation, not a one-time transaction, 2) States the specific ask with a reasonable justification, 3) Handles "we can't do that" — the counter-offer that still gets something, 4) Handles "our pricing is firm" — what to ask for instead (upgrades, flexibility, payment terms), 5) Closes either way without burning the relationship. Also: what event planners consistently fail to negotiate that they could?

8. The Vendor Reference Check System

Write a vendor reference check system for vetting new vendors. Vendor type: [describe]. What I need to verify: [reliability / quality / communication / problem-solving / pricing accuracy / how they handle day-of issues]. Build a system with: 1) The email to send when requesting a reference — what to ask for, 2) 7 specific questions to ask references — not "would you recommend them?" but questions that reveal actual performance, 3) What a reference's hesitation or vague answer signals (what to read between the lines), 4) Red flags that should make you pause even if references are technically positive, 5) How to document vendor performance over time so I can make better decisions next year. Also: what should I always ask that most event planners forget to ask when checking references?

9. The Preferred Vendor List & Pitch

Help me build and pitch my preferred vendor network. My event specialty: [describe — wedding / corporate / social]. Vendors I want to include: [list categories — photographers, caterers, florists, DJs, venues, etc.]. What I offer vendors who work with me regularly: [consistent referrals / collaborative communication / organized clients / etc.]. Write: 1) An outreach email to a vendor I'd like to add to my preferred list — what I offer them and what I'm looking for, 2) A "preferred vendor" questionnaire to vet new vendors — what I need to know before recommending them to clients, 3) How to present my preferred vendor list to clients — why recommendations matter, 4) What to do when a preferred vendor disappoints — how to handle it without drama, 5) How to grow the network through styled shoots or collaborative marketing. Also write a one-paragraph bio for each vendor type on my website.

Marketing & Business Growth

10. The Wedding Website & Portfolio Copy

Write website copy for my event planning business. My specialty: [wedding / corporate / luxury / social / destination]. My style: [describe the vibe — modern minimalist / romantic garden / corporate sleek / fun and eclectic]. My differentiators: [what makes working with me different — be specific, not "personalized service"]. Ideal clients: [describe who you want to work with]. Write: 1) Homepage headline and sub-headline that speaks to my ideal client's desire (not "full-service event planner"), 2) About page copy — leads with what it's like to work with me, not my backstory, 3) Services page intro — the philosophy behind how I plan events, 4) A "why hire an event planner?" section that addresses the most common objection, 5) A gallery caption formula that tells the story behind each event. Under 500 words total. Avoid clichés like "your dream day" and "stress-free."

11. The Social Media Content Plan

Create a 30-day Instagram content plan for an event planning business. My events: [describe the types of events you photograph / document]. My posting comfort: [photos only / willing to do reels / behind-the-scenes / before-and-after]. Target audience: [brides / corporate clients / party hosts / mix]. Build a plan with: 1) 20 post ideas for the month across different content types (portfolio, education, behind-the-scenes, personal, social proof), 2) For each post: the caption hook (first line that stops the scroll), the body direction, and a hashtag strategy, 3) 3 Instagram Stories ideas per week (more casual, more personal), 4) How to repurpose one event into 5 different posts, 5) The one type of content event planners never post that would actually get them the most inquiries. Also: write one complete caption from the plan — pick the one with the best engagement potential.

12. The Corporate Event Package Pitch

Write a proposal package for corporate event planning services. Target: [HR director / marketing manager / executive assistant / C-suite / office manager]. Event types I can plan: [describe — company retreats / product launches / conferences / holiday parties / team building / client appreciation events]. What corporate clients care most about: [stress-free / on-brand / within budget / impresses stakeholders / handled completely]. Write: 1) A one-page capabilities overview for corporate prospects — what I do, for whom, and with what results, 2) A cold email to reach out to a corporate prospect who hasn't heard of me, 3) A follow-up email sequence if they don't respond (2 follow-ups), 4) A pricing structure that makes sense for corporate clients (retainer / day rate / percentage of budget / fixed packages), 5) The specific phrases that close corporate clients vs. the ones that lose them. Also: how is pitching corporate clients different from pitching wedding clients?

Operations & Growth

13. The Post-Event Client Experience

Design a post-event client experience system that generates referrals and testimonials. My events: [describe]. When most referrals happen: [right after the event when emotions are high / months later when they see photos / both]. Build a system with: 1) Day-after message (sent the morning after): short, warm, celebrates the success, 2) One-week follow-up: photo delivery info, vendor thank-you reminders, the testimonial ask (timing is everything — ask when they're happiest), 3) The testimonial request: the 3 specific questions to ask so they write a testimonial that sells (not just "it was perfect"), 4) 3-month follow-up: anniversary for weddings, check-in for corporate — plants the seed for the next event, 5) The referral program: what to offer clients who send you new business (be specific to events — not a gift card). Write the day-after message and one-week email in full.

14. The Crisis Communication Plan

Write communication templates for event crisis scenarios. Scenario 1: A vendor cancels 2 weeks before the event. Scenario 2: The venue double-books and the event must move. Scenario 3: Severe weather threatens an outdoor event (communication timeline: one week out, 48 hours, day-of). Scenario 4: A key element fails on event day (tech failure, catering delay, entertainment no-show). Scenario 5: The client is publicly unhappy — posting negatively on social media after the event. For each scenario write: 1) The first communication to the client — within the first hour, 2) The action plan — what you're doing right now to fix it, 3) The update once you have a solution, 4) Post-crisis follow-up — what you do to make it right after the event. Goal: a crisis that's handled well can become the reason they refer you. A crisis handled poorly becomes a negative review.

15. The Annual Business Review for Event Planners

Help me review my event planning business for the year. Data: Total revenue: $[X]. Events planned: [X]. Average event value: $[X]. Most common event type: [describe]. Best client source: [referrals / Instagram / Google / venue recommendation / other]. Highest-margin event type: [describe]. Events that felt most rewarding vs. most draining: [describe]. Run a review that: 1) Calculates my effective hourly rate (revenue ÷ total hours including planning, communication, and day-of), 2) Identifies the event type or client type that's most profitable relative to effort, 3) Recommends one pricing change for next year — increase, repackage, or add a minimum, 4) Names the single referral or marketing source to invest in more, 5) Identifies one type of event to stop taking. Direct assessment — what should change and what's working that I should protect?
Pro tip: Start with Prompt 4 (Client Update Email System) — it's the highest-ROI immediately. Event planning clients are anxious by nature; consistent, proactive communication dramatically reduces the "just checking in" emails that fragment your day. Create the 5 templates once, adapt them for each event, and you'll spend significantly less time on client management.
