15 ChatGPT Prompts for Virtual Assistants

Handle more client work in less time, charge what you're worth, and build a VA business that scales — with AI as your silent co-worker.

The VA industry is being transformed by AI — and VAs who use it well are winning bigger clients and higher rates, not being replaced by it. The key is using AI to handle the repeatable parts of your work so you can focus on judgment, relationships, and strategy.

These 15 prompts cover the full VA workflow: client onboarding, task execution, client communication, and business development. Use them in ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini.

Client Onboarding & Setup

1. The Client Discovery Questionnaire

The most expensive mistake a VA makes is starting work before understanding what the client actually needs. This prompt builds your intake process.

Create a client discovery questionnaire for a new VA client. I specialize in: [your services: inbox management / social media / bookkeeping / research / content / scheduling / etc.]. The questionnaire should uncover: 1) Their actual pain points — not just "I need help with email" but why email is a problem and what ideal looks like, 2) How they like to communicate and be updated, 3) Their decision-making style — do they want you to decide or confirm everything?, 4) Their top 3 priorities for the first 30 days, 5) What they've tried before and why it didn't work, 6) Access and tools they use. Keep it under 20 questions. Mark the 5 that are non-negotiable to answer before signing the contract.

2. The Service Scope Document

Write a scope of services document for a new client engagement. My services for this client: [list what you'll do]. What's explicitly NOT included: [list anything they might assume is included]. Deliverables: [what will you produce — reports, completed tasks, communication, etc.]. Communication expectations: [response time, check-in frequency, preferred channels]. Revision policy: [how many rounds, what counts as a new request]. Write a clear, plain-language document that protects both parties — not corporate legalese. It should be something I can email at the start of a new engagement and reference when scope creep happens. Under 400 words.

3. The 30-60-90 Day Plan Builder

Build a 30-60-90 day plan for a new VA client engagement. Client: [describe their business — industry, size, main challenges]. My role: [describe your services]. Their stated goal: [what they told you they want]. What I've observed in the first week: [any processes, systems, or pain points you've noticed]. Build a plan that: Day 1-30: Quick wins — 3 tasks I can complete that will immediately demonstrate value. Day 31-60: Systems — 2 processes I'll optimize or build from scratch. Day 61-90: Expansion — what additional value I can add based on what I've learned. Each phase should have a measurable outcome. This is what I share with clients at the 30-day check-in to show progress and justify renewal.

Task Execution

4. The Inbox Zero System

Help me build an email management system for my client. Their situation: [describe — how many emails/day, what types, what requires their personal attention vs. what I can handle]. Their industry: [describe]. Write: 1) A categorization system for their inbox (5-7 labels/folders with clear rules for what goes where), 2) Template responses for the 5 most common email types they receive (leave [CLIENT NAME] and [SPECIFIC DETAIL] placeholders), 3) A weekly email summary format — what I'll report to them each week so they stay informed without being in their inbox, 4) A decision matrix: which emails need their input vs. I can handle independently. This system should save them 1+ hour per day.

5. The Research Report Template

I need to deliver a research report to my client on: [topic]. Their decision that depends on this research: [what are they deciding?]. What they already know: [context]. Time I have: [X hours]. Format they prefer: [brief bullets / detailed report / slide-ready summary]. Build me a research framework that: 1) Lists the 7 most important questions to answer, 2) Identifies the 5 best sources to check first (be specific to this topic), 3) Provides the report structure — headers and what each section should answer, 4) Gives a "good enough" standard — what's the minimum viable research that serves their decision? Also write the executive summary template I'll fill in after completing the research.

6. The Meeting Notes & Action Items System

Transform these raw meeting notes into a clean summary and action item list: [paste messy meeting notes or transcript]. Format the output as: 1) Meeting summary (3-5 bullet points — what was decided, not what was discussed), 2) Action items table: Person responsible | Task | Deadline | Dependencies, 3) Open questions — things that came up but weren't resolved, 4) Next meeting agenda (based on what needs follow-up). Also: identify any action items that are my responsibility as the VA and flag them clearly. This should be ready to send to all attendees within 30 minutes of the meeting ending.

Client Communication

7. The Weekly Status Update

Write a weekly status update template I can send to clients every Friday. My typical work covers: [list your main service areas]. Build a template that: 1) Opens with the 3 most important things completed this week (outcomes, not tasks), 2) Flags anything that needs their attention or decision before next week, 3) Shows progress on ongoing projects, 4) Notes any blockers or things I'm waiting on from them, 5) States what I'll focus on next week. Keep it under 200 words — clients don't read long updates. Also create a shorter Slack/DM version (under 60 words) for clients who prefer that format. The goal: keep them informed without needing to chase me for updates.

8. The Difficult Conversation Script

Help me handle this difficult client situation: [choose — they're unhappy with my work / they keep changing priorities mid-task / they're unresponsive and it's blocking my work / they want me to do something outside my scope / they're late paying / they're micromanaging]. Write: 1) How to open the conversation (first sentence — direct but not confrontational), 2) The key points to make clearly, 3) What I want from this conversation (a specific outcome), 4) How to respond if they get defensive, 5) How to end it constructively regardless of how it goes. Give me word-for-word language, not advice. Under 150 words for the core message.

9. The Rate Increase Email

Write an email to an existing client announcing a rate increase. Current rate: $[X]/hour or $[X]/month. New rate: $[Y]. Effective date: [date — give at least 30 days notice]. Reason: [honest reason — haven't raised rates in X years / increasing expertise / market rates / cost of living]. Client relationship: [how long, what results you've delivered]. Write an email that: 1) Opens by referencing the work we've done together, 2) States the new rate and effective date clearly in the first paragraph, 3) Explains the context in 2 sentences without over-apologizing, 4) Makes it easy for them to continue — no pressure but clear next steps, 5) Ends warmly. Under 150 words. Also write a version for if they push back on the rate.

Business Development

10. The VA Niche Positioning Statement

Help me define my VA niche and write my positioning statement. My background: [list relevant experience, skills, industries you've worked in]. Services I'm best at: [list your strongest skills]. Types of clients I've enjoyed most: [describe]. Write: 1) 3 potential niche positions — each with a specific client type, specific services, and why my background makes me credible, 2) For the strongest niche: a one-sentence positioning statement ("I help [specific client] do [specific outcome] by [specific method]"), 3) A 3-sentence bio for my website that leads with the positioning, 4) How to describe what I do in 10 seconds at a networking event. Avoid generic VA descriptions like "I help busy entrepreneurs." Be specific enough that the right clients immediately feel "this is for me."

11. The LinkedIn Outreach System

Build a LinkedIn outreach system for finding VA clients. My niche: [describe]. Ideal client: [describe — industry, role title, company size, what they're struggling with]. Write: 1) The profile headline formula that attracts inbound inquiries (not "Virtual Assistant" — describe the outcome), 2) A connection request message template (under 300 characters — LinkedIn limit), 3) A follow-up message after connecting (under 200 words — adds value, doesn't pitch), 4) A second follow-up if no response after 5 days (under 100 words), 5) How to identify the right people to connect with (specific search criteria). Goal: 5 qualified conversations per week without feeling like spam. Make each template feel like it comes from a person, not a bot.

12. The Service Package Designer

Design 3 VA service packages I can sell instead of hourly rates. My strongest services: [list]. Typical client size: [solo entrepreneur / small team / mid-size business]. Current hourly rate: $[X]. Target monthly income: $[X]. Build packages at 3 price points that: 1) Are named for the outcome, not the hours (e.g., "Inbox Zero Package" not "10 hours/month"), 2) Have clear deliverables so clients know exactly what they're getting, 3) Have a logical upgrade path — each tier should make the next one feel obvious, 4) Price based on value, not hours (even if the hourly math works out the same). Also write the one-paragraph description for each package I can put on my website or send in proposals.

Productivity & Systems

13. The Client Handoff Document

Create a client handoff document template for when a client engagement ends. This document should allow another VA (or the client themselves) to take over my work seamlessly. Include sections for: 1) Overview of recurring tasks and when they happen, 2) Logins and access (leave placeholder for actual credentials), 3) Ongoing projects and their current status, 4) Key contacts — who to go to for what, 5) Quirks and preferences — things not written anywhere that matter (e.g., "client responds faster to Slack than email", "always CC their assistant on client emails"), 6) Files and where to find them, 7) What I'd do differently if starting over (so they avoid my early mistakes). This is my professionalism asset — it's what I give every client at offboarding.

14. The Multi-Client Prioritization System

I currently manage [X] clients simultaneously and struggle with prioritization. My clients: [brief description of each — their industry, what I do for them, how demanding they are]. Common conflicts: [what typically pulls me in different directions — multiple urgent requests at once, overlapping deadlines, etc.]. Build a prioritization system that: 1) A decision framework for triaging incoming requests (what's urgent vs. important vs. can wait), 2) How to communicate delays to one client without making them feel deprioritized, 3) A daily schedule structure that protects deep work time for each client, 4) A boundary-setting script for when a client's "urgent" request isn't actually urgent. Make it simple enough to use in real-time when I'm already stressed.

15. The AI Toolkit for VAs

I'm a VA who wants to use AI to work faster and offer more value. My current services: [list]. Create a practical guide for integrating AI into my VA workflow: 1) For each of my services, name the specific AI task that would save the most time, 2) A prompt template for my most time-consuming repeatable task: [describe the task], 3) How to present my AI use to clients — should I tell them? How does it affect my rates?, 4) 3 services I could start offering that didn't exist 3 years ago (because AI makes them possible), 5) The one AI skill that would most increase my hourly rate if I mastered it. Give me concrete, actionable recommendations — not a general overview of AI tools.
Pro tip: Prompt 4 (Inbox Zero System) is the fastest win. Most VAs spend 30-40% of their time on email. Building a proper system with templates and a categorization framework — once — saves hours every week. Use this prompt to build it in an hour, then refine it as you learn your client's preferences.
