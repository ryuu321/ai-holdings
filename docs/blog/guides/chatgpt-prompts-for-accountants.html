15 ChatGPT Prompts for Accountants & CPAs

Spend less time drafting emails and explaining concepts — and more time on the high-value work only you can do. AI won't replace accountants. Accountants using AI well will replace accountants who don't.

Accounting is one of the most communication-heavy professions. Between client emails, engagement letters, tax notices, financial explanations, and practice marketing, the writing and admin work can consume a third of your week. These prompts help you reclaim that time.

These work for CPAs, tax preparers, bookkeepers, financial controllers, accounting firm owners, and accounting professionals in public practice or industry. Always verify AI-generated content for accuracy before sending to clients — AI can draft, but the professional judgment is yours. Use in ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini.

⚠️ Important: Never share client financial data, tax information, or personally identifiable information with AI tools. Use placeholder names and figures when testing prompts.

Client Communication

1. The Plain-Language Tax Explanation

The #1 complaint clients have about accountants is that they don't explain things clearly. This prompt fixes that.

Explain [tax concept or accounting topic] in plain language for a client with no accounting background. Topic: [depreciation / estimated taxes / S-corp election / pass-through income / QBI deduction / basis / passive activity rules / etc.]. Client context: [small business owner / W-2 employee with side income / real estate investor / new retiree / first-time filer with a 1099]. Write an explanation that: 1) Uses one concrete analogy to make the concept click, 2) Explains what it means for them specifically (not just what it is in general), 3) Answers "so what do I need to do about this?", 4) Is under 200 words, 5) Avoids jargon — if you must use a technical term, define it immediately. Also write a follow-up sentence they could use to explain this to their spouse or business partner.

2. The Client Email Templates

Write email templates for the most common client communications in an accounting practice. Create templates for: 1) Requesting missing documents (polite, with a specific list and a deadline), 2) Delivering bad news — a larger-than-expected tax bill (acknowledge the news before explaining it), 3) Explaining an IRS notice (what it is, whether to worry, what happens next), 4) Requesting an extension on behalf of a client, 5) Following up on an unsigned engagement letter or unpaid invoice, 6) Sending the completed return for signature and explaining the key numbers. For each template: subject line + body under 150 words. Tone: professional but warm — not bureaucratic. Leave [BRACKETS] for client-specific details. Also write a version of template #3 for a client who tends to panic at any IRS correspondence.

3. The Engagement Letter Clarity Rewrite

Rewrite this section of my engagement letter in plain language that clients will actually read. Section to rewrite: [paste the specific section — scope of services, limitation of liability, fee structure, etc.]. Current problems: [clients don't read it / they're surprised by things that are in it / the language is legal boilerplate they don't understand]. Rewrite it so: 1) The main point is stated clearly in the first sentence, 2) What IS included is as clear as what is NOT included, 3) The fee structure section explains exactly how billing works with no ambiguity, 4) A non-accountant could understand every sentence, 5) Length is reduced by at least 30% without losing substance. Note: this is a drafting aid — your attorney should review any changes to the actual engagement letter.

Tax Planning & Advisory

4. The Year-End Tax Planning Email

Write a year-end tax planning email to send to clients in [month]. Client type: [small business owners / self-employed / high-income W-2 / real estate investors / mix]. What I want them to do before December 31: [list the specific actions — max retirement contributions, defer income, accelerate deductions, make charitable gifts, review estimated tax payments, etc.]. Write an email that: 1) Opens with a one-paragraph summary of why year-end matters this year specifically (market conditions, law changes, etc.), 2) Lists 4-6 specific actions with deadlines, 3) Explains who each action applies to (not everyone needs to do everything), 4) Makes a clear CTA: schedule a call before [date] to review their specific situation, 5) Is scannable — clients will read this on their phone. Under 400 words. Also write a shorter version for a text/WhatsApp follow-up (under 80 words).

5. The Business Financial Review Presentation

Help me structure a business financial review presentation for a client. Client: [describe — type of business, annual revenue, biggest financial challenges]. What I want to cover: [revenue trends, margins, cash flow, key ratios, year-over-year comparison, areas of concern, recommendations]. Format: [verbal meeting / slide deck / written report]. Build a presentation structure that: 1) Opens with a 2-minute executive summary — the 3 most important things to know, 2) Shows the numbers in a way that's visual and comparative, not just a spreadsheet dump, 3) Translates each metric into business implications ("your accounts receivable days have increased — here's what that means for cash flow"), 4) Recommends 2-3 specific actions, 5) Ends with what to watch in the next quarter. Write the talking points for the executive summary section.

6. The New Client Tax Strategy Summary

Write a first-year tax strategy summary for a new client. Client profile: [describe — employment status, business structure, income level, major assets, financial goals]. What I've identified from their prior returns or initial meeting: [list the key observations — missed deductions, wrong entity structure, under-saving for retirement, etc.]. Write a summary that: 1) Opens with "based on what I've seen, here are the 3-4 biggest opportunities", 2) Explains each opportunity in plain language with estimated tax impact where possible, 3) Lists what I need from them to implement each strategy, 4) Sets expectations for the engagement — what we'll tackle this year vs. longer-term, 5) Ends with "here's what happens next." Under 400 words. This summary makes clients feel like they made the right choice hiring me.

Practice Management & Growth

7. The Prospect Proposal Template

Write a proposal template for a new accounting services prospect. Prospect: [describe — type of business, what they need, size, pain points they mentioned]. Services I'm proposing: [list — tax prep, bookkeeping, advisory, payroll, etc.]. My firm's differentiators: [what actually makes you different — specialization, tech stack, proactive communication, industry focus]. Write a proposal that: 1) Opens with their situation and the problem you're solving (not "thank you for meeting with us"), 2) Proposes the engagement in plain language — what's included, what's not, what you need from them, 3) Presents pricing clearly without apologizing for it, 4) Includes 2-3 specific reasons why your firm specifically is right for them — based on what they told you, 5) States a clear next step and deadline. Under 600 words. Also write a follow-up email to send if you don't hear back in 5 business days.

8. The Difficult Client Conversation Script

Help me script a difficult conversation with a client. Situation: [choose — client is unhappy with their tax bill / client questions your fees / client has been unresponsive and it's causing problems / client asks you to do something outside your services or ethical standards / client wants to under-report income / client is always late with documents]. Write: 1) How to open the conversation directly but without being confrontational, 2) The core message in clear, professional language, 3) The most likely pushback and how to respond to each, 4) How to close with clear next steps — whether the relationship continues or not, 5) What to document after the conversation. For the "client asks me to do something unethical" scenario: also include how to handle it in writing and what the professional obligations are at a high level.

9. The Referral Network Builder

Help me build a referral network for my accounting practice. My specialty: [tax planning for small businesses / bookkeeping / real estate accounting / high-net-worth / startup accounting / etc.]. My ideal referral sources: [financial advisors / attorneys / business coaches / commercial bankers / insurance agents / real estate agents]. Write: 1) An outreach email to a professional in each category — specific, not generic, explains the mutual benefit, 2) A 30-second introduction for networking events ("what do you do?" — but for accountants), 3) What to offer referral sources (clear value, no kickbacks / fee-splitting issues — flag if needed), 4) A follow-up system: how to stay top of mind with referral partners without being pushy, 5) The most common mistake accountants make when trying to build referral networks. Also: which type of referral partner typically sends the highest-quality leads for accounting?

Client Education & Content

10. The Client Newsletter Template

Write a monthly client newsletter template for an accounting practice. Client base: [small business owners / individuals / mix]. Goal: [keep clients informed / demonstrate expertise / generate referrals / sell advisory services]. Build a template with: 1) Subject line formula (give me 3 options for this month), 2) Tax tip of the month: one actionable thing clients should know or do this month, 3) "Did you know?" section: one surprising tax fact or planning idea, 4) Important deadlines section: upcoming filing deadlines relevant to your client base, 5) A brief firm update (new service, new team member, or firm achievement — not just self-promotion), 6) A soft CTA: one reason to reach out. Under 300 words total. Also: which months should have a stronger CTA and why?

11. The FAQ Page Content

Write FAQ page content for my accounting website. My specialty: [describe]. Target clients: [describe]. Write answers to the 10 most common questions my ideal clients ask: include at least — "How much do your services cost?", "Do I really need a CPA or can I use TurboTax?", "What documents do I need to bring?", "How long does it take to file my taxes?", "What happens if I'm audited?". For each question: 1) A plain-language answer under 100 words, 2) A one-sentence that explains why this matters to them, 3) A CTA that makes sense for that question (schedule a call, upload documents, learn more). Also write meta descriptions for the FAQ page and the services page of my website (under 160 characters each).

12. The LinkedIn Content Plan

Create a 30-day LinkedIn content plan for an accountant building a professional reputation. My goal: [attract small business clients / build referral relationships with financial advisors / establish thought leadership in [specialty] / all of above]. My comfort zone with content: [sharing tips / opinion posts / personal stories / educational explainers]. Build a plan with: 1) 12 post ideas for the month (3 posts per week), 2) For each post: the topic, the angle (what's the hook?), and the format (text / image / short video), 3) 2 posts that are specifically designed to get engagement from referral sources (attorneys, financial planners), 4) 1 post that demonstrates expertise in my specialty, 5) How to turn a client question into a post without violating confidentiality. Also write one complete post from the plan — pick the one with the highest potential engagement.

Efficiency & Operations

13. The Client Onboarding Checklist

Build a new client onboarding checklist and workflow for an accounting practice. Services I provide: [tax prep / bookkeeping / advisory / payroll]. Pain points with current onboarding: [slow document collection / clients don't know what to expect / too much back-and-forth / setup takes weeks]. Build a workflow with: 1) Pre-engagement: what to send before the engagement letter is signed (expectations doc, document list, access requests), 2) Day 1: what happens immediately after signing (welcome email, portal access, first document request), 3) Week 1-2: what gets set up and in what order, 4) First deliverable: what the client receives and when — the moment they feel like they made the right decision, 5) 30-day check-in: how to confirm everything is working. Also write the welcome email that sets the tone for the entire engagement (under 200 words, warm and professional).

14. The Tax Season Communication Plan

Create a tax season communication plan to reduce client stress and staff overwhelm. My firm size: [solo / 2-5 staff / 5-20 staff]. Tax season months: [January through April, or year-round]. Common problems: [clients submit documents at the last minute / clients call constantly for status updates / documents are incomplete / clients expect faster turnaround than possible]. Build a plan with: 1) January: the "tax season is starting" email that sets expectations and outlines what clients need to do and when, 2) February: a mid-season status email for clients who haven't submitted documents, 3) March 15: the small business extension conversation, 4) April: the "we may need to file an extension" communication — how to deliver it, 5) Post-season: the follow-up that turns tax clients into year-round advisory clients. Write the January kickoff email in full (under 300 words, sets expectations without overwhelming).

15. The Scope Creep Handler

Write scripts for handling scope creep in an accounting practice. Common situations: [client asks "while you're in there, can you also..." / client adds a new entity mid-engagement / client asks for research or advice outside the engagement / bookkeeping client wants tax advice included / client sends documents late and expects the same deadline]. For each situation write: 1) How to recognize it early (the signal that scope is expanding), 2) The response in the moment (verbal or email) — how to say "that's outside our scope" without sounding difficult, 3) How to convert the out-of-scope request into a paid add-on, 4) The follow-up email confirming the new scope and fee, 5) How to update the engagement letter if needed. Also: write a general "additional services" email template for services added mid-year.
Pro tip: Start with Prompt 1 (Plain-Language Tax Explanation) — it pays dividends immediately and builds long-term client loyalty. Clients who understand what you're doing for them refer more often, question your fees less, and stay with you longer. Clear communication is the highest-ROI investment most accountants can make.
