15 ChatGPT Prompts for Executive Assistants

The best EAs don't just manage calendars — they make their executives more effective. AI helps you draft faster, think more strategically, and demonstrate the kind of value that defines a career-defining EA.

Executive assistants operate at the intersection of every function in an organization. In a single day you might draft a board presentation, manage a complex international itinerary, navigate a sensitive vendor dispute, and prepare your executive for a difficult conversation. These prompts help you do all of it faster — without sacrificing quality.

These work for executive assistants, senior executive assistants, C-suite EAs, chief of staff adjacents, and administrative professionals supporting senior leadership. Use them in ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini. Always review before sending — especially anything going out in your executive's name.

Executive Communication

1. The Executive Email Draft

Writing in someone else's voice while maintaining your own quality standards is the core EA skill. This prompt helps you draft emails your executive will barely need to edit.

Draft an email on behalf of my executive. Executive's role and communication style: [describe — CEO / CFO / VP / formal / direct / collaborative / warm]. Recipient: [who they're emailing — peer executive / board member / employee / external partner / vendor]. Purpose: [what the email needs to accomplish — schedule a meeting / deliver feedback / respond to a request / approve something / decline something / follow up on a previous conversation]. Key points the email must convey: [list 2-4 essential points]. Tone: [describe relative to their usual style]. Write an email that: 1) Opens with the most important point (executives don't bury the lead), 2) Is under 150 words unless the situation genuinely requires more, 3) Has a clear action item or next step at the end, 4) Matches how they actually write — not how most people think executives write. Also write a version for if the email needs to be more sensitive or careful.

2. The Meeting Preparation Brief

Prepare a pre-meeting brief for my executive. Meeting details: [who they're meeting, purpose, context, any background I have]. What my executive needs to know: [key talking points, decisions that may come up, the other party's priorities, any sensitivities]. Time available: [X minutes to read this brief]. Write a one-page brief with: 1) Who they're meeting and the most important thing to know about this person/organization right now, 2) Meeting purpose and what success looks like, 3) 2-3 key points my executive should make or questions they should ask, 4) Known sensitivities or landmines to avoid, 5) One talking point that builds the relationship beyond the agenda item. Format: scannable in 3 minutes — bullets, bold key words, no more than one page. Also: what one thing should my executive know that might not be in the formal agenda?

3. The Sensitive Email Response

Help me draft a response to a sensitive email received by my executive. Situation: [describe the incoming email or message — a complaint / an angry vendor / a difficult board member / a sensitive employee matter / a political situation between departments]. What my executive wants to accomplish: [acknowledge / de-escalate / commit to follow-up / decline / redirect]. What should NOT be said: [list any specific things to avoid]. Write a response that: 1) Acknowledges the sender's concern without conceding anything prematurely, 2) Is measured — says what needs to be said without over-explaining or over-apologizing, 3) Leaves the relationship intact for future interaction, 4) Ends with clear next steps (even if the next step is "we'll be in touch"), 5) Is written at my executive's level — appropriate tone for a senior leader. Also write a note explaining to my executive why I drafted it this way, so they can decide whether to modify it.

Calendar & Operations

4. The Travel Itinerary Builder

Build a comprehensive travel itinerary for my executive. Trip details: [destination(s), dates, purpose of trip, meetings already confirmed]. What I need to include: [flights / hotel / ground transportation / meals / meeting prep time / personal preferences]. Executive's preferences: [describe — aisle seat / specific hotel brands / dietary restrictions / morning or evening flights preferred / how much buffer time between commitments]. Build an itinerary with: 1) Day-by-day timeline with all logistics, 2) Buffer time between commitments appropriate to travel time and jet lag, 3) What to have ready the night before each day (documents, contacts, talking points needed), 4) Emergency contacts and backup options for critical transportation, 5) A one-paragraph summary my executive can read to understand the full trip without going through the details. Also: what do experienced EAs always include in travel itineraries that first-timers forget?

5. The Calendar Management Protocol

Help me build a calendar management protocol for my executive. My executive's priorities: [describe their top 3-4 priorities — board relations / team management / external partnerships / deep work / specific projects]. Current calendar problems: [over-booked / too many low-value meetings / no deep work time / people bypass me and book directly / last-minute additions]. Build a protocol with: 1) Categories of meetings and how to handle each (which I can schedule without asking, which need approval, which to decline by default), 2) Protected blocks: what time should be guarded and how to communicate that diplomatically, 3) Response to "can we get 30 minutes with [executive]?" — how to qualify and respond, 4) Weekly calendar review process: how and when to brief my executive on the upcoming week, 5) What to do when someone escalates past me to schedule directly. Also write a standard response for meeting requests that I can't accept right now.

6. The Project Management Summary

Write an executive project status summary for [project]. Project: [describe — what it is, who's involved, what stage it's in]. Audience: [my executive / the board / external stakeholders]. What's going well: [describe]. What's behind or at risk: [describe — be honest, not defensive]. What decisions are needed: [what my executive needs to decide or unblock]. Write a summary that: 1) Opens with the status in one sentence (on track / at risk / behind — and by how much), 2) Provides the 3-4 key updates in bullet form, 3) States clearly what's needed from my executive — not "FYI" but a specific ask, 4) Is under 200 words unless the situation genuinely requires more, 5) Doesn't bury the problems. Also write a 60-second verbal briefing version — what to say if my executive asks for an update while walking between meetings.

Research & Analysis Support

7. The Briefing Document Builder

Build a briefing document on [topic, organization, or person] for my executive. What this briefing is for: [a first meeting / a negotiation / a board presentation / a media interview / a new business relationship]. What I know: [describe the available information]. What my executive needs: [background / key facts / context / talking points / questions to ask / risks to know]. Build a one-page briefing with: 1) Who/what this is: essential background in 3-4 sentences, 2) Why this matters right now: what's the immediate context or opportunity, 3) Key talking points or questions for my executive, 4) What to know about the person or organization's current priorities (what keeps them up at night?), 5) What NOT to bring up — known sensitivities or current issues. Format: scannable, no jargon, readable in 5 minutes.

8. The Research Summary

I need to research [topic] for my executive. What the research is for: [a specific decision / a presentation / a competitive analysis / industry background / due diligence on a potential partner]. What my executive will do with this: [describe the decision or action the research informs]. What I've found so far: [describe or paste key information]. Time available for research: [X hours]. Build a research framework with: 1) The 5 most important questions this research needs to answer, 2) The best sources to check first for this type of information (types of sources, not specific links), 3) What "good enough" looks like — the minimum to inform the decision without over-researching, 4) The summary format my executive can read in 5 minutes, 5) The one thing I should make sure not to miss. Also write the executive summary section I'll fill in after completing the research.

9. The Presentation Deck Outline

Create a presentation outline for [presentation topic and audience]. Audience: [describe — board / all-hands / external stakeholders / leadership team]. What this presentation needs to accomplish: [inform / persuade / get approval / inspire / report results]. Key messages: [what are the 2-3 things the audience must walk away knowing or believing?]. Length: [X slides / X minutes]. Create a slide-by-slide outline with: 1) Slide title for each slide, 2) What goes on each slide — the key point, not just the category, 3) Data or evidence needed for each slide (what to gather before writing), 4) Where the narrative tension or key decision point should land, 5) A strong closing slide that leaves the audience knowing exactly what happens next. Also: what do most executive presentations get wrong that I should make sure to avoid?

Relationship Management

10. The Stakeholder Communication System

Build a stakeholder communication system for my executive. Key stakeholders: [list the 5-8 most important relationships — board members / key clients / major investors / internal peers / government or regulatory contacts]. What good stakeholder management looks like: [regular touch, appropriate formality, proactive updates, relationship maintenance]. Current gaps: [what's falling through the cracks — who isn't hearing from my executive often enough?]. Build a system with: 1) Communication cadence for each stakeholder tier (monthly / quarterly / event-driven), 2) A 30-second update format for proactive relationship maintenance (what to include when nothing urgent is happening), 3) The most important stakeholder to reconnect with this month and why, 4) How to draft relationship maintenance communications in my executive's voice, 5) How to track stakeholder interactions so nothing falls through the cracks. Also write a sample relationship-maintenance email for the top stakeholder tier.

11. The Diplomatic Decline

Write response templates for declining or redirecting requests on behalf of my executive. Request types I need to handle: 1) Meeting request from someone external who shouldn't get direct executive access, 2) Speaking invitation my executive can't accept, 3) Donation or sponsorship request that's being declined, 4) Media interview request that we're not ready for or declining, 5) Request from a board member or senior internal person that's being redirected to someone else. For each: 1) A polite, professional decline that doesn't burn the relationship, 2) A redirect option where appropriate (who should they contact instead?), 3) How to leave the door open for future engagement without over-committing. Word-for-word templates that can be adapted quickly. Under 100 words each.

12. The Crisis Communication Draft

Help me draft a crisis communication for my executive. Situation: [describe the situation requiring executive communication — an incident, a public issue, a major announcement, a sensitive internal matter]. Audience: [employees / board / media / customers / all of the above]. What the communication needs to accomplish: [acknowledge / explain / reassure / commit to action / all of the above]. Time pressure: [how quickly does this need to go out?]. Draft: 1) The initial statement — acknowledges the situation without admitting fault prematurely (unless it's clearly warranted), 2) What to commit to specifically vs. what to keep vague until more is known, 3) The follow-up communication once more information is available, 4) How to brief my executive before they speak publicly — 3-5 bullet points. Also: what do PR professionals always include in crisis communications that most executive communications miss?

Career Development

13. The EA-to-Executive Value Proof

Help me articulate and document the value I create for my executive. What I do: [describe your key responsibilities and accomplishments — be specific, with examples]. What my executive's time is worth: [their approximate hourly value based on compensation]. The problem: EA value is often invisible because it prevents problems before they happen. Build: 1) A framework for documenting "invisible wins" — problems prevented, time saved, relationships protected, 2) How to quantify my work: what is one calendar decision worth? What's one saved relationship worth?, 3) My value proposition: a one-paragraph statement of what I do for my executive that I can use in a performance review, 4) The 3 metrics that best demonstrate EA impact over the past year, 5) How to present this at a salary review without sounding defensive. Also: what do executives say their best EAs have that good EAs don't — what's the difference that matters most?

14. The EA Career Development Plan

Build a career development plan for an executive assistant who wants to advance. My current level: [EA / Senior EA / Executive EA / Chief of Staff adjacent]. What I want: [senior EA / chief of staff / operations role / transition out of EA / highest-compensation EA possible]. My strengths: [describe what you're best at]. What I need to develop: [describe your gaps — strategic thinking / financial literacy / project management / industry knowledge / executive presence]. Build a plan with: 1) The specific skill to develop this quarter that has the highest career ROI, 2) How to get stretch assignments that build experience without waiting for a formal role change, 3) The EA professional community and resources that actually matter (types of organizations, certifications worth pursuing), 4) How to have the career conversation with my current executive without seeming like I'm leaving, 5) What the highest-earning EAs have that average EAs don't — and how to close that gap specifically.

15. The EA Performance Review Preparation

Help me prepare for my performance review as an executive assistant. My accomplishments this year: [list specific wins — projects managed, crises handled, relationships preserved, time saved, processes improved]. Feedback I've received: [positive and developmental]. What I want: [salary increase / promotion / expanded scope / formal chief-of-staff title / recognition]. What I know about how my executive perceives my work: [describe]. Prepare: 1) How to open the review by leading with business impact, not tasks, 2) 5 specific accomplishments framed as business outcomes (not "I managed the calendar" but what that made possible), 3) How to make the ask for what I want — directly and without apologizing, 4) How to respond if they're surprised by the ask or say "let me think about it", 5) What to do if the answer is no — how to get a clear timeline and criteria for the future. Word-for-word language for the most critical moments.
Pro tip: Start with Prompt 2 (Meeting Preparation Brief) — it has the highest immediate impact on your executive's perception of your value. EAs who brief their executives well before important meetings are indispensable. EAs who don't are replaceable. A one-page brief that takes you 20 minutes to prepare can save your executive hours of awkwardness and lost credibility.
