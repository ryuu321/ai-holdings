15 ChatGPT Prompts for Writers

Use AI as a creative partner — not to write for you, but to help you write more, faster, and better. Beat blocks, develop your voice, and finally finish what you start.

The fear with AI writing tools is that they'll replace your voice. Used right, they do the opposite — they give you a thinking partner that helps you find your voice faster, work through ideas that are stuck, and handle the structural work so you can focus on the actual writing.

These 15 prompts work for fiction writers, bloggers, essayists, content writers, and anyone who writes for a living or wants to. Use them in ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini — always make the output your own.

Beating Writer's Block

1. The Block Diagnosis

Writer's block isn't one thing. It's five different problems with five different solutions. This prompt figures out which one you have.

I'm stuck on my writing project: [describe what you're working on — type, stage, what you've written so far]. I've been stuck for [X days/weeks]. When I try to write, here's what happens: [describe — blank page, writing and deleting, avoiding it, etc.]. Diagnose my block. Give me: 1) What type of block this sounds like (fear, perfectionism, structural problem, unclear purpose, etc.), 2) The most likely root cause based on what I've described, 3) The one exercise that's most likely to unlock me TODAY (not "write every day" — something specific for right now), 4) What to do if that doesn't work. Be direct. Don't coddle me.

2. The 10-Minute Unsticker

I need to unstick my writing in the next 10 minutes. Project: [describe]. Where I'm stuck: [describe the exact scene, paragraph, or section]. The problem: [choose — I don't know what happens next / the scene feels flat / I wrote myself into a corner / the dialogue isn't working / I can't get the opening right]. Give me: 1) Three different directions I could take this — make them genuinely different, not variations on the same idea, 2) One writing exercise specific to this exact problem (5 minutes max to do it), 3) The question I'm probably avoiding answering about this project. Don't write the scene for me — help me write it myself.

3. The Idea Expander

I have a writing idea that feels interesting but underdeveloped: [describe your idea in 2-3 sentences]. Help me expand it by: 1) Identifying what's genuinely interesting about this idea (the core that's worth developing), 2) Asking me 5 questions that, if I answered them, would double the depth of this idea, 3) Suggesting 3 angles or approaches — what's the conventional way to write this, and what are two unexpected ways that might be more interesting?, 4) Identifying any tension or conflict that's implicit in the idea that I might not have noticed. Don't give me a plot or essay outline yet — just help me understand what I actually have here.

Craft & Voice Development

4. The Voice Analyzer

Most writers can't describe their own voice. Once you can, you can develop it intentionally.

Analyze my writing voice. Here are three samples of my writing that I'm happy with: [paste samples — aim for 200+ words total]. Analyze: 1) Sentence structure patterns — what do I tend to do (long/short, complex/simple, rhythm), 2) Word choice tendencies — formal/informal, abstract/concrete, common/unusual, 3) How I handle emotion — directly stated, implied, metaphorical, 4) My relationship with the reader — authoritative, conversational, intimate, distant, 5) Two strengths of my voice I should lean into, 6) One pattern that might be limiting me. Then write one paragraph in my voice on this topic: [any topic]. This is my reference point for when I write — I want to understand my own voice better than a reader does.

5. The Style Stretcher

I want to develop my range as a writer. Take this paragraph I've written: [paste paragraph]. Now rewrite it in three different styles — not parody, but genuine attempts: 1) More compressed and spare (Hemingway-direction — what can be cut?), 2) More expansive and sensory (what details, what texture can be added?), 3) More unusual — break one convention of the original (sentence structure, POV, tense, voice). After each version, explain what choices you made and why. I want to learn from the comparisons, not just use the rewrites.

6. The Scene Dissection

Dissect this scene I've written and tell me what's working and what isn't: [paste scene — ideally 200-500 words]. Be specific and direct. Cover: 1) What's genuinely working — the one or two things I should protect in revision, 2) Where the tension drops and why, 3) Any place where I'm telling what I should be showing (or vice versa — sometimes showing slows things down), 4) One line or moment that's stronger than the rest — why does it work?, 5) The single most important thing to fix in revision. Don't soften your feedback. I need a real edit, not encouragement.

Structure & Planning

7. The Outline Generator (Non-Fiction)

Create an outline for a [type: essay / article / book chapter / long-form piece] on this topic: [describe topic]. Target reader: [describe who will read this]. Core argument or takeaway: [what do you want readers to think/feel/do after reading?]. Length target: [word count or pages]. Build an outline that: 1) Opens with a hook that earns the reader's attention without clickbait, 2) Has a clear through-line — each section leads to the next, 3) Builds to the core argument rather than stating it immediately, 4) Ends with something that gives the reader something to do with what they've learned. For each section: include the purpose, not just the topic — why is this section here?

8. The Novel Structure Mapper

Help me map the structure of my novel. What I have so far: [describe premise, main characters, and what you've written]. Genre: [genre]. Target length: [word count]. The core conflict: [what is the fundamental opposition in this story?]. Map my story onto a three-act structure, but also tell me: 1) Where my current plan has structural weaknesses (scenes or sections with no tension function), 2) Where my protagonist needs to make a decision that reveals character — are there enough of these moments?, 3) The midpoint — what reversal or revelation should happen at the halfway point?, 4) How the ending earns everything the setup promised. Be honest if what I've described sounds like it has structural problems — I need to know now, not after I've written 80,000 words.

9. The Series Bible Builder

Help me build a series bible for my writing project: [describe the series — fiction, non-fiction, newsletter, blog, etc.]. What I have so far: [describe existing content, characters, world, or recurring themes]. Build a series bible that covers: 1) The core premise in one sentence (what every entry in this series is fundamentally about), 2) The rules of this world or the consistent elements that make it recognizable as a series, 3) Character profiles for the 3 main characters (or topic areas if non-fiction) — their consistent traits, arc, and function, 4) Things that are off-limits — what this series will never do, 5) The promise to readers — what they can count on every time they read something in this series. This is my north star document. Keep it under 600 words so I'll actually use it.

Publishing & Audience Building

10. The Query Letter Writer

Write a query letter for my book. Book details: Title: [title]. Genre: [genre]. Word count: [X]. Comparable titles: [2-3 books published in the last 5 years that are similar]. Protagonist: [name, age, what they want, what stands in their way]. Core conflict: [the central tension]. Theme: [what the book is ultimately about]. Write a query letter that: 1) Opens with the hook paragraph — protagonist, inciting incident, stakes (under 100 words), 2) Summarizes the plot in 150-200 words — include the choice the protagonist must make at the climax, 3) Closes with the "housekeeping" paragraph (word count, genre, comps, brief bio). Model it on what gets requests from agents, not what feels impressive.

11. The Newsletter Content System

Design a content system for my writing newsletter. My niche: [describe what you write about and who reads it]. Current subscriber count: [X]. Publishing frequency: [weekly/biweekly/monthly]. Time I can spend per issue: [X hours]. Build a system that includes: 1) A repeatable format — specific sections that appear in every issue so readers know what to expect, 2) A content calendar structure for the next 8 issues (topics, not specific titles), 3) One type of content I can batch create to make publishing easier, 4) How to repurpose each newsletter into at least 2 other content formats. The goal: sustainable publishing without burnout, content that grows the list, and a format readers look forward to.

12. The Book Proposal Executive Summary

Write the overview section of a book proposal for a non-fiction book. Book concept: [describe your book idea]. Target reader: [who specifically will buy this book — be as specific as possible]. The problem this book solves: [what pain or gap does it address?]. Why now: [why is this the right moment for this book?]. Competing books: [list 3-4 books that are the closest comps]. What's different about mine: [how is your approach, angle, or argument distinctly different?]. Write a 300-400 word overview that: 1) Opens with the reader's problem, not the book's premise, 2) Makes a clear and specific promise, 3) Establishes why I'm the person to write this, 4) Ends with a compelling reason this book needs to exist.

Writing Habit & Business

13. The Writing Schedule Builder

Build me a realistic writing schedule. My situation: [describe — job, family, current writing output, what you're working on]. My goal: [finish [X] by [date] / publish [X] per week / etc.]. My biggest obstacle to writing consistently: [describe honestly]. Build a schedule that: 1) Works with my actual life — don't give me "wake up at 5am" if that's not me, 2) Has a minimum viable session (what's the shortest session that still makes meaningful progress?), 3) Protects writing time from the specific things that eat it (be specific to what I've described), 4) Includes a weekly reset — how do I recover when I fall off the schedule? Give me the actual schedule with times, not a framework I have to figure out myself.

14. The Writing Income Plan

Help me map a path to earning money from my writing. My situation: [describe — what you write, what you've published, any income so far, your goals]. Time available per week: [X hours]. Build a realistic income plan that: 1) Identifies the 3 most practical income streams for my specific writing type and experience level, 2) Shows what I'd need to do in the next 90 days to start earning from each stream, 3) Gives a conservative estimate of what each stream could realistically earn in year 1, 4) Identifies the one stream with the highest probability of success given my starting point. Be honest about timelines — I'd rather have a realistic plan than an optimistic one I abandon.

15. The Rejection Recovery Protocol

I just got a rejection for my writing: [describe — query rejection, submission rejection, pitch rejection, etc.]. The feedback (if any): [paste feedback or "no feedback given"]. Help me process this productively: 1) What, if anything, is useful information in this rejection (separate the signal from the noise), 2) What NOT to do right now (specific actions that feel productive but aren't), 3) One concrete improvement I can make to the work or pitch based on this, 4) When and how to resubmit or submit elsewhere, 5) The honest answer to whether this rejection suggests a problem with the work or just a numbers game. I don't need encouragement — I need a clear-headed assessment.
Pro tip: Use Prompt 4 (Voice Analyzer) before anything else. Most writers spend years guessing at their own voice. Getting a clear articulation of what makes your writing yours gives you a reference point for every piece you write — especially useful when AI-assisted drafts start to drift from your natural style.
