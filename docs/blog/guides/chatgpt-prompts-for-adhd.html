15 ChatGPT Prompts for ADHD

Beat task paralysis, manage time blindness, and build routines that actually stick — using AI as your external executive function.

If you have ADHD, you already know that generic productivity advice doesn't work for your brain. "Just make a to-do list" doesn't help when the list itself causes overwhelm. "Time-block your calendar" doesn't work when you lose track of time entirely.

These 15 ChatGPT prompts are designed specifically for ADHD brains — they account for task paralysis, hyperfocus, rejection sensitivity, working memory limitations, and the particular way ADHD affects executive function.

Copy them, fill in the brackets, and use them in ChatGPT (free or Plus), Claude, or Gemini.

Task Breakdown & Getting Started

1. The Micro-Step Generator

When a task feels too big to start, this breaks it into steps your brain can actually initiate.

Act as an ADHD productivity coach who specializes in task initiation. I'm completely stuck on starting [describe the task]. Break it down into the smallest possible first steps — each one should take under 5 minutes and feel genuinely doable. Add a "dopamine hook" to make each step feel rewarding. Give me 8 micro-steps.

2. The Resistance Analyzer

Procrastination is rarely about laziness — it's usually about something specific. This prompt finds the real blocker.

I've been avoiding [task] for [timeframe]. Ask me 5 diagnostic questions to figure out WHY I'm avoiding it — is it boring, scary, unclear, or something else? After I answer, give me a specific strategy to overcome that exact type of resistance.

3. The Body Double Script

Body doubling (working alongside someone) is one of the most effective ADHD strategies. This prompt creates a virtual version.

Be my virtual body double for the next [time]. I need to work on [task]. Check in with me every [interval] minutes by asking me: 1) What I just accomplished, 2) What I'm doing next, 3) Any obstacles I hit. Start by asking me what my first micro-task is.

Time Management & Scheduling

4. The Realistic Schedule Builder

ADHD time estimates are notoriously optimistic. This prompt builds schedules that account for transition time, interruptions, and energy fluctuations.

I need to create a realistic schedule for [day/week]. Here are my tasks: [list tasks]. I have ADHD, so: 1) Triple my time estimates, 2) Add 15-minute transition buffers between tasks, 3) Block my highest energy time ([morning/afternoon/evening]) for my hardest tasks, 4) Schedule actual breaks every 90 minutes. Output as a time-blocked schedule in a table format.

5. The Priority Sorter

When everything feels equally urgent (ADHD urgent = everything), you need an external system to sort it.

Here's my current task list: [paste tasks]. I have ADHD and struggle with prioritization. Evaluate each task on: 1) True urgency (real deadline vs. anxiety-perceived deadline), 2) Impact if not done today, 3) Which tasks are actually in my control. Then give me a ranked list of my top 3 tasks for today, and tell me which tasks I can safely ignore or delegate.

6. The Time Blindness Hack

I have time blindness and need to complete [task] in [timeframe]. Create an "internal alarm system" for me: specific sensory cues, environmental reminders, and behavioral checkpoints I can use to stay aware of time passing. Be creative — generic calendar reminders don't work for me.

Routine Building

7. The Habit Stack Designer

ADHD brains do better when new habits are attached to existing ones. This prompt designs the stacking sequence.

I want to build a habit of [new habit] but I can never remember to do it. Here are habits I do reliably every day: [list 3-5 existing habits]. Design a habit stack where [new habit] is attached to one of my existing habits. Include: the exact trigger, the habit, the reward, and how to troubleshoot if I skip it.

8. The Morning Routine Optimizer

Help me design a 20-minute morning routine for someone with ADHD. My main challenges are: [e.g., getting out of bed, decision fatigue, forgetting things]. The routine should: have zero decisions to make, use visual/physical cues, include a dopamine spike in the first 5 minutes, and end with a clear transition to [work/school/etc]. Make it idiot-proof.

9. The Streak Repair Protocol

Breaking a streak often leads to "all or nothing" thinking. This prompt helps you recover without spiraling.

I broke my [habit/routine] streak after [number] days. I'm feeling [frustrated/defeated/like giving up]. Help me: 1) Reframe this setback without toxic positivity, 2) Analyze what caused the break, 3) Design a "minimum viable version" of the habit for my next attempt, 4) Create a specific plan for tomorrow that makes it easier to restart. Be honest but kind.

Focus & Hyperfocus Management

10. The Focus Session Planner

I need to do a focused work session on [task] for [time period]. Design a complete session structure for my ADHD brain: 1) A 2-minute "entering focus" ritual, 2) The exact task scope (what counts as DONE for this session), 3) How to handle distracting thoughts that pop up (note them without acting), 4) A reward I get for completing the session, 5) How to end the session cleanly so I don't lose track of where I was.

11. The Hyperfocus Exit Strategy

Getting into hyperfocus is easy. Getting out before you forget everything else is the hard part.

I'm prone to hyperfocusing on [topic/task] and losing 4+ hours without realizing it. Create a "hyperfocus interrupt" system: specific alarms or cues I should set, physical interventions to snap me out of it, a 3-question self-check I can run every 45 minutes, and a "save state" process so I can pick up exactly where I left off later.

Emotional Regulation & RSD

12. The RSD Deescalation Script

Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria hits ADHD people disproportionately hard. This prompt helps process it.

I'm experiencing a strong emotional reaction to [perceived rejection/criticism/failure]. I have ADHD and RSD. Help me: 1) Validate that this feeling is real and intense without telling me I'm overreacting, 2) Reality-test whether the rejection is as severe as it feels, 3) Give me a 5-minute grounding exercise, 4) Help me decide if/how to respond to the situation after I've regulated. Don't rush me to "feel better."

13. The Self-Compassion Reframe

I feel like I failed at [task/goal/day]. I keep thinking [negative thought about myself]. Help me reframe this using self-compassion — not toxic positivity, but genuine acknowledgment that ADHD makes [thing] genuinely harder, that this doesn't define my intelligence or worth, and a specific next step that feels manageable right now.

Work & Communication

14. The Email Response Unsticker

Emails pile up because responding feels overwhelming. This prompt breaks the barrier.

I've been avoiding responding to this email for [time]. [Paste the email]. I'm stuck because: [reason — I don't know what to say / it feels confrontational / I need to check something first / other]. Write 3 possible responses ranging from "brief and professional" to "thorough and detailed." Let me pick the one that feels right.

15. The Meeting Prep Accelerator

I have a meeting about [topic] in [time]. I haven't prepared. Give me: 1) The 3 things I absolutely need to know going in, 2) 2 good questions to ask so I look engaged even if I zoned out, 3) A 30-second summary of my talking points if asked, 4) What to do with the action items after — knowing I'll probably forget them the moment I leave the room.
Pro tip: Save the prompts that work best for you in a document or note-taking app. ADHD brains benefit from having systems ready to go — don't rely on remembering to use a prompt when you're already in overwhelm.
