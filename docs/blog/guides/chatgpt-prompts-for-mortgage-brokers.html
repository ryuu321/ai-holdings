ChatGPT Prompts for Mortgage Brokers

15 free prompts to write better client communications, generate more referrals, and save hours on marketing and admin every week.

Mortgage brokers and loan officers spend enormous amounts of time explaining complex processes to anxious borrowers, building referral relationships, and staying top-of-mind in a relationship-driven business. These 15 prompts handle the writing so you can focus on deals.

Important: These prompts assist with communication and marketing — not with providing mortgage advice, rate quotes, or loan recommendations. All AI-generated content must comply with RESPA, TILA, the Fair Housing Act, and your state licensing requirements before use. Never input client Social Security numbers, credit scores, income figures, or other sensitive financial data into AI tools. Have your compliance officer review marketing materials.

1. Client Communication

Prompt 1 — Pre-Application Education Email

Write an email to educate a first-time homebuyer before their mortgage application. Client profile: [first-time buyer / refinancer / investment property / self-employed borrower] Their concerns: [credit score / down payment / debt-to-income ratio / how long it takes / what documents they need] My goal: [set expectations / reduce anxiety / make them feel prepared and confident] Write: Subject line → Opening (acknowledge it can feel overwhelming) → What the process looks like (5-step overview in plain language) → What to prepare now (document checklist) → What to avoid doing before the loan closes (big purchases, new credit) → What I will handle for them → Easy next step (schedule a call, download checklist). Under 350 words. Clear, human, no jargon.
Pro tip: First-time buyers who feel prepared at application are 40% less likely to go dark mid-process. Proactive education is your best client retention tool.

Prompt 2 — Rate Lock / Market Update Email

Write a client market update email about current mortgage rates. Current rate environment: [describe in general terms — rising / falling / volatile / holding steady] What this means for clients in process: [should they lock / timing considerations] What this means for buyers on the sidelines: [general market context] Compliance note: Do not quote specific rates or guarantee anything. Write: Subject line → What is happening in the rate market (plain language, no speculation) → What this means for buyers in different situations → What I recommend they do next (call me — rates are personal) → Reminder that I am here to explain options for their specific situation. Under 300 words. Informative but non-committal on specific rates or outcomes.

Prompt 3 — Closing Day Congratulations + Referral Ask

Write a closing day congratulations message + referral request. Client type: [first-time buyer / move-up buyer / refinancer / investor] My relationship with this client: [worked together for X months / referral from their agent / repeat client] What the referral ask should feel like: [genuine and easy, not transactional] Write: (1) Email version (warm congratulations → brief note about what the process was like → natural ask: "if you know anyone who is thinking about buying, I would love an introduction") under 200 words, (2) Handwritten card version (under 80 words — feels personal, not form letter), (3) SMS version for a client you have a close relationship with. No pressure, no scripts that sound like a call center.

2. Referral Partner Marketing

Prompt 4 — Real Estate Agent Partnership Email

Write outreach emails to build referral relationships with real estate agents. My lending specialty: [first-time buyers / jumbo / self-employed / VA loans / investors / FHA / conventional — choose your focus] What I offer agents: [fast pre-approvals / reliable closings / communication throughout / access to multiple lenders] What makes me different from every other LO: [be specific — your turnaround time, your communication style, your track record] Write: (1) Cold email to an agent I have never worked with (150 words — specific to their market, specific ask: coffee meeting or phone call), (2) Follow-up after we met once but have not worked together yet (1 week after meeting), (3) Value-add email — share a useful market insight or buyer education resource (no pitch, just value). Each sounds human, not templated.
Pro tip: Agents refer lenders who make them look good to their clients. Your pitch should answer: "What will my clients experience when they work with you?"

Prompt 5 — Builder / Developer Partnership Proposal

Write a proposal to a new home builder or developer to become their preferred lender. The builder: [large national / regional / custom / luxury] What buyers need from a lender on new construction: [construction-to-perm / bridge loans / extended rate locks / builder incentive programs] What I can offer buyers: [specific loan products, turnaround, support] What I can offer the builder: [make their buyers deal-ready, reduce fall-through risk, smooth closing process] Write: (1) Initial outreach email (150 words — specific to their development or buyer profile), (2) One-page partnership proposal outline (what I offer, how it works, what makes me reliable), (3) Follow-up email after their existing preferred lender arrangement ends or falters. Professional, not salesy.

Prompt 6 — Monthly Referral Partner Newsletter

Write a monthly email newsletter for my real estate agent referral partners. Month: [current month] Market topic: [one relevant theme — rate movement context / buyer confidence / inventory trends / a common deal challenge] My goal: [stay top-of-mind / add value / position myself as the go-to resource] Newsletter sections: (1) Brief market context (what is happening — plain language, no rate predictions), (2) Buyer education piece they can share with their clients, (3) "Deal tip of the month" — something that helps agents or their buyers navigate the process, (4) Available for referrals reminder (soft — "happy to jump on a call for any buyers in your pipeline"). Under 300 words. Useful, not promotional.

3. Client Education

Prompt 7 — Mortgage Concept Explainer

Write plain-language explainers for mortgage concepts my clients struggle to understand. Topics: [fixed vs. adjustable rates / points and buydowns / PMI / DTI ratio / loan-to-value / pre-qualification vs. pre-approval / escrow / closing costs breakdown / rate lock / amortization — choose 3] For each: (1) What it is (one sentence a first-time buyer would understand), (2) Why it matters to them specifically (not abstract), (3) The question clients often forget to ask about it, (4) Common misconception to clear up. Each under 200 words. No specific rate quotes or loan recommendations. These are educational explainers for website, email, or social use.

Prompt 8 — First-Time Buyer Guide

Write a comprehensive first-time homebuyer guide for my website or as a downloadable lead magnet. Buyer profile: [young professionals / families / single buyers / specific city/market if relevant] The main fears first-time buyers have: [credit not good enough / can not afford it / process is confusing / scared to make the wrong choice] My markets: [state(s) I am licensed in] Guide structure: (1) Are you ready to buy? (honest assessment — not a sales pitch), (2) Step-by-step process overview (application through closing in plain language), (3) What you actually need to qualify (general — not specific advice), (4) Down payment options and assistance programs (mention they exist — I will help them find the right fit), (5) Common first-time buyer mistakes, (6) Glossary of terms they will hear, (7) Next step: talk to me. This guide should feel like advice from a trusted friend, not a bank brochure.
Pro tip: A well-written first-time buyer guide is your best lead magnet. People share it, agents recommend it, and it pre-qualifies leads before they call you.

4. Social Media & Content

Prompt 9 — LinkedIn Content Plan

Build a 30-day LinkedIn content plan for a mortgage broker. My audience on LinkedIn: [real estate agents / financial professionals / HR directors who refer relocating employees / general homebuying audience] My specialty: [describe] Goal: [referral partner relationships / direct buyer leads / professional brand building] Compliance note: No specific rate quotes, no outcome guarantees. 30 content ideas: For each — format (post / article / poll / video script), topic/angle, hook sentence, CTA. Mix: homebuying education → market context (no predictions) → myth-busting → behind-the-scenes (what happens during underwriting) → professional stories (general, no client names) → tips for agents → community content. 90% value, 10% business development.

Prompt 10 — Google Business Profile Posts

Write 10 Google Business Profile posts for my mortgage brokerage. My location: [city/state] My specialty: [loan types or buyer focus] Goal: [local visibility / education / trust-building / reviews] 10 posts, each under 200 words: Mix — homebuying tip → mortgage myth-busting → local market context (general) → what to expect during [phase of process] → client preparation tip → referral partner spotlight. Each: hook sentence → body → CTA (call me / schedule a free consultation / download my guide). No rate quotes in posts.

5. Operations

Prompt 11 — Document Request Email

Write a document request email to send to borrowers at different stages. Stage: [initial application / after pre-approval / ready to submit to underwriting / conditions received from underwriting] Documents typically needed at this stage: [list for the stage you choose] Client type: [W-2 employee / self-employed / retired / investor] Write: Clear, friendly email that: Explains why we need these documents (not just a list with no context) → Exactly what to send and in what format → How to send (portal / email) → Deadline and why it matters → Reassurance that this is normal and we are making progress. Under 200 words. Clients who understand why they are doing something comply faster than clients who just get a list.

Prompt 12 — Referral Request System

Build a referral request system for after loan closing. My relationship with clients: [I worked with them for [timeframe] — describe the experience] When I want to ask: [immediately at closing / 30 days after / 1 year anniversary] Write a full referral sequence: (1) Closing day message (email + card version — warm, no ask yet), (2) 30-day follow-up (check in on their first month — soft referral mention), (3) 6-month anniversary email (how is the house? — referral ask is natural), (4) 1-year anniversary email (market update + "thinking of you"), (5) Script for asking for a Google review (after they express satisfaction — direct, easy, specific). Each touch should feel like it comes from a person who genuinely cares, not an automated drip campaign.

6. Difficult Situations

Prompt 13 — Loan Denial Communication

Help me communicate a loan denial or setback to a client compassionately. Situation: [loan denied due to credit / DTI too high / property issue / employment gap / insufficient reserves] What options exist for them: [wait and improve credit / find a different program / work with a different lender / address the specific issue] My goal: [keep the relationship / help them understand next steps / make sure they do not feel like they failed] Write: (1) Call preparation script — how to open this conversation, what to say, and how to deliver bad news without making them feel like it is the end, (2) Follow-up email after the call (summary of what was discussed, actionable next steps, timeline to try again), (3) What NOT to say (common phrases that feel dismissive or shift blame).
Pro tip: Clients who receive a compassionate denial often come back 6–12 months later ready to close. How you handle bad news defines your reputation more than easy wins.

Prompt 14 — Rate Objection Responses

Help me respond to rate and fee objections without sounding defensive. Common objections: [I can get a lower rate online / my bank offered me a better rate / your fees are too high / rates went up since we started] For each objection, write: (1) Acknowledgment (do not dismiss) → (2) The real comparison they should be making (total cost, not just rate) → (3) What I offer that the other option does not → (4) How to close: "Let me show you the actual side-by-side" or similar. Keep each response under 150 words. Professional, not combative. The goal is education, not winning an argument.

7. Professional Development

Prompt 15 — Annual Production Review

Help me review my mortgage production this year. Loans closed: [number] | Volume: [$] | Average loan size: [$] Top referral sources: [list] Loan products closed most: [purchase / refi / FHA / VA / jumbo / conventional] Biggest win: [describe] Biggest deal that fell apart: [describe — no client names] Goal for next year: [specific] Analyze: (1) My most profitable referral source — how to double down on it, (2) The loan product I do best at — should I specialize further, (3) The gap between this year and my goal — what has to change, (4) The referral partner I should have invested more in, (5) The honest answer: what am I avoiding that would most accelerate my production next year?
