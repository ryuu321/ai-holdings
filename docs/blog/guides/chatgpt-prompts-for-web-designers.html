ChatGPT Prompts for Web Designers

15 free prompts to win better clients, write stronger proposals, handle scope creep, charge what you're worth, and build a sustainable web design business.

Web design is a craft business — and craft businesses fail when the designer doesn't learn to run the business side. These 15 prompts cover everything from landing your first $5K project to handling the client who wants 47 revisions to building recurring income beyond one-off builds.

1. Client Acquisition

Prompt 1 — Cold Outreach Email

Write a cold outreach email to prospect for web design clients. Target type: [local businesses / e-commerce stores / professional services / restaurants / coaches / saas startups] What I offer: [website redesign / new website build / landing pages / Webflow / Shopify / WordPress] My niche or specialization: [if any] Proof I have: [case study result, before/after, client outcome] Write an email that: Opens by referencing something specific about their current website (don't say "I noticed your website" — say specifically what you noticed) → Quantifies the gap or opportunity → States what I can do briefly → Shares a relevant proof point → Easy next step. Under 150 words. Never "I would love the opportunity" — that's weak. Be direct and confident.
Pro tip: Audit 5 prospects' websites before writing outreach. Specific observations ("your contact form isn't mobile-responsive and likely losing 30–40% of leads") open more conversations than generic pitches.

Prompt 2 — Portfolio Case Study

Write a web design portfolio case study. Project: [describe] Client type: [industry, company size] The problem they came to me with: [what wasn't working] My solution: [what I built and the key decisions] The measurable result: [traffic increase / leads / sales / faster loading / conversion rate — whatever you have] My role: [solo / team / subcontractor] Write: (1) Project headline (benefit or outcome, not just "website redesign"), (2) Challenge section (100 words — what was broken and why it mattered), (3) Solution section (150 words — your approach, key decisions, what made it different), (4) Results section (75 words — quantified if possible), (5) One quote from the client (real or representative). Keep client identity private if needed.

2. Proposals & Pricing

Prompt 3 — Web Design Proposal

Write a web design project proposal. Client: [company name] | What they do: [describe] Their goal for the site: [leads / ecommerce / brand / booking / all] Scope I'm proposing: [pages, features, integrations, platform — be specific] Timeline: [weeks] Investment: $[amount] | Payment structure: [50/50 / 3-part / retainer] My process: [discovery → design → development → launch → support] Proposal sections: (1) Project overview (restate their goals — shows you listened), (2) Our approach (your process phases), (3) What's included (specific deliverables), (4) What's not included (prevents scope creep later), (5) Timeline, (6) Investment and payment schedule, (7) What we need from you (content, feedback turnaround, access), (8) Next step. Professional but personable — not a corporate contract.

Prompt 4 — Pricing & Package Strategy

Help me design my web design pricing and package structure. My skills: [what I build — platform, specialization] My experience: [years, types of projects, notable clients] Market: [local / remote / niche] Income goal: $[amount]/month Hours I want to work: [maximum] Current rates (if any): $[amount] Design: (1) 3-tier package structure (starter / standard / premium), (2) Pricing for each with what's included, (3) Add-ons that increase project value naturally, (4) Monthly retainer options (hosting + maintenance + support), (5) How to raise rates without losing current clients. Calculate: how many projects at each tier I need to hit my income goal. Show me how retainers change the math.
Pro tip: Monthly maintenance retainers ($200–$500/month per client) are the fastest path to predictable income as a web designer. 10 retainer clients = $2K–$5K/month before you take on a single new project.

3. Client Management

Prompt 5 — Client Onboarding Package

Write a client onboarding package for new web design clients. My process: [discovery → wireframes → design → development → revisions → launch] What clients often struggle with: [providing content / decision-making delays / feedback without direction] Common friction points: [revision rounds, asset delays, scope changes] Create: (1) Welcome email (warm, sets expectations, outlines next steps), (2) Client questionnaire (10 questions to extract their goals, style preferences, audience, competitors, content plan), (3) Timeline + revision policy (clear but not legal-sounding), (4) Content collection guide (what they need to provide and in what format), (5) "What great feedback looks like" guide (concrete vs. vague feedback examples). This package should eliminate 80% of the friction that slows down projects.

Prompt 6 — Scope Creep Handler

Help me handle a scope creep situation with a client. What was originally agreed: [scope of the project per contract/proposal] What they're now asking for: [the new or expanded request] How they framed it: [exact quote if possible] The financial impact: [hours or cost to do the extra work] Our relationship: [new client / long-term / first project together] Give me: (1) How to respond in the moment (call or email), (2) The exact language to use to introduce a change order without conflict, (3) How to frame additional scope as a value-add, not a problem, (4) The change order email (brief, professional, includes the cost), (5) What to do if they push back and say it was "included." Scope creep kills profit — this conversation is essential.
Pro tip: The easiest scope creep to prevent is in the proposal. "3 rounds of revisions" is better than "revisions until approved." Specific language in proposals eliminates ambiguous client expectations.

4. Design Process & Communication

Prompt 7 — Design Presentation Script

Help me present a website design to a client. Project: [describe] What I'm presenting: [first design concepts / mid-project revision / final mockups] Client personality: [decisive / indecisive / detail-oriented / big picture] The design decisions I need to explain: [3–4 key choices that need narrative context] My goal: [approval to proceed / feedback / final sign-off] Write: (1) Opening framing (remind them of their goals and how this design serves them), (2) Walkthrough narrative — how to explain each section of the site in terms of the user's journey, not the design features, (3) How to introduce the 3 design decisions proactively (before they ask), (4) How to ask for approval without putting them on the spot, (5) How to handle "I don't know, I'll need to think about it."

Prompt 8 — Website Copywriting Brief

Write a website copywriting brief for a client who needs help with their own site copy. Client: [type of business] Website purpose: [leads / ecommerce / portfolio / SaaS / service business] Target visitor: [who comes to the site and what they're looking for] Brand tone: [professional / warm / technical / bold / approachable] Pages to write copy for: [list] Key messages to convey: [what must the visitor know and feel] Brief format: (1) Site purpose and visitor goal, (2) Brand voice guidelines with do/don't examples, (3) SEO keywords to weave in naturally, (4) Page-by-page content goals, (5) CTA hierarchy — primary and secondary actions on each page. This brief goes to whoever writes the copy — client, copywriter, or AI.

5. Business Growth

Prompt 9 — Referral Partner Strategy

Help me build a referral network for my web design business. My specialty: [niche, platform, client type] My location: [local / national / remote] Current referral sources: [describe or "none"] Identify: (1) The top 5 referral partner types who work with my ideal clients, (2) The outreach approach for each (email / LinkedIn / networking / co-marketing), (3) What to offer referral partners (reciprocal referrals / commission / co-content), (4) How to stay top-of-mind with partners who've already sent referrals, (5) The one referral partnership type with the highest ROI for web designers specifically. Write the first outreach email for my #1 partner type.

Prompt 10 — Recurring Revenue Strategy

Help me build recurring revenue into my web design business. Current income model: [project-based only] My skills: [web design, development, SEO, copywriting, etc.] Average project fee: $[amount] Client base: [how many past clients, what industry] Time available for recurring work: [hours/week] Design a recurring revenue strategy: (1) Website care plan tiers (what's included at $X/month), (2) How to convert current project clients to care plans, (3) The script to pitch a care plan at project handoff, (4) Additional services that naturally pair with care plans (SEO / content / paid ads / email), (5) Revenue calculation: if I convert [X]% of past clients, my monthly recurring income becomes $[Y]. Show me the path from $0 to $3K/month recurring.
Pro tip: The best time to pitch a care plan is at project launch — when they're happiest and most aware that the site needs maintenance. "What would you like to do about ongoing support?" closes at a much higher rate than pitching 3 months later.

6. Content & Marketing

Prompt 11 — LinkedIn Content Strategy

Build a LinkedIn content strategy for my web design business. My specialty: [platform, niche, client type] Target audience on LinkedIn: [small business owners / marketing directors / founders / other designers] Goal: [client inquiries / networking / thought leadership] Content comfort level: [writing / video / carousel] Time available: [X posts/week] Content strategy: (1) 3 content pillars that attract my ideal client, (2) 20 specific post ideas across those pillars, (3) Hook templates for each pillar type, (4) How often to post and what type to lead with, (5) The 1 type of content that generates the most client inquiries for web designers specifically. Include: what NOT to post (tech jargon, designer-to-designer content that clients don't care about).

Prompt 12 — Client Testimonial & Case Study System

Build a system for collecting testimonials and case studies from web design clients. My current approach: [asking after launch / not asking / waiting for them to volunteer] What I want: [written testimonial / video testimonial / full case study / all] Create: (1) The right moment in the project to ask (timing is everything), (2) The exact message to send requesting a testimonial (easy to say yes), (3) 5 guided questions that get specific, useful testimonials (not "great experience!"), (4) How to turn a strong testimonial into a case study with their permission, (5) Where to publish and use testimonials for maximum lead generation. Include a "testimonial refusal" response — what to do if they decline.

7. Career & Development

Prompt 13 — Niche Positioning Statement

Help me define and communicate my web design niche. My skills: [what I build — platform, features, integrations] My best clients: [industry, size, situation — be specific] My results: [what outcomes clients get] Market I'm in: [geographic / remote / global] Competitors I compare to: [generalist agencies / other freelancers / platforms like Squarespace] Define: (1) My ideal client profile (hyper-specific — not "small businesses"), (2) My unique positioning in one sentence: "I help [who] do [what] by [how]", (3) My differentiator — what I do that a generalist or a DIY platform doesn't, (4) How to communicate this on my website, proposals, and social profiles, (5) The type of client I should stop taking so I can deepen the niche that makes me most money.
Pro tip: "Web designer for fitness coaches" earns 3x what "web designer" earns because fitness coaches assume you already understand their business. Niching down is how you raise rates without justifying them.

Prompt 14 — Skills Development Plan

Build a 6-month skills development plan for a web designer. My current skills: [list: platforms, languages, design tools, specializations] Skills I want to develop: [list: what would make me more valuable or open new markets] Income goal: $[monthly] | Current income: $[monthly] Time available for learning: [hours/week] Create: (1) Skills audit — what gaps are most limiting my income right now? (2) Priority order for skill development (highest ROI skills first), (3) Month-by-month learning plan with specific resources, (4) How to charge for new skills before I'm fully confident (the legitimate path), (5) A project idea to build with each new skill (portfolio + learning simultaneously).

Prompt 15 — Annual Business Review

Help me review my web design business this year. Revenue: $[amount] from [number of projects] Average project value: $[amount] Recurring revenue: $[amount/month] Best project (and why): [describe] Worst project (client or type, and why): [honest] What I tried that didn't work: [marketing, pricing, niching attempts] Where I am vs. where I wanted to be: [honest gap] Analyze: (1) My average project value trend — going up, down, or flat? Why? (2) The client type that's most profitable — am I actively targeting more of them? (3) The client or project type that drains me — should I stop taking them? (4) My top 3 priorities for next year, (5) The hard truth: what's actually holding me back from $10K months?
