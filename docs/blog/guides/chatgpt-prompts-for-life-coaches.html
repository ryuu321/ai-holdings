15 ChatGPT Prompts for Life Coaches

Build a more impactful practice, attract clients who are ready to grow, and run your coaching business without the administrative overwhelm — with AI as your silent support system.

Life coaching is one of the most rewarding — and most business-intensive — professions. You're running discovery calls, designing sessions, following up with clients, marketing your practice, and managing your own mindset, all at once. These prompts help you do more of what you do best: coaching.

These work for certified life coaches, executive coaches, relationship coaches, career coaches, mindset coaches, and any coaching professional building a solo or small practice. Use them in ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini. Always adapt AI output to your coaching philosophy and client relationship.

Client Intake & Discovery

1. The Discovery Call Script

The discovery call is where most coaches lose potential clients — not because of their coaching, but because of the structure of the call itself.

Write a 45-minute discovery call script for a life coaching practice. My specialty: [career coaching / mindset / relationships / life transitions / executive performance / etc.]. My ideal client: [describe who you coach best — their situation, goals, and what they're struggling with]. My program: [brief description — what I offer, length, investment]. Write a script that: 1) Opens with a connection-building question (not "tell me about yourself"), 2) Uncovers the real problem beneath the surface problem they described, 3) Helps them articulate the cost of staying where they are, 4) Introduces my program as a natural solution (not a pitch), 5) Handles the most common objections: "I need to think about it" / "It's expensive" / "Bad timing", 6) Closes with a clear decision, not "let me know." Also write 3 powerful coaching questions to use if the conversation stalls. Goal: a call structure that serves the client AND converts.

2. The Client Intake Form

Write a client intake form for new coaching clients. My coaching focus: [describe your niche]. Program length: [X weeks/months]. What I need to know before Session 1: [list what's actually useful for you to know — not just demographics]. Create a form with: 1) 8-12 questions that give me what I actually need to coach effectively from Session 1, 2) Questions that prime clients for growth (the act of answering should shift their thinking), 3) A "what would make this coaching experience a 10/10 for you?" question that sets mutual expectations, 4) Questions about their preferred communication style, accountability preferences, and past experience with coaching or therapy, 5) A permission question: "Are there topics you'd like me to avoid or approach carefully?" Also write the introductory paragraph that explains why you're asking these questions — not just "please fill this out."

3. The Goal Clarification Framework

Create a goal clarification exercise for new coaching clients. The problem: most clients come in with surface-level goals ("I want to be more productive") that aren't actually what they want. Build a structured exercise with: 1) A sequence of 5 deepening questions that move from surface goal → real desire (what do you want? why? what would that give you? what would change? who would you be?), 2) A "future self" visualization prompt clients can write about before Session 1, 3) A values clarification exercise — 10 questions that help clients identify which values their current situation violates, 4) A "success looks like" section where clients write specifically what's different in their life in 6 months, 5) A "fear inventory" — what are they afraid coaching will reveal or require them to change? Make it feel like a coaching experience, not a survey.

Session Design & Delivery

4. The Session Structure Builder

Design a session structure for [type of coaching session: first session / mid-program check-in / goal-setting / stuck client / breakthrough session / final session]. Client situation: [describe briefly — what they're working on, where they are in their program]. Session length: [45 / 60 / 90 minutes]. Build a session plan with: 1) Opening ritual (2-3 minutes): how to transition them from their day into a coaching mindset, 2) Check-in question (5 minutes): what's shifted since last session — what are they noticing?, 3) Focus question: what's the most important thing to work on today?, 4) Core coaching block: 3 powerful questions to explore this session's focus, 5) Insight capture: how to help them articulate what they discovered, 6) Action commitment: 1-2 specific commitments with accountability built in, 7) Closing ritual: how to end with energy and forward momentum. Also: one observation I should make if they seem resistant or stuck.

5. The Powerful Question Generator

Generate powerful coaching questions for [specific situation or stuck point]. Situation: [describe — client avoids starting / keeps repeating the same pattern / says they want change but doesn't act / is people-pleasing / is in transition and scared / is dealing with perfectionism / other]. Generate: 1) 5 "what" questions that open up possibility (not "why" questions that create defensiveness), 2) 3 perspective-shift questions (what would X say? what would you tell a friend?), 3) 2 somatic/body-based awareness questions (where do you feel this in your body?), 4) 2 future-pull questions (what would be possible if this weren't a problem?), 5) 1 "what are you making this mean?" question to surface limiting beliefs. For each question, note when to use it and what it's designed to unlock. Avoid: leading questions, questions that give advice in disguise ("have you considered...?").

6. The Between-Session Homework Designer

Design between-session homework for a coaching client. What we worked on this session: [describe the key insight or decision from the session]. What they committed to: [describe the action they said they'd take]. Client profile: [describe their style — do they prefer structured tasks or open exploration? Are they analytical or intuitive?]. Design homework that: 1) Is specific enough that they'll know whether they did it, 2) Is small enough that they'll actually do it, 3) Reinforces the insight from today's session through action, 4) Includes one reflection question to process the experience, 5) Has a built-in accountability check (how they'll report back). Also write: the follow-up message I'll send midweek to check in (under 60 words, supportive not pressure-adding).

Marketing & Client Attraction

7. The Coaching Niche Statement

Help me define and articulate my coaching niche. My background: [describe your experience — personal transformation, professional background, certifications, life experience]. Who I've coached: [describe past or ideal clients — age, gender, life stage, profession, what they struggle with]. What makes me different from other coaches: [be specific — not "I'm authentic" but actual differentiators]. Write: 1) My coaching niche in one sentence ("I help [specific person] achieve [specific outcome] so they can [deeper desire]"), 2) A one-paragraph bio for my website that leads with who I serve and what I help them do — not with my credentials, 3) How to describe what I do when someone at a party asks "so what do you do?" — under 20 words, no coaching jargon, 4) The title to use instead of "life coach" that speaks to my specific audience (e.g., "Career Transition Coach" "Empty Nester Coach" "Burnout Recovery Coach"), 5) Three content themes that would attract exactly my ideal client.

8. The Testimonial & Social Proof System

Build a testimonial collection system for my coaching practice. When I ask clients for testimonials: [describe — end of program, mid-program, never because it feels awkward]. The problem: [testimonials are too vague / clients say great things verbally but don't write them / I feel uncomfortable asking]. Build a system that: 1) The exact email to send at program completion requesting a testimonial — includes 3 specific questions so they know what to write, 2) A text/DM version for clients I have that kind of relationship with, 3) How to use video testimonials — the ask, the format, how to display, 4) How to turn a client's result into a case study they approve (outline format), 5) How to ask for a testimonial from a client mid-program when they share a breakthrough in a session ("you could write that — would you?"). Also write: the LinkedIn recommendation request template.

9. The Content Strategy for Coaches

Create a 90-day content strategy for a life coaching practice. My niche: [describe]. Platforms I'm on: [Instagram / LinkedIn / podcast / email / YouTube / etc.]. My goal: [attract discovery calls / build email list / establish thought leadership / referral network]. My content comfort zone: [writing / speaking / short video / long form]. Create a strategy with: 1) 3 content pillars that attract my specific client (not generic coaching content), 2) Weekly cadence: what to post where and how often — realistic for a solo coach, 3) 5 specific content ideas for this month that would resonate with my ideal client's current pain point, 4) How to repurpose one piece of content across 3 platforms, 5) The one content format that builds the most trust for coaching (and why). Avoid: content that sounds like every other coach's feed ("your mindset is everything" type posts).

Business Operations

10. The Coaching Program Designer

Help me design a coaching program. What clients want to achieve: [describe the transformation]. Current offer (if any): [describe what you currently sell — 1:1 sessions, packages, etc.]. My time available: [X hours/week for coaching]. Revenue target: [X/month]. Design a program with: 1) Program length and structure (why this timeline for this transformation), 2) What's included — session frequency, session length, between-session support, 3) Pricing: what to charge and how to present it (not "what are your rates?" but an anchored package), 4) A 3-tier option structure: starter / core / premium — with what each adds, 5) How to present this on my website — the copy that makes the right clients feel "this is exactly what I need." Also: what results/transformation can I promise, and how do I state it credibly without over-promising.

11. The Client Retention System

Build a client retention and re-enrollment system for my coaching practice. Current situation: [how long programs run, what happens at the end, how many clients continue]. What I want: [higher re-enrollment / better offboarding experience / more referrals from past clients]. Build a system for: 1) Week 2 of program: plant the seed for what comes after (how to mention it naturally, not as a pitch), 2) Week before final session: the conversation about what's next and how to continue momentum, 3) Final session structure: what should happen in the last session to make them feel complete AND inspired to continue, 4) 30-days-after check-in: the message to send a month after program ends, 5) 6-month anniversary: how to re-engage past clients who are ready for more. Write word-for-word language for the most important touchpoints — especially the re-enrollment conversation.

12. The Coaching Contract & Ethics Guide

Write clear, plain-language coaching agreements and ethics communication for clients. What to cover: Program scope and what coaching IS and is NOT (especially how it differs from therapy), Confidentiality policy, Cancellation and rescheduling policy, Payment terms and refund policy, What happens if a client discloses something that concerns safety. Write: 1) A coaching agreement summary email to send before Session 1 (the plain-language version that gets read — not the legal contract), 2) How to explain the coaching vs. therapy distinction in a way that doesn't feel defensive, 3) A script for handling a client who is presenting issues beyond coaching scope — how to refer them to a therapist without damaging the relationship, 4) Your cancellation policy communication: firm but warm. Note: this is a framework to adapt — consult an attorney for actual legal agreements.

Personal Practice & Growth

13. The Client Progress Tracker

Build a session notes and client progress tracking system. I currently track: [describe — nothing structured / Google Docs / a CRM / paper notes]. What I need to track: [session insights, homework completed, progress toward goals, patterns across sessions]. Build a system with: 1) A session notes template I can fill in within 10 minutes after each session, 2) A quarterly review template: how to assess each client's progress and adjust the coaching approach, 3) A pattern-recognition guide — what patterns should I look for across sessions that signal a client is ready to go deeper or is stuck?, 4) How to use session notes to write a personalized mid-program progress summary for clients (they love these), 5) A simple metric: how do I know if my coaching is actually working? What should I ask clients to track?

14. The Coach's Supervision & Reflection Tool

Help me reflect on a difficult coaching session. Session situation: [describe — client pushed back / seemed disengaged / had a breakthrough but then shut down / kept asking for advice / I felt triggered by something / I lost the thread]. Use coaching supervision principles to: 1) What might have been happening for the client in this moment (multiple possibilities — not conclusions), 2) What might have been happening for me as the coach that affected the session, 3) What I could try differently in the next session to address what came up, 4) A question I should bring to my own coach or supervisor about this, 5) One insight about my coaching that this session might be revealing. This is for reflective practice — help me see this session as data, not failure.

15. The Business Review & Pricing Audit

Help me audit my coaching business and pricing. Data: Total clients this year: [X]. Average program length: [X months]. Average program value: $[X]. Client source breakdown: [referrals / social media / podcast / website / etc.]. Client satisfaction: [describe — do they refer? do they re-enroll?]. Time spent coaching vs. admin vs. marketing: [rough %]. Run an audit that: 1) Calculates my real effective hourly rate (total income ÷ total hours, including non-coaching time), 2) Identifies which program or client type is most profitable relative to time and energy, 3) Recommends a pricing change (increase, repackage, or raise the floor), 4) Identifies the single client acquisition channel to double down on, 5) Names one thing to stop doing that's consuming time without driving revenue. Give me direct analysis, not a list of things to "consider."
Pro tip: Start with Prompt 1 (Discovery Call Script) — it has the most immediate impact on your revenue. Most coaches are better at coaching than selling, and a structured discovery call bridges that gap without feeling salesy. A well-structured 45-minute call can double your conversion rate from inquiry to client.
