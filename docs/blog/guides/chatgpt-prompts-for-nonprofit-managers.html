ChatGPT Prompts for Nonprofit Managers

15 free prompts for nonprofit directors, program managers, and development professionals. Do more mission with less bandwidth.

Nonprofit work is relentless. You're writing grant proposals on Tuesday, managing volunteers on Wednesday, creating impact reports on Thursday — all with a fraction of the resources a for-profit team would have. These 15 prompts help you work faster, communicate your mission more powerfully, and spend more time on the work that actually matters.

1. Grant Writing & Funding

Prompt 1 — Grant Proposal Narrative

Help me write a compelling grant proposal narrative. Organization: [name and 1-sentence mission] Program applying for funding: [describe the program] Funder: [foundation name, if known — what are their priorities?] Grant amount requested: $[amount] Problem we address: [describe the need with data if possible] Our solution: [describe the program/intervention] Target population: [who is served, how many] Outcomes: [what changes as a result, how measured] Write a 600–800 word narrative covering: compelling problem statement → our unique approach → evidence base → target outcomes → organizational capacity → call to action. Use the funder's language. Avoid jargon.
Pro tip: Mirror the language from the funder's guidelines back to them. If they say "equity-centered," use that phrase in your narrative.

Prompt 2 — Logic Model Builder

Help me build a logic model for our program. Program name: [name] Target population: [who] Problem/need: [describe] What we do (activities): [list 3–5 key activities] What we provide (outputs): [list measurable outputs — e.g., "150 participants trained"] Build a complete logic model with: Inputs (resources needed) → Activities → Outputs (numbers served, sessions held) → Short-term outcomes (knowledge/attitude change) → Medium-term outcomes (behavior change) → Long-term outcomes (systems/condition change). Format as a table I can drop into a grant proposal.

2. Donor Communication & Fundraising

Prompt 3 — Donor Thank-You Letter

Write a warm, specific thank-you letter for a [major/mid-level/first-time] donor who gave $[amount] to [specific program or general operations]. Donor name: [if known] Their connection to the cause: [if known] Impact their gift will have: [specific — e.g., "funds 3 months of after-school tutoring for 20 students"] The letter should: Open with their specific impact (not "thank you for your gift") → Paint a picture of the change they're making → Briefly share a success story → Reinforce their identity as someone who makes a difference → Close with a warm, non-asking statement. Max 250 words. No corporate tone.
Pro tip: Lead with impact, not gratitude. "Your gift is sending Maria to college" beats "Thank you for your generous donation."

Prompt 4 — Year-End Fundraising Appeal

Write a year-end fundraising appeal email for our nonprofit. Organization: [name] Mission: [one sentence] This year's headline achievement: [your biggest win with numbers] The gap we're facing: [what funding challenge or unmet need] Ask: $[amount] or "[what the amount does]" Deadline: December 31 Write: Subject line (+ 2 alternatives) → Email body (350–450 words). Open with a specific story about one person served → Connect to the donor's values → Share the year's impact with numbers → Create urgency around year-end → Clear, single CTA. Avoid clichés like "make a difference this holiday season."

3. Program Design & Impact Measurement

Prompt 5 — Program Design Framework

I'm designing a new program to address [problem] for [target population] in [community/context]. Available resources: [staff, budget, space, partners] Time horizon: [program length] Similar programs I know of: [list or "none"] Design the program: (1) Core theory of change (2–3 sentences), (2) Key activities and sequence, (3) Who delivers what (staffing model), (4) How we recruit/engage participants, (5) What success looks like at 90 days, 6 months, 1 year, (6) The biggest assumption we're making that could sink this program. Be honest about risks.
Pro tip: The biggest assumption question is the most important one. Name it explicitly so you can test it early rather than discover it after 12 months.

Prompt 6 — Impact Report Writing

Help me write our annual impact report narrative. Year: [year] Key data points: [paste your numbers — people served, programs delivered, outcomes achieved] Headline story: [describe one participant's journey or transformation] What didn't work: [optional — what challenge you faced] What's next: [your priority for next year] Audience: [donors, funders, board, community — or all] Write: (1) Opening narrative (200 words, story-led), (2) Impact by the numbers (suggest 4–6 callout statistics), (3) Organizational highlight, (4) Closing that connects past impact to future need. Tone: Honest, specific, inspiring — not promotional.

4. Volunteer & Team Management

Prompt 7 — Volunteer Recruitment Message

Write a volunteer recruitment post for [platform: Facebook/email/Nextdoor/LinkedIn]. Role: [volunteer role description] Time commitment: [hours/week or one-time event] What volunteers do: [specific activities] Skills needed: [any requirements or "none — everyone welcome"] Impact: [what volunteers accomplish by showing up] Sign-up link: [URL] Make it: Specific about the role → Honest about the time ask → Clear about the impact → Easy to take action. Avoid guilt-tripping or vague "make a difference" language. Include 2 subject line/headline variations.

Prompt 8 — Difficult Volunteer Conversation

Help me prepare for a difficult conversation with a volunteer who [describe situation: isn't showing up, overstepped their role, had a conflict with staff, isn't following our processes, needs to be released from their role]. My goal in this conversation: [retain and redirect / release with dignity / set clear expectations] Our relationship: [how long they've volunteered, their contribution] What I need to say: [the core message] Give me: (1) How to open the conversation, (2) The key message stated clearly and kindly, (3) How to handle defensiveness, (4) What resolution/outcome I should aim for, (5) How to close in a way that preserves dignity regardless of outcome.
Pro tip: Volunteers aren't employees — they can walk away. Lead with appreciation for their commitment, then address the issue specifically.

5. Board Relations & Governance

Prompt 9 — Board Meeting Preparation

Help me prepare for our board meeting. Key decisions the board needs to make: [list] Financial snapshot to present: [describe financial position] Program updates: [key wins and challenges] Items that need board input (not just FYI): [list] Sensitive topic to navigate: [if any] Create: (1) Meeting agenda (timed), (2) Executive Director report (one-page format), (3) How to frame the key decision so the board can actually decide (background → options → recommendation → ask), (4) Questions to prepare for. Keep the agenda under 90 minutes.

Prompt 10 — Board Recruitment Strategy

Help me recruit [X] new board members. Current board gaps: [skills missing: legal / finance / fundraising / DEI / community voice / sector expertise] Current board demographics: [optional — describe or note gaps] Networks we can tap: [list current board's networks, community connections] What board service looks like: [meeting frequency, fundraising expectations, committee work] Create: (1) Board member profile/job description (1 page), (2) 3-sentence pitch for different audiences, (3) Outreach email template, (4) Questions to ask in a board recruitment coffee chat, (5) Red flags to watch for in prospective board members.

6. Communications & Marketing

Prompt 11 — Storytelling Content Calendar

Build a 30-day content calendar for our nonprofit's social media. Mission: [one sentence] Platforms: [Facebook / Instagram / LinkedIn / email] Posting frequency: [X times/week] Upcoming events or milestones: [list] Content types I have access to: [photos, videos, stats, stories, partner content] Goal: [donor acquisition / volunteer recruitment / community awareness / all] For each post idea: Platform → Content type → Topic/angle → Caption hook (first line) → CTA. Include a mix of: impact stories, behind-the-scenes, data/stats, community spotlights, appeals, and mission-moment content.
Pro tip: Real stories with real photos outperform stock images 3–5x on nonprofit social media. Invest 30 minutes/month collecting client stories with permission.

Prompt 12 — Media Pitch for Local Coverage

Write a media pitch to get coverage in [local newspaper / TV station / community blog]. Our story: [what's happening — program launch, milestone, event, human interest story] The hook for local readers: [why does the community care?] The spokesperson: [who can be interviewed] Visual opportunity: [what a camera crew / photographer could capture] Timing: [why now / any news peg] Write: (1) Email subject line, (2) Email pitch (200 words max — tight), (3) 3 suggested story angles from different editorial perspectives (human interest / data story / community impact). Include a brief quote from the spokesperson.

7. Strategy & Organizational Health

Prompt 13 — Strategic Planning Facilitation

I'm facilitating a strategic planning session for our nonprofit board and leadership. Organization: [name, mission, size]. Time available: [half-day / full day / multi-session]. Design a facilitation agenda that covers: (1) Organizational assessment (strengths, challenges, external trends), (2) Mission and values alignment check, (3) Strategic priority-setting (3-year horizon), (4) Goal development (SMART goals for each priority), (5) Resource alignment (budget + staff implications). Include specific facilitation questions for each section. I need real discussion — not a rubber-stamp session.

Prompt 14 — Staff Retention Strategy

I'm a nonprofit manager trying to retain our team despite nonprofit salary constraints. Current team size: [number]. Biggest retention risk: [describe — burnout, salary gap, limited growth, mission fatigue]. Budget for retention initiatives: [tight / some flexibility]. Develop a retention strategy: (1) Non-monetary benefits that actually matter to mission-driven staff, (2) Career development and growth pathways I can create, (3) Culture and recognition practices that reinforce mission connection, (4) How to have honest conversations about salary limitations without losing people, (5) Early warning signs someone is about to leave — and what to do then.
Pro tip: Mission connection is your #1 retention tool — but only if staff can see how their daily work connects to real outcomes. Make that explicit.

Prompt 15 — Annual Review & Goal Setting

Help me conduct my annual organizational review. This year: [describe key programs, financials, staffing, and community outcomes in 3–5 bullet points] What exceeded expectations: [list] What fell short and why: [honest assessment] External environment changes: [funding landscape, community needs, policy, etc.] Next year's resource reality: [budget increase / flat / cut] Synthesize: (1) The most important thing we learned this year, (2) What we should stop doing, (3) What we should double down on, (4) Our top 3 priorities for next year with rationale, (5) The one question that keeps me up at night that we haven't answered yet.
