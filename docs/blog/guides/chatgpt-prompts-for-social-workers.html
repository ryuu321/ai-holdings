ChatGPT Prompts for Social Workers

15 free prompts to write cleaner case notes, coordinate resources faster, and reclaim time from documentation.

Important: Never enter real client names, identifying details, or protected health information (PHI) into AI tools. Use role descriptions, hypothetical scenarios, or anonymized placeholders only. Review all AI-generated documentation before use — you remain the professional of record.

Social workers carry some of the highest documentation burdens of any profession. These 15 prompts help you write faster, find resources more efficiently, prepare for difficult conversations, and manage your caseload without burning out — all while keeping the human judgment that AI can never replace.

1. Case Documentation

Prompt 1 — Case Note Structure

Help me structure a case note using the [DAP / SOAP / BIRP / PIE] format. Situation type: [initial assessment / ongoing visit / crisis intervention / discharge planning / court-ordered follow-up] What happened during the session: [describe in plain language — no PHI, use role descriptions like "client" or "the family"] Key observations: [behavior, affect, engagement, changes since last contact] Goal being addressed: [which goal from the care plan] What I plan to do next: [next steps, referrals, follow-up timing] Write a complete case note in the selected format. Use professional social work language. Flag anything that sounds like clinical overreach or unsupported conclusions.
Pro tip: Use AI to draft, then edit with your direct observation notes in hand. AI gives you structure — you provide the accuracy.

Prompt 2 — Assessment Summary

Help me write a psychosocial assessment summary based on the following information. Client role: [adult individual / family / youth / elder] Presenting concern: [general category — housing instability / family conflict / substance use / grief / unemployment] Strengths identified: [list 3–5 — coping skills, support network, motivation, employment history, etc.] Risk factors: [list — without specific identifying detail] Protective factors: [what is helping stabilize the situation] Client's stated goals: [what the client said they want] Write a structured psychosocial summary (300–400 words) appropriate for a case file. Lead with strengths, include risk and protective factors, and close with client-identified goals.

Prompt 3 — Progress Note for Court or Legal Review

I need to write a progress note that may be reviewed by a court, attorney, or child protective services. Context: [family reunification / custody evaluation / mandated services / involuntary commitment / guardianship] Service period covered: [general timeframe — e.g., 90 days] What was provided: [services, contacts, interventions — no client names] Client engagement level: [consistent / intermittent / resistant / improving] Progress toward goals: [specific goal + whether on track, partial, or not met] Safety considerations: [general — any ongoing concerns] Write a factual, professional progress note. Avoid clinical diagnoses outside your scope. Use objective language — describe behaviors, not interpretations.
Pro tip: Court-reviewed notes need to be especially objective. If you write "client appeared to be lying," a judge will question your credibility. Write "client's account differed from [collateral source]'s account in the following ways."

2. Resource Coordination

Prompt 4 — Resource Search Strategy

Help me identify what types of community resources typically exist for a client with the following needs. Location type: [urban / suburban / rural] Primary needs: [list — e.g., emergency housing, food assistance, mental health services, domestic violence support, transportation, childcare] Population: [adults / elderly / youth / veterans / immigrants / people with disabilities] Barriers to access: [no transportation / documentation concerns / language / disability / work schedule] List the categories of resources that typically exist in this area, common program names or types to search for, what eligibility requirements usually look like, and how to advocate for a client who does not meet standard eligibility.

Prompt 5 — Referral Letter

Write a professional referral letter from a social worker to a community service provider. Referring agency: [your organization type — not the actual name] Receiving agency type: [mental health clinic / housing authority / food bank / substance use program / workforce services] Reason for referral: [general presenting need] Urgency level: [routine / urgent / emergency] What the receiving agency needs to know about service preferences or barriers: [communication style, scheduling constraints, language needs, etc.] What follow-up is expected: [one-time service / ongoing / intake appointment confirmation] Write a clear, professional referral letter. Include a specific ask for what you need back from them.

Prompt 6 — Resource Gap Analysis

Help me analyze a resource gap I'm seeing repeatedly in my caseload. Population I serve: [general description] Unmet need I keep encountering: [describe the gap — e.g., no weekend mental health services, no bilingual housing counselors] What resources currently exist: [list what does exist, even if inadequate] Who is most harmed by this gap: [which clients, what consequences] What I am doing as a workaround: [current improvised solutions] Write: (1) a clear description of the gap suitable for an internal report or grant application, (2) what data I should be collecting to document this gap, (3) what advocacy steps could address it, and (4) how to raise it with my supervisor or agency leadership.
Pro tip: Documenting service gaps is advocacy work. If you raise it verbally and nothing changes, document it in writing. That paper trail supports grant applications, policy change, and your own professional record.

3. Client Communication

Prompt 7 — Plain Language Explanation

Rewrite the following social services policy, legal notice, or eligibility requirement in plain language for a client with a 6th-grade reading level. Original text: [paste the policy or legal language] Client's situation: [general context for why they need to understand this] What action the client needs to take: [specific next step] Rewrite in simple, direct language. Use short sentences. Avoid jargon. Include a one-sentence summary at the top and a clear "what you need to do" statement at the end.

Prompt 8 — Difficult Conversation Preparation

Help me prepare for a difficult conversation with a client or family. Situation type: [service denial / safety plan discussion / report to CPS / case closure / involuntary intervention / confronting a boundary violation] What I need to communicate: [the core message in one sentence] What reaction I expect: [anger / denial / grief / shutdown / manipulation] What outcome I need from the conversation: [specific goal — agreement, acknowledgment, safety plan signed, referral accepted] My concern: [what I'm worried about going wrong] Write: (1) how to open the conversation, (2) language for the hardest moment, (3) how to respond if the client reacts with [the expected reaction], (4) how to close even if it does not go well, (5) what to document afterward.

Prompt 9 — Family Meeting Agenda

Help me plan a family meeting. Purpose: [case review / safety planning / conflict resolution / discharge planning / reunification] Who will be present: [list roles — client, parent, guardian, extended family, other providers — no names] Tensions I anticipate: [what disagreements or dynamics I expect] Decision that needs to be made: [specific outcome needed] Time available: [e.g., 60 minutes] Write a meeting agenda with time allocations, language for opening and framing, how to handle a situation where the meeting derails, and how to close with clear next steps even if consensus is not reached.
Pro tip: Family meetings often fail because no one has defined what "success" looks like before the room fills up. Write your success criteria on paper before you walk in.

4. Professional Writing

Prompt 10 — Treatment Plan Goals

Help me write SMART treatment plan goals for a client situation. Presenting problem: [general — e.g., housing instability, grief, family conflict, substance use recovery support] Client strengths: [what the client can build on] Timeframe: [30 / 60 / 90 / 180 days] Services being provided: [type of support — case management, counseling referral, skills groups, etc.] Barriers to progress: [practical and motivational] Write 3 SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) with corresponding objectives and progress indicators. Frame goals from the client's perspective — "client will..." not "worker will ensure client..."

Prompt 11 — Grant Narrative Support

Help me write a program description or needs statement for a grant application. Program type: [describe the service — e.g., housing stability services for formerly incarcerated individuals] Population served: [general description — demographics, geography, need level] Problem the program addresses: [describe the gap and its impact] What the program does: [activities, services, approach] Outcomes we expect: [what will change for clients] Evidence base (if any): [research or models the program draws from] Write a 400–600 word needs statement and program description. Use compelling but factual language. Avoid jargon. Lead with the human impact.

Prompt 12 — Supervision Preparation

Help me prepare for a supervision session. Cases I want to discuss: [describe by situation type, not name — e.g., "a family in reunification with safety concerns"] What I need from supervision: [consultation / emotional support / ethical guidance / clinical direction / advocacy for a resource] Stuck point: [where I feel uncertain or overwhelmed] My own reaction to this case: [honestly — what feelings is it bringing up for me] Write: (1) how to frame each case efficiently for supervision, (2) the specific question I should be asking, (3) how to ask for what I need directly, and (4) how to process my countertransference outside of supervision time.
Pro tip: Good supervision is a skill you have to learn — it is not just showing up. The social workers who get the most from supervision are the ones who come with specific questions, not just "I need help with this case."

5. Self-Care and Sustainability

Prompt 13 — Vicarious Trauma Check-In

Help me reflect on signs of vicarious trauma or compassion fatigue. What I've been dealing with lately: [general themes — not case specifics. E.g., "high-exposure trauma cases, repeated family separations, court involvement"] How I'm feeling at work: [honest assessment — energy, engagement, empathy levels] How it's showing up outside work: [sleep, relationships, thoughts about leaving the field] What coping strategies I currently use: [list] What I know helps but am not doing: [honest answer] Write: (1) reflection questions to assess where I am on the compassion fatigue continuum, (2) a personalized plan for the next 30 days that is realistic given my schedule, (3) language to use when asking my employer for support, and (4) the difference between vicarious trauma and burnout — and which this sounds more like.

Prompt 14 — Caseload Boundary Setting

Help me think through boundary issues in my current caseload. Situation: [describe the dynamic — a client who contacts me outside hours, a family that has become dependent, a case where I've gotten too personally invested, a colleague who offloads cases to me] What boundary is being crossed: [my time / emotional bandwidth / professional role / scope of practice] Why I haven't addressed it yet: [honest — fear of hurting the client, guilt, unclear if it's actually a problem] What a healthy version of this situation would look like: [describe] Write: (1) how to name the boundary issue clearly, (2) language for the conversation with the client, family, or colleague, (3) how to document it, and (4) what to do if the dynamic does not change after I address it.

Prompt 15 — Annual Professional Review

Help me reflect on my year in social work practice. Cases that had the most impact: [types — no identifying detail] Skills I developed: [what I got better at] Moments I struggled most: [honest — ethically, emotionally, professionally] What I avoided this year: [client populations, case types, conversations] Where I want to grow next year: [skills, specialization, leadership, better work-life balance] Write: (1) a self-evaluation narrative suitable for a performance review (350 words — lead with impact, not activity), (2) how to quantify social work outcomes even when they are hard to measure, (3) one honest growth area to name directly with your supervisor, and (4) what career investment would have the highest return for where I want to be in 3 years.
